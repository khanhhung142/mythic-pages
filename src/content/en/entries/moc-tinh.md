## The Story

In high antiquity, in the land of Phong Châu, there grew a great tree called the chiên đàn. Its trunk rose more than a thousand arm-spans. Its branches spread until no one could say where the shade ended, covering thousands of leagues of earth. White cranes came from every direction to nest in its crown, so many that people named the land beneath it Bạch Hạc, the land of the white crane.

The tree lived a long time, thousands of years past counting. Then it grew old. The trunk dried, the leaves fell, the roots rotted. But this tree did not die the way other trees die. The essence it had drawn from earth and sky over all those centuries pooled in the rotting wood, and the tree became a demon. The demon changed shape without warning and was savage beyond anything the people knew. It caught people and ate them. Those who went into the forest did not come back. Those who stayed home were not safe either. There was no telling where it would strike, for it was here one day and elsewhere the next, and it never kept to one place.

Lạc Long Quân, the lord of dragon blood who watched over the people of the southern land, heard of their misery and came to rid them of it.

That battle was like no other he had fought. His enemy had been a tree, but on the battleground there was no tree to be found. It took the shape of a great beast and charged him, fang and claw; when he struck back it dissolved, slid into the shadows of the forest, and came at him again from behind in another shape. He brought his magic down on one spot and it was already somewhere else. He drove it up the mountain and it slipped down a ravine. The whole forest fought on its side: any trunk might be the demon, any boulder might be the demon. So Lạc Long Quân ordered gongs and drums beaten, roll after roll without pause, the sound pounding off the cliff walls. The demon dreaded great noise; wherever it fled, the drumming followed, until no shadow was left to hide it. Seven days and seven nights the two of them fought. Whole stands of forest broke and fell. The people of the region stood far off and listened to the ground shake.

On the seventh night the demon's strength gave out. It yielded. It submitted. But it did not die. Its body was a thousand years of sacred essence soaked into wood; the dragon lord's magic could break its fury but could not cut out the spirit inside. It abandoned its old ground and fled, changing form as it went, slipping deep into the forests and mountains, and from time to time it came back to take people as before.

The people ran out of answers. They built a shrine and worshipped it. Every year, on the thirtieth day of the twelfth lunar month, the last day of the year, they brought one living person to the shrine and offered them up, buying one year of peace. They called the demon Thần Xương Cuồng.

Under the Qin, Nhâm Ngao came south as magistrate of Long Xuyên. When he learned that living people were fed to the shrine, he was appalled and moved to end it. Xương Cuồng rose in fury and killed him. After that the people were more afraid than ever, and their offerings more careful.

The custom ran on for another thousand years and more. Not until the reign of Đinh Tiên Hoàng did anyone master the demon. That man was Văn Du Tường, a Taoist come down from the north, past eighty years old, widely traveled, fluent in the tongues of many peoples. He understood that this demon feared no ordinary blade. It could not be fought head-on. It had to be lured, and to lure it he needed something it had never seen.

He chose six skillful men and taught each one an act. Thượng Kỵ galloped a horse and leaned down to snatch things off the ground. Thượng Can climbed a high pole. Thượng Hiểm walked a rope. Thượng Đát tumbled and somersaulted. Thượng Toái curled inside a bamboo cage and rolled. Thượng Câu swung through the air by his arms. In the eleventh month of that year, Văn Du Tường raised the Phi Vân pavilion, twenty spans high, and opened a festival where the six men performed everything they knew. Drums sounded, crowds shouted, people poured in like water.

Xương Cuồng had lived thousands of years and had never seen anything like it. It crept in among the crowd to watch. It watched, absorbed, and forgot to guard itself. Below the pavilion, Văn Du Tường murmured his secret incantation. When it was done, he drew his sword and cut. Xương Cuồng and its whole band of minions died on the spot, and not one of them could ever take demon form again.

Only then did the offering of living people end. Of the thousand-year chiên đàn tree, no trace remains. Only the name Bạch Hạc, the land of the white crane, still sits by the meeting of the three rivers to this day.

## The Plot According to _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_

Mộc Tinh truyện (木精傳) stands fourth in fascicle I of the 22-story recension edited by Vũ Quỳnh in 1492, directly after Ngư Tinh and Hồ Tinh. In the collection's original structure it was story 22, the last. That relocation is the key to the tale's largest textual problem (next section).

The Hán original fixes the details worth keeping precise:

- The **chiên đàn** tree (旃檀) stands in Phong Châu, **over a thousand arm-spans tall** (高千餘仞), its canopy covering thousands of leagues. Cranes nest in it, and the land is named **Bạch Hạc** (白鶴).
- After thousands of years the tree withers and **turns demon** (化為妖精). The original describes the demon in exactly three phrases: **shape-shifting and ferocious** (變幻勇猛), **here one moment, there the next** (在此在彼), **habitually eating people** (常食人). Not one word about its appearance.
- The victor of the first battle, in the original's own words: **涇陽王以術勝之**, Kinh Dương Vương defeated it by magic. The demon is only **"slightly subdued"** (稍屈); it does not die.
- The people build a shrine; every year on the **30th day of the 12th lunar month** (歲終十二月三十日) they offer **a living person** (薦以生人) to be left in peace. They call the demon **Thần Xương Cuồng** (猖狂神).
- The southwestern border touches the **Land of Monkeys** (獼猴國). The Hùng kings order the Bà Lô people (now in Diễn Châu, Nghệ An) to capture people from the headwaters (山源頭獠子) each year as tribute.
- Under the Qin, **Nhâm Ngao** (任囂, also read Nhâm Hiêu), magistrate of Long Xuyên, moves to abolish the sacrifice. **Xương Cuồng kills him in fury** (猖狂怒殺囂). The people worship more carefully than before.
- In the reign of **Đinh Tiên Hoàng** (968-979), the exorcist **Văn Du Tường** (文俞祥), a northerner past eighty, trains six performers: Thượng Kỵ (上騎), Thượng Can (上竿), Thượng Hiểm (上險), Thượng Đát (上韃), Thượng Toái (上碎), Thượng Câu (上鉤). In the 11th month he raises the **Phi Vân** pavilion, twenty spans high, and opens a festival. Xương Cuồng comes to watch; Văn Du Tường recites a **secret incantation** (密咒) and cuts it down with his sword. The demon and all its minions die, "never able to become demons again." The sacrifice of living people ends.

One point of transparency: this entry's Story follows folk tradition, giving the first battle to Lạc Long Quân and taking the seven days and nights and the gongs and drums from the popular retellings. The original is far terser and names a different victor. The discrepancy has earned its own section.

## Kinh Dương Vương or Lạc Long Quân: _the Hero Problem_

The Hán original has one sentence on the first battle, and the sentence is clear: 涇陽王以術勝之, Kinh Dương Vương defeated the demon by magic. Yet folk tradition, standard retellings, school textbooks, and most Western scholarship credit Lạc Long Quân. Keith Taylor himself, in _The Birth of Vietnam_ (1983, p. 5, n. 15), writes of "the victory of Lac Long Quan over the thousand-year-old demon-tree in Me-linh," changing both the hero and the location (the original says Phong Châu). Lê Hữu Mục, in his 1960 translation, split the difference and called the episode "the third great deed of Lạc Long Quân, performed together with his father Kinh Dương Vương."

Two explanations exist, and both rest on the history of the text:

**(i) An editorial artifact.** In the original arrangement, Mộc Tinh was story 22, the last, standing outside the Lạc Long Quân cycle. When Vũ Quỳnh reorganized the collection in 1492, he moved it to fourth position, after Ngư Tinh and Hồ Tinh, forming a "three demon battles" cluster. The first two tales name Lạc Long Quân; the third names Kinh Dương Vương. Vũ Quỳnh moved the story but did not revise its content, creating an internal contradiction that later copyists never resolved.

**(ii) Hero assimilation in oral transmission.** Once the trio had taken shape, later tellers assigned all three victories to one figure so the myth cycle would read as a single program. Lạc Long Quân is the central actor of the opening tales, and he absorbed the battle that the written text gives to his father.

The two explanations do not exclude each other: the first created the conditions, the second finished the job. The one certainty is what stands on the page. The original credits Kinh Dương Vương, and anyone citing the original must follow it.

## Textual History: _Manuscripts and Variants_

About fifteen handwritten manuscripts of the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ survive, systematized by Nguyễn Thị Oanh in her doctoral dissertation (Hanoi National University of Education, 2005) and her 720-page monograph (NXB Khoa học Xã hội, 2024). She sorts them into three groups:

- **The Kiều Phú group**: carries Kiều Phú's 1493 postface and his three orthodox Confucian revisions. Representative: ms. A.3 (Institute of Hán-Nôm Studies), 38 stories.
- **The "erased Kiều Phú" group**: copies descending from the Kiều Phú tradition in which scribes deliberately reversed his revisions to restore Vũ Quỳnh's text.
- **The Đoàn Vĩnh Phúc group**: carries Đoàn Vĩnh Phúc's 1554 postscript and adds a third fascicle of 17 stories drawn from the _Việt Điện U Linh Tập_ and elsewhere. Representative: ms. A.2914, the oldest surviving copy, written in a dense cursive hand and transcribed in the Later Lê and Tây Sơn periods.

Kiều Phú's three revisions do not touch the Mộc Tinh story (they concern Tản Viên, Đổng Thiên Vương, and Lý Ông Trọng), but they are the chief markers for grouping the manuscripts. In every manuscript carrying the full 22-story core, Mộc Tinh sits fourth, and its core content is stable. The variation lives in the details:

- **The tree's height**: most manuscripts give "over a thousand arm-spans" (高千餘仞) or "a thousand trượng" (千丈); the "eighty thousand thước" of some popular retellings is an inflation with no textual basis.
- **The exorcist's name**: most manuscripts write Văn Du Tường (文俞祥); the original carries an interlinear note giving Văn Du Niên (文俞年). Nguyễn Hãng's _Thiên Nam Vân Lục Liệt Truyện_ calls him Vân Du. Other sources record Dũ Văn Mâu.
- **The performers' names**: broadly stable, though some manuscripts swap Thượng Can (上竿) and Thượng Hiểm (上險).
- **Annotation layers**: ms. A.2914 carries original geographic annotations under the story's title, a layer absent from HV.486; its prose also runs longer, in the Lê-era Hán manner, where HV.486 is compressed in the Nguyễn-era copying style.

The familiar "red hair and pale skin" and the battle of "seven days and seven nights" or "a hundred days and nights" belong to no Hán manuscript at all. They are modern accretions of the popular retellings. The original says only: he used magic, he won, and the demon did not die.

## In the Official Histories: _No_

**Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư** (Ngô Sĩ Liên, 1479) keeps the Hồng Bàng genealogy but drops all three demon-slaying tales, touching the supernatural layer only through the Lạc Việt custom of tattooing the body against water creatures. Ngô Sĩ Liên states that he consulted the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_, which means he read the Mộc Tinh story and deliberately left it out of the national history.

**Khâm Định Việt Sử Thông Giám Cương Mục** (compiled 1856-1884) goes further and rejects the entire Kinh Dương Vương / Lạc Long Quân stratum. Emperor Tự Đức pronounced the material baseless tales of "ox demons and serpent gods" and demoted all of it to an annotation under the Hùng kings.

Mộc Tinh is therefore a tale purely of the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ line, sharing the fate of Ngư Tinh and Hồ Tinh: the traditional historians knew it, read it, and drew the boundary that keeps it outside the annals with their own hands.

## Motifs and Symbolism: _Three Layers of the Chiên Đàn_

The tree's name carries three layers of meaning, each opening a different reading.

**The Buddhist, Sanskrit layer.** Chiên đàn (旃檀) transcribes _candana_, sandalwood, the most sacred wood in Buddhism, said by tradition to have been carved into the first image of the Buddha. The paradox sits inside the name itself: the holiest of trees becomes the most dangerous of demons. One reading: the greater the spiritual power, the more dangerous its decay.

**The vernacular Nôm layer.** Đặng Tiến and others have proposed that through phonetic derivation, "chiên đàn" can be read as "chàn" or "chằn," the word behind "chằn tinh," an old Vietnamese term for demons in general. Read this way, "cây chiên đàn" simply means "the demon tree," a synonym of "Mộc Tinh" itself. The name wears Sino-Buddhist prestige over a vernacular core.

**The World Tree layer.** A tree a thousand arm-spans tall, shading thousands of leagues, has the dimensions of the World Tree, the axis mundi of Việt-Mường cosmology. The Mường keep a cosmological epic centered on a colossal Si tree that is the pillar of the universe; the Russian scholar Nikolai Nikulin devoted a study to this myth. The chiên đàn of the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ may be a Sinicized, literary rendering of that indigenous World Tree.

## Xương Cuồng and the _Tiger God_

Đặng Tiến, in his 2010 essay "Cọp, từ Mộc Tinh đến ông Ba Mươi," advanced a hypothesis: Xương Cuồng is the Tiger God. His chain of evidence has five links:

**(i)** The sacrifice to Xương Cuồng falls on the 30th day of the 12th month, and the folk name for the tiger is "Ông Ba Mươi," Mr. Thirty.

**(ii)** The Chinese divinatory system Qimen Dunjia includes a malevolent spirit named "Bạch Hổ Xương Cuồng" (白虎猖狂), tying the white tiger directly to the name Xương Cuồng.

**(iii)** In the twelve Earthly Branches, Dần is the tiger; the tenth Heavenly Stem is Kỷ; by Đặng Tiến's reckoning the two combine to make 30, "the day of the tiger sacrifice."

**(iv)** Phạm Đình Hổ, in the 18th-century _Vũ Trung Tùy Bút_, records a cult of a "tiger demon" at Ngọc Cục village (Đường An, Hải Dương) that received living human offerings, and compares it directly to Nhâm Ngao's encounter with the Xương Cuồng sacrifice. That cult lasted until 1800.

**(v)** Ecology: tigers ambush from the roots of great trees. To early observers, the tiger haunting an ancient tree and the spirit of the tree itself could be one being; the line between tree spirit and tiger spirit was thin.

**What remains open.** The hypothesis has not become a scholarly consensus. It is one interpretation, built on its own chain of textual and ethnographic data, and it lacks independent confirmation from archaeology or systematic fieldwork. What all sides do record: the tiger-demon cult at Hải Dương that Phạm Đình Hổ describes was real, lasted until 1800, and Phạm Đình Hổ himself was the first to set it beside the Xương Cuồng sacrifice.

## The Three-Demon Cycle: _Three Demons, Three Ecologies_

Mộc Tinh is the final link of the demon-slaying trio that opens the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_:

| Demon | Hán | Ecology | Territory | Method | Outcome |
|-------|-----|---------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Ngư Tinh | 魚精 | Sea | The eastern coast | Red-hot iron block | Dies; corpse becomes an island |
| Hồ Tinh | 狐精 | Plain, lakes and marshes | Long Biên / West Lake | Water Palace legions flood the den | Dies; den becomes a lake |
| Mộc Tinh | 木精 | Forest and mountains | Phong Châu | Magic, then stratagem | Survives until the Đinh dynasty |

Read as a set, the three tales are a program of civilizing the land: clearing the dangers of the sea, the plain, and the forest, the three ecologies the ancient Vietnamese had to live with, so the people could settle before the Hồng Bàng story divides the hundred sons between mountain and sea. Taylor (1983) reads the whole cycle through the formula "sovereign power came from the sea": the lord of the watery realm moves inland to clear the ground for his people. This is land-clearing, not conquest.

Within that frame, Mộc Tinh holds a place of its own: it is the only demon that **does not die in the first encounter**. Ngư Tinh dies. Hồ Tinh dies. Mộc Tinh is merely "slightly subdued" (稍屈), then lives on for thousands of years, through the Hùng kings, through the Qin, until the Đinh dynasty finishes it. Mapped onto the three ecologies, the detail means this: the dangers of sea and plain, the ancient Vietnamese brought under control relatively fast; the forest, with its predators, its diseases, and its unassimilated communities, held out far longer. The Mộc Tinh story encodes exactly that persistence.

See the Hồ Tinh entry for the middle link of the trio, and the Lạc Long Quân entry for the dragon lord's full career.

## The Sinification _Paradox_

The tale's ending contains a paradox: a native Vietnamese demon that Vietnamese heroes could not destroy is finally finished by a Taoist from the north. Three readings exist:

**(i)** An acknowledgment that Chinese ritual technology ran deeper: the Taoist incantation does what the Vietnamese king's magic could not.

**(ii)** An acknowledgment with a built-in limit: the Vietnamese heroes did the main work thousands of years earlier, and the northerner only cleaned up an enemy already beaten down.

**(iii)** Pragmatic syncretism: Vietnamese culture absorbed useful Taoist technique while keeping its own mythological frame.

Liam Kelley (Journal of Vietnamese Studies 7.2, 2012, pp. 87-130) pushes past all three readings. He argues that the entire Hồng Bàng cycle, the three demon battles included, is an "invented tradition" in Hobsbawm's sense, built by fifteenth-century Vietnamese literati rather than remembered from millennia of oral transmission. His data: the text is written in classical Chinese, a language no one spoke; several tales owe ideas and whole passages to other written works; and Vũ Quỳnh's own preface links the collection to Gan Bao's fourth-century _Soushen Ji_. Tạ Chí Đại Trường published a rebuttal in the same journal issue (pp. 139-162). Half a century earlier, Gaspardone (BEFEO 34, 1934, pp. 76-77) had found in the collection elements that are not Chinese at all, shared instead with Southeast Asia.

**What remains open.** The debate turns on one question: how much of the Mộc Tinh story is old oral inheritance, and how much is early Lê composition? Kelley's side holds the textual evidence; the other side holds the Southeast Asian motif layer that Gaspardone and Taylor both recorded, which is hard to assign to a fifteenth-century writing desk. This is a question of dating and authorship. It does not touch what the tale's own details assert about the Vietnamese as a distinct community, with its own land, its own lord, and its own way of dealing with what threatened it.

## Geography: _Phong Châu and Bạch Hạc_

Phong Châu was the political center of Văn Lang, the region of the Hùng temple on Nghĩa Lĩnh mountain. Bạch Hạc, where the white cranes nested in the chiên đàn, is now a ward of Việt Trì city in Phú Thọ province, at the meeting of three rivers: the Red, the Black, and the Lô.

The fiercest demon of the trio appears at the sacred heart of the kingdom. That placement is hard to read as accident: the tale sets its threat against the core of the community's identity rather than in some distant corner of forest. And unlike Hồ Tinh's West Lake, this place keeps no physical trace of its tale. The Xương Cuồng shrine is gone, the chiên đàn survives only in text, and only the toponym Bạch Hạc still vouches for the opening scene.

## China and _Southeast Asia_

The concept of **tinh** (精, Chinese _jīng_) supplies the tale's mechanism: all things hold latent spiritual essence, and over time, absorbing the essence of sun and moon (日月精華), a thing may become a conscious being. Chinese zhiguai literature has a clear precedent: in the _Soushen Ji_, Penghou (彭侯) is the "essence of wood" (木之精), a creature living inside a thousand-year camphor tree, shaped like a black dog without a tail.

Across Southeast Asia, the tree spirit is a standing motif. Myanmar has the Yokkaso dwelling in great trees; Thailand has Nang Mai and Nang Ta-khian; the Philippines has the anito of the balete tree; Laos has the Phi Pa, the forest ghost. The shared template runs in four steps: a tree accumulates spiritual power with age; the ancient tree becomes the seat of a strong spirit; the spirit demands offerings; harm to the tree brings disaster. Taylor (1983, p. 5, n. 15) compared Mộc Tinh directly to a tree-spirit legend of Borneo (from Henry Ling Roth, _The Natives of Sarawak and British North Borneo_, 1:177-78), in which a felled tree regrows and its spirit demands worship and sacrifice. The structures are nearly identical.

Mộc Tinh sits squarely inside that Southeast Asian template, dressed in a Chinese coat: a specific historical chronology running from the Hùng kings to the Đinh dynasty, a Chinese Taoist as the final victor, and classical Chinese as the medium. How thick that coat is, and who tailored it, is where the modern scholarship divides (see the Sinification Paradox above).

## Modern _Scholarship_

In international scholarship, Mộc Tinh has almost no file of its own. Taylor's entire _The Birth of Vietnam_ gives it one footnote (n. 15, p. 5), and that footnote exists to make the Borneo comparison. The three demon battles are usually discussed as a unit, without separating the individual demons. No peer-reviewed article in English focuses on Mộc Tinh alone. The fullest treatment available is Nguyễn Thị Oanh's 720-page textual study (2024), which has not yet reached an international readership. Xương Cuồng has its own Vietnamese Wikipedia article; on the English side there is close to nothing.

The large open question is the historicity of the human sacrifice. Phạm Đình Hổ records a real sacrificial cult in Hải Dương lasting until 1800, but that was a tiger cult, not a tree cult. Archaeology has produced no independent evidence of human sacrifice to a tree spirit at Phong Châu. The tale may encode a real historical memory, or it may be a narrative structure that borrows a historical timeline to claim credibility. Both possibilities stand, and neither has gained decisive evidence.

## Living _Legacy_

Mộc Tinh is the least known demon of the trio. Hồ Tinh has West Lake in the middle of Hà Nội. Ngư Tinh has Bạch Long Vĩ island as its monument. Mộc Tinh has nothing left: the Xương Cuồng shrine is lost, the chiên đàn exists only on paper, and the name Bạch Hạc survives without most people connecting it to the legend.

In popular culture, Xương Cuồng appears now and then in games, comics, and web novels as "the strongest demon of the southern land," usually drawn as a root monster or a giant tree-man. All of that imagery is modern invention; the original describes no appearance at all. Children's versions go the other way and cut the human sacrifice and Văn Du Tường entirely, keeping only a single victorious battle against a tree demon. What disappears in both cases is exactly what sets the tale apart: the thousands of years in which the demon would not die, the sacrifice that ran until the Đinh dynasty, and the northern Taoist's hand in the ending.

## Notes _for Use_

**Who wins the first battle.** The Hán original credits Kinh Dương Vương (涇陽王以術勝之). Folk tradition and much of the scholarship (including Taylor 1983) credit Lạc Long Quân. Citing the original means following Kinh Dương Vương; telling the folk version means saying that it is the folk version.

**The location.** The original says Phong Châu. Taylor's footnote says "Me-linh"; do not use Mê Linh when citing the original.

**The demon's appearance.** The original gives none. Red hair, pale skin, root monsters, and tree-men are all modern inventions.

**The length of the battle.** "Seven days and seven nights" and "a hundred days and nights" appear in no Hán manuscript.

**The tree's size.** The original: "over a thousand arm-spans" (some manuscripts: a thousand trượng). "Eighty thousand thước" is a popular inflation.

**The exorcist's name.** Văn Du Tường (文俞祥); an interlinear note in the original gives Văn Du Niên (文俞年); the _Thiên Nam Vân Lục Liệt Truyện_ calls him Vân Du; some sources record Dũ Văn Mâu.

**Nhâm Ngao / Nhâm Hiêu.** The same person (任囂), two readings of one name.

**The sacrifice.** No independent archaeological evidence confirms human sacrifice to a tree spirit at Phong Châu. The real cult Phạm Đình Hổ records in Hải Dương (until 1800) was a tiger cult, not a tree cult.

**Thi viện misattribution.** Several websites (Thi viện among them) misattribute the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ to Nguyễn Dữ. The LNCQ was compiled by Trần Thế Pháp and edited by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú; Nguyễn Dữ wrote the _Truyền Kỳ Mạn Lục_.
