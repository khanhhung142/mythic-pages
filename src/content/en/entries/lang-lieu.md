## The Story

Of the twenty-two sons of the sixth Hùng king, Lang Liêu was the eighteenth. In a court where every son held the rank of quan lang, eighteenth meant close to invisible: too far down to be counted on, not far enough down to be indulged. His mother had been a woman of low rank in the palace, without power or family behind her, and she had died of illness years before. His brothers kept households full of retainers; around Lang Liêu there were only a few people. He lived quietly, nearer to the rice fields than to the throne hall.

That year the country had just come out of war. An invading army of the Ân, come down from the north, had been beaten back, and the borders were quiet again. The king, feeling his age, summoned his twenty-two sons and set the condition by which he would choose an heir: whoever found the finest, rarest food and offered it to the former kings at the year's end, fulfilling the way of a filial son, would receive the throne.

The words were barely spoken before the princes began planning their expeditions. Some fitted out boats to hunt the sea for rare fish, some sent men up into the forests after strange game. Precious things piled up in their households, more than anyone could count.

Lang Liêu went back to his own quarters, and there, night after night, he lay awake. He had no boats to send to sea, no men to send into the mountains. And the appointed day came closer with every sunrise. What he feared was not exactly losing the throne; the throne had never been considered his to lose. He feared the day of the offering itself: twenty-one brothers each with a gift for the former kings, and he alone with empty hands, a son who could not complete a son's duty. By day he could think of nothing. By night he could not sleep.

Then one night, in his broken sleep, someone came to him. The visitor had the shape of a man but was not an ordinary man, and he stood before Lang Liêu and said:

"Among all the things of heaven and earth, nothing is more precious to people than rice. Rice feeds them; by rice they grow strong. One never tires of eating it, and nothing can take its place. Make cakes of glutinous rice, some square and some round, to figure the shapes of heaven and earth. Wrap them in leaves and hide good things inside, as a sign of the care of the parents who bore and raised you."

Lang Liêu woke with a start. It was still dark. He sat up alone in the night, and for the first time in many nights his chest was light. He said to himself, gladly: "The divine one has helped me."

The thing the god had told him to use, he had. Glutinous rice was no rarity from mountain or sea; it sat in his own granary, it grew in his own village fields. In the morning he set to work. He chose the whitest rice, picked through it grain by grain, keeping only the full and unbroken ones, and washed them clean. He wrapped the rice in green leaves into a square, set the good filling at the heart, bound it tight, and boiled it long and thoroughly: the shape of the earth, with the things of the world hidden inside. Then he steamed a batch of rice, pounded it smooth, and shaped it into a perfect round: the shape of heaven. No one guided his hands. The dream had given him the meaning; which rice to choose, which leaves to cut, how long to boil, that was the work of hands that knew work.

At the year's end, the offerings of the twenty-two princes covered the palace courtyard. Delicacies of the mountains and seas stood heaped on every side. Lang Liêu's table held two kinds of cake.

The king tasted each offering in turn. At Lang Liêu's cakes he stopped, ate, found the taste good and unlike anything he knew, and called his son forward. Lang Liêu told him the dream and unfolded the meaning: square and round, earth and heaven, the leaves outside and the filling within. The king weighed it and gave his judgment: this offering, of all of them, was the right one for the former kings. The eighteenth son would rule.

The twenty-one brothers went out to hold the frontier lands, and villages and settlements grew up where they went. And in the capital, the son no one had counted sat on the throne, not by great boats or numerous retainers, but by white rice and a dream he had trusted in the right way.

## The character in _Chưng Bính Truyện_

Lang Liêu (郎僚) appears in writing in one place: **Chưng Bính Truyện** (蒸餅傳), the eighth tale of juan I in Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái, in the redaction completed by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú in 1492-1493. Outside LNCQ and its derivatives, no early text records him. Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư keeps only the framework of eighteen Hùng reigns in its outer annals; Việt Sử Lược compresses the whole era into a notice. Lang Liêu is a wholly legendary figure: he exists in a tale, in the ngọc phả registers, and in a cake, and nowhere in the historical record.

For the full text, the Classical Chinese original, the manuscripts, and the editorial history of the tale itself, see **Chưng Bính Truyện: The Tale of the Steamed Cake**. This entry concerns the figure.

## Variant traditions: _name and genealogy_

**The name.** LNCQ writes 郎僚, Lang Liêu. The ngọc phả branch and oral tradition write 薛郎僚, Tiết Lang Liêu, shortened to **Tiết Liêu**. The literary text keeps "Lang Liêu"; most Vietnamese grew up hearing "Tiết Liêu" through the popular retellings in circulation since the early twentieth century.

**The registers make him the seventh Hùng king.** The Hùng Đồ Thập Bát Diệp Thánh Vương Ngọc Phả Cổ Truyền, issued in 1472 under Lê Thánh Tông, gives the tale's ending a dynastic sequel: Lang Liêu ascends as **Hùng Chiêu Vương Minh Tông hoàng đế**, seventh of the eighteen Hùng kings. Fixing a legendary figure into an official king list, with temple name and reign, was standard procedure in the Lê and Nguyễn ngọc phả system: it turned story into something shaped like history. Maspero (BEFEO 18, 1918, p. 1, fn. 2) noted a popular pamphlet on "the history of the eighteen Hùng reigns" distributed at the annual Đền Hùng ceremony, which belongs to the same late fifteenth-century project.

**The number of brothers.** LNCQ counts twenty-two quan lang (公子二十二人). Most versions in circulation today, textbooks included, say twenty sons. Lang Liêu's place as eighteenth is stable across every branch.

**The dream figure.** LNCQ calls him 神人, a divine person, and gives no name. Some contemporary commentary on the internet, citing Việt Điện U Linh, identifies him with Hậu Tắc (后稷), the grain lord of the Chinese classics. The medieval text offers no basis for the identification; it is a modern reading.

**The meaning of the filling.** The critical apparatus records two readings at the same point: the leaves and filling signify 父母生育之重, "the weight of parental rearing," or 父母生育之狀, "the form of parental rearing." Weight or shape: one character's difference, two ways of understanding the cake.

**The modern folk version** (Wikisource; Grade 6 textbooks) adds emotional texture the medieval text does not have: the mother's death told at length, the brothers mocking Lang Liêu, the prince sitting alone in despair. All later accretion, added for a general audience and above all for children.

## Cultural significance

### The orphan and the king's real question

The tale is built on a deliberate paradox: a contest in honoring one's ancestors is won by the son with the least to offer. Lang Liêu has no rarities; he has rice, and a dream telling him that rice is exactly what matters.

Nguyễn Đổng Chi (Lược Khảo về Thần Thoại Việt Nam, 1956) read the story as a creed of wet-rice civilization: the plainest thing, the daily grain and a pair of hands, defeats the delicacies of mountain and sea because it stands closer to the earth and to the actual life of the people. Lang Liêu does not end the story rich. He ends it worthy, because he understood the question his father was asking. That question, read carefully, is not "who has the most impressive thing." The king asks who can offer the *right* thing to the former kings and so complete the way of a filial son. Lang Liêu, who lives nearer the grain than any of his twenty-one brothers, is the only one who answers it. The tale keeps him exactly there: he wins by virtue and by understanding, and by nothing so cheap as luck or cleverness.

One silence in the structure deserves notice. The motherless son wins the contest that honors parental care. The cake he makes signifies "the weight of the parents' rearing," and his own mother is not there to see it. The silence is in the Classical Chinese original; no later teller added it.

### Square and round: a borrowed frame, local matter

The scheme the dream figure uses to explain the cakes, round heaven and square earth (天圓地方), belongs to the gaitian (蓋天) cosmology of the Chinese classics, attested from the Zhoubi Suanjing and the Lüshi Chunqiu; Đinh Gia Khánh and Nguyễn Ngọc San (1960) identified the source. What the tale does with the borrowed frame is the Vietnamese part: it pours heaven and earth into glutinous rice, green leaves, and bean filling, the matter of the delta fields, and turns a cosmological proposition into a thing held in two hands. Lê Hữu Mục (1960) observed that the dream figure's explanation is "very objective and grounded in reality": the square cake is visibly square, the round cake visibly round, and whoever picks one up reads the sign without an interpreter.

Cao Huy Đỉnh (Tìm Hiểu Tiến Trình Văn Học Dân Gian Việt Nam, 1974) adds a further reading: the mother who dies out of favor and the son who triumphs reflect the passage from a matrilineal order to a Confucian patrilineal one; the woman is pushed out of the story, and her care is preserved inside the cake. The text never says this. Its structure bears the reading.

### Culture hero: the god teaches, the man makes

Few figures in the Hùng-king cycle receive divine instruction through a dream. Phù Đổng Thiên Vương is a different case altogether: Thánh Gióng *is* divine, a god in flesh. Lang Liêu is an ordinary man who is given guidance and then does the work himself. The structure, a god teaches and a human makes, is the standing template of the **culture hero** in agricultural mythology: knowledge of cultivation or food-making is sacralized by arriving through a divine intermediary. What is Lang Liêu's own in this template is the division of labor. The dream gives him meaning: square and round, heaven and earth, the parents' care. The choices belong to him: which grains, which leaves, how long the fire burns. The line "Lang Liêu woke startled and said with joy: the divine one has helped me" (郎僚驚覺，喜曰：神人助我也) is one of the least commented moments in the tale, and one of the most human. He is glad because he finally holds the answer to the question that had kept him awake, and for no other reason. No miracle was handed to him; a way of working was.

### From royal offering to household cake

In the LNCQ text, the two cakes are an offering to the former kings at the year's end, bound to court ritual and to the legitimacy of the throne. Today bánh chưng is the Tết cake of every household. The passage from palace rite to family custom is the folklorization that most practices tied to the Hùng cult underwent; in this case the object kept the square and round shapes whose meaning the tale had fixed. In May 2023, the Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism added "the craft of making bánh chưng and bánh giầy" to the national intangible cultural heritage register.

## Living _heritage_

**Education.** "Sự tích bánh chưng bánh giầy" is the legend unit of the Grade 6 literature curriculum, in every current textbook series. Lang Liêu is among the first models of filial piety and the dignity of plain work that Vietnamese children meet in school.

**Art.** Tạ Huy Long's illustrated Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái (NXB Kim Đồng, 2017) renders Lang Liêu and his cakes in a modern graphic idiom and carried the figure to a new generation of readers.

**Worship.** Local ngọc phả records place Lang Liêu shrines in the Việt Trì area of Phú Thọ, concentrated at Hương Trầm and Dữu Lâu. His cult is narrow beside that of the Hùng kings or Lạc Long Quân: he is remembered through the story and the cake far more than through temples.

**Daily life.** Every Tết, millions of families wrap bánh chưng. The maker may not think of Lang Liêu at all. But when the story is told over the boiling pot, it is still the same story: the son who had nothing, taught in a dream, winning everything with rice.

## Notes _for use_

**Lang Liêu is a legendary figure, not a historical one.** No annal records his life; the only sources are LNCQ, a medieval literary text, and the ngọc phả, which are hagiography. To write of him as a datable king is to repeat the 1472 registers' historicizing operation, not to report history.

**The ngọc phả serve the history of the cult, not chronology.** The title Hùng Chiêu Vương and the reign figures attached to it are products of the Lê court's canonization project. They document belief and communal memory, nothing earlier.

**Two names, two branches.** "Lang Liêu" follows LNCQ; "Tiết Liêu" follows the ngọc phả and the folk tradition. Citations should say which branch they use.

**Thi viện misattribution.** Some websites credit LNCQ to Nguyễn Dữ. Nguyễn Dữ wrote Truyền Kỳ Mạn Lục, a different work.

For the tale's full textual dossier, see **Chưng Bính Truyện: The Tale of the Steamed Cake**.
