## The Story

An Dương Vương, king of Âu Lạc, of the Thục clan and named Phán, wanted to raise a great citadel in the land of Việt Thường to hold his country for the long run. He chose the ground, mustered the people, and began piling up the ramparts. But whatever was built by day collapsed by night. As fast as the walls went up they fell, again and again, all the labor melting back into level earth. The king had an altar set up, kept a fast, and prayed to heaven and earth to learn why the ground would not hold his walls.

One day an old man came from the east and stood before the gate, sighing that the citadel would never be finished. The king, glad, welcomed him in and asked his counsel. The old man said: an Envoy of Thanh Giang will come and build the walls together with the king, and only then will they stand. Having said this, he left.

The next day the king waited at the eastern gate. Sure enough, a golden turtle rose from the water and came walking across its surface, calling itself the Envoy of Thanh Giang, versed in the affairs of heaven and earth, of yin and yang, of ghosts and spirits. The king received it into the citadel with joy. The turtle told him what was wrong: this ground was haunted by the ghosts of the ancient dead and by spirit creatures, and their ringleader was a white-chicken spirit hidden on Mount Thất Diệu, which rose each night to throw down the walls. The turtle taught the king how to be rid of them, and the white-chicken spirit and its ghosts were destroyed. After that the building went smoothly. In half a month the citadel stood, more than a thousand trượng around, coiled like a snail shell, and so it was called Loa Thành, the Snail Citadel.

The turtle stayed with the king about three years and then asked to return to the water. Before it left, the king thanked it, saying that thanks to the spirit his walls now held, but if an enemy came from outside, what would he defend himself with. The turtle then shed one of its claws and gave it to the king, telling him to make it into a crossbow trigger; whenever an enemy came, he had only to bring out that crossbow and shoot, and he need fear nothing. The king had his minister Cao Lỗ make the crossbow, using the turtle claw as the trigger. Finished, it was named the Sacred Radiance Golden-Claw crossbow, and a single shot loosed a volley of arrows, so the enemy fell like cut grass.

At that time Triệu Đà brought his army against Âu Lạc. The king brought out the magic crossbow and shot, and Đà's troops were badly beaten and had to pull back and hold their ground, suing for peace. Đà then sent his son Trọng Thủy to ask for the hand of the king's daughter, Mỵ Châu. The king agreed, gave his daughter in marriage, and let Trọng Thủy live in the citadel as his son-in-law. The young couple loved each other dearly. One day Trọng Thủy sweet-talked his wife into letting him see the magic crossbow in secret. Suspecting nothing, Mỵ Châu brought her husband in to look. Trọng Thủy studied how the trigger was made, then fashioned a false trigger and, choosing a quiet moment, swapped it for the true turtle-claw trigger, which he hid away. His work done, he asked leave to visit his father. At their parting he asked his wife: if one day our two countries fall out, north and south cut off from each other, where shall I find you? Mỵ Châu answered: I have a coat of goose down; wherever I go I will pluck the down and scatter it along the road as a sign.

Trọng Thủy carried the trigger home. Once Triệu Đà had it, he raised his army at once and came. Trusting in his crossbow, the king sat playing a board game and laughed, saying: does Đà not fear the magic crossbow? But when the enemy troops fell upon him and he brought out the crossbow to shoot, its power was gone. His army broke. The king set Mỵ Châu behind him on his horse and fled south. Wherever they went, Mỵ Châu plucked her goose down and scattered it, and Trọng Thủy's troops followed the trail of down in pursuit.

The king fled all the way to the shore of the East Sea. Before him lay open water, behind him the enemy; there was nowhere left to go. The king raised his face and called on the Envoy of Thanh Giang to save him. The golden turtle parted the water, surfaced, and cried out: "The one sitting behind you on the horse is the enemy!" The king came to his senses, turned, and cut down Mỵ Châu with his sword. Before she died she prayed: if she had kept faith and been deceived by another, let her die and become pearls to wash away the shame. Her blood ran into the water, and the sea clams that swallowed it turned it into pearls. The king took his seven-inch rhinoceros horn and followed the turtle down through the parted water into the sea.

Trọng Thủy came after, and found only his wife's body. He carried it back and buried it at Loa Thành, and it turned to jade. Unable to bear his grief, Trọng Thủy threw himself into a well inside the citadel and died. Ever after, people say, if you take water from that well to wash the East Sea pearls, the pearls shine the brighter.

## The plot as recorded in _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_

In the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_, the tale bears the Chinese title 金龜傳 (Kim Quy truyện), the Tale of the Golden Turtle. Its plot runs in four stages: the turtle helps An Dương Vương clear the spirits and build Loa Thành; the turtle gives a claw for the crossbow trigger; Triệu Đà uses a marriage alliance to seize the trigger; and in the final scene at the shore, the turtle names Mỵ Châu the "enemy behind." The [text as quoted by the Cổ Loa Relic Site Management Board](https://thanhcoloa.vn/truyen-ve-duc-vua-an-duong-vuong-qua-thu-tich-co) has the turtle call itself "Envoy of Thanh Giang," knowing "the affairs of heaven and earth, of yin and yang, of ghosts and spirits," and, in giving the claw, instruct that it be made into the trigger named "Sacred Radiance Golden-Claw crossbow."

Kim Quy is not the tale's largest presence, but it is the hinge of every turning point. When the turtle appears, the walls can be built; when it gives the claw, the country can be defended; when it speaks one sentence, the tragedy closes. The deity does not act in the human's place: it supplies the human with means (a way to clear the spirits, a claw, one warning), while victory or defeat stays in human hands. Âu Lạc falls not because the crossbow lost its power but because a state defense secret leaked out along a line of kinship.

## Two cycles of Kim Quy: _Cổ Loa and Hoàn Kiếm_

The Vietnamese Golden Turtle lives in two separate stories, roughly sixteen centuries apart in their legendary settings.

**The first cycle, Cổ Loa**, is the root tale bound to An Dương Vương, recorded early in the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ and the dynastic histories. Here the turtle gives humans a weapon to found and hold a country.

**The second cycle, Hoàn Kiếm**, belongs to Lê Lợi and the resistance against Ming occupation in the early 15th century. Long Quân lent the Lam Sơn army the sacred Thuận Thiên sword; after the enemy was driven out and Lê Lợi had taken the throne, he was boating on Tả Vọng Lake when the Golden Turtle surfaced to reclaim the sword for Long Quân. The king returned it, the turtle took it in its mouth and sank, and from then the lake bore the name Hoàn Kiếm (還劍), "returned sword." The [version held by SCOV](https://scov.gov.vn/que-huong/goc-thieu-nhi/truyen-thuyet-ve-ho-guom.html) identifies the Golden Turtle as the emissary Long Quân sent to take back the sacred sword.

The two cycles are independent in text and dating. What joins them is a shared motif: the turtle deity as emissary of the water spirit, lending or granting a weapon to defend the country and then reclaiming it. At Cổ Loa the deity gives a claw, and the theft of that claw brings the country down. At Hoàn Kiếm the deity lends a sword and deliberately calls it back once the work is done. Popular tradition and modern schoolbooks call both "the Golden Turtle" or "Kim Quy," a symbol of Long Quân, though the source texts do not fuse the two cycles into one.

## Origins: _the golden-turtle layer is a later accretion_

The motif of a spirit helping An Dương Vương build a crossbow has a very old root in Chinese texts, but the original helper was not a golden turtle. The _Records of the Outer Territories of Giao Châu_ (Giao Châu ngoại vực ký, quoted in the _Commentary on the Water Classic_) records a spirit named Cao Thông who came down to help An Dương Vương make a crossbow that killed three hundred men at a shot. At this textual layer there is no turtle. The golden turtle, the Thanh Giang title, the destruction of the white-chicken spirit, and the building of Loa Thành are a later accretion, crystallized in the storytelling tradition and then set down in full form by the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_. [Trần Quốc Vượng and Đỗ Văn Ninh](https://his.ussh.vnu.edu.vn/vi/news/nguyen-thanh-dat/ve-an-duong-vuong-tran-quoc-vuong-do-van-ninh-5708.html) read the An Dương Vương legend as a multilayered complex, in which the crossbow and Cổ Loa attach to a real historical kernel of a citadel and a military technology, while the golden turtle belongs to the mythic layer laid over it.

This layering matters because it separates three things often merged into one: the real citadel of Cổ Loa (archaeology), the story of An Dương Vương (historical legend), and the deity Kim Quy (myth). All three can be true in their own way without any one having to prove another.

## Position within _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_

The Tale of the Golden Turtle belongs to the "historical, diplomatic, political" cluster of the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_, standing beside Bạch Trĩ (set in the Western Zhou) and Lý Ông Trọng (set in the Qín). If Bạch Trĩ establishes equal standing with the north from high antiquity, Kim Quy establishes a link of succession: from the Hồng Bàng clan and the Hùng kings to An Dương Vương and the country of Âu Lạc. In ms. A.1300 (which has lost its opening), the Kim Quy tale survives from its second half and is numbered 13, showing its place in that recension, though other recensions arrange their story counts differently (see Variants). On the book's authorship and date, the [Encyclopedia of Vietnam](https://bktt.vn/L%C4%A9nh_Nam_tr%C3%ADch_qu%C3%A1i_li%E1%BB%87t_truy%E1%BB%87n) records that the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ is traditionally ascribed to Trần Thế Pháp of the late Trần, edited by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú, who wrote the preface around the Hồng Đức years 1492-1493.

## Present in official history, _but the historian doubts himself_

The _Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư_ (Ngô Sĩ Liên, 1697 printing) records the An Dương Vương story in the Ngoại kỷ, in the Thục era, in annalistic form: it adds dates, pares down the marvelous details, and turns the story into a dated sequence of events. Yet Ngô Sĩ Liên does not hide his reserve. He records the turtle and the crossbow and then raises a question about the credibility of things so strange, keeping the same "use it and hold it at arm's length" stance he applies to the whole high-antiquity section. The [1697 Toàn Thư text](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/History-of-Greater-Vietnam?uiLang=vn) places the Thục era directly before the section on the Triệu, meaning the historian treated An Dương Vương and Âu Lạc as a real dynasty in the timeline of the Vietnamese country, even as he left the turtle episode itself open to doubt.

## The Hoàn Kiếm cycle: _from "obtaining the sword" to "returning the sword"_

The Hoàn Kiếm cycle is not in the dynastic histories in the form familiar today. The _Lam Sơn thực lục_ (1431, prefaced by Lê Thái Tổ) records only that Lê Lợi obtained a sword inscribed with the two characters Thuận Thiên (順天), "in accord with heaven," and the single character Lợi, taking it as a sign of the mandate; it has no scene of returning the sword to the Golden Turtle. The _Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư_ in its Lê section likewise records no borrowing or returning of a sword.

[Lê Thời Tân and colleagues (Journal of Science, HCMC University of Education)](https://nghiencuulichsu.com/2019/08/05/truyen-thuyet-ho-hoan-kiem-mot-cach-doc-lien-van-ban/) reconstruct how the story formed: later records such as the _Tang thương ngẫu lục_, the _Sơn cư tạp thuật_, the _Đại Nam nhất thống chí_, and the verse collection _Long Biên bách nhị vịnh_ stress the detail of a "lost sword" at the lake, and only around the late 19th century were the loose pieces strung into a complete "returned sword" story tied to the name Hoàn Kiếm Lake. This is a textbook case of a place name growing a legend: the name of the lake came first, and the story explaining the name was built up afterward to fit.

Separating two questions here does not touch the stance. The first question is textual: when the returned-sword story took shape. The second is meaning: the Lam Sơn resistance was the just war of a people reclaiming independence from Ming occupation. The first question remains open on dating; the second is not a reading open to debate.

## Motif and symbol: _the claw-trigger and the enemy behind_

**The magic crossbow is a native weapon deified.** The turtle claw as a trigger, one shot loosing a volley of arrows, is the legend's way of mythologizing a real military-technical secret of the ancient Việt. The story gives a supernatural origin to a thoroughly earthly capacity: casting and firing bronze arrows in mass. Precisely because that weapon is the Việt's own, made by Việt hands (through Cao Lỗ) from a spirit's gift, its loss to the enemy means the loss of the country. The weapon does not betray; people do.

**The enemy behind.** The line "the one sitting behind you on the horse is the enemy" is the moral core of the whole tale. The deity points not to the enemy ahead, toward which the king is fleeing, but to the enemy directly behind, the person closest to him. The tragedy of Âu Lạc comes not from a lack of weapons or courage but from a lapse of vigilance within the household itself. Mỵ Châu is thus a double figure: censured for letting a state secret slip, and pitied because she kept faith and was deceived. The prayer to become pearls and the motif of the well-water that washes those pearls hold both shades together in a single ending.

## The archaeology of Cổ Loa: _kept apart from the legend_

Cổ Loa is a real archaeological site, and the finds there are independent of the golden-turtle legend. According to [Nam C. Kim, Lại Văn Tới, and Trịnh Hoàng Hiệp (_Antiquity_ 84/326, 2010, pp. 1011-1027)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/antiquity/article/abs/co-loa-an-investigation-of-vietnams-ancient-capital/00332829F65222D3FB94642A83A09979), the main rampart of the middle circuit was built in the later centuries BCE, before the arrival of Han imperial China, and it was not even the first defense on the spot. A charcoal sample from a trash pit beside a kiln used to produce arrowheads gave a radiocarbon measurement of 2190±35 BP, per the [open PDF of the article](https://os.pennds.org/archaeobib_filestore/pdf_articles/Antiquity/2010_84_326_Kimetal.pdf).

The same article records that at Cầu Vực, just outside Cổ Loa's southern gate, a hoard of about ten thousand tanged bronze projectile points was found. This vast arrow hoard is where the crossbow legend and the archaeological record meet suggestively: a concentrated, large-scale weapons-production center, exactly where the legend places Loa Thành and the magic crossbow. But meeting is not proof. [Nam C. Kim (_The Origins of Ancient Vietnam_, Oxford University Press, 2015)](https://www.anthropology.wisc.edu/staff/kim-nam-c/) reads Cổ Loa as one of the earliest cases of state and urban formation in Southeast Asia, on the strength of material evidence, not of the turtle story. The turtle claw stays with the myth; the hoard of ten thousand arrowheads stays with the archaeology.

## Shrine and worship: _Kim Quy at Cổ Loa_

Kim Quy is not only a character in a tale; at Cổ Loa the golden turtle is an object of actual worship. [According to material from the Cổ Loa Relic Site Management Board](https://thanhcoloa.vn/truyen-ve-duc-vua-an-duong-vuong-qua-thu-tich-co), within the cult of An Dương Vương here the golden turtle is worshipped under the title Envoy of Thanh Giang (清江使者), the emissary of the water spirit. The complex holds many investiture decrees of the Lê and Nguyễn dynasties granted to Cổ Loa village for the worship of An Dương Vương, along with local jade registers and spirit records.

This body of material must be read for what it is. Spirit records and jade registers are hagiographic texts serving worship, preserving the memory and belief of a community, not annalistic history. They are valuable evidence about the religious life around Kim Quy and An Dương Vương, about how the Cổ Loa community kept the story alive across centuries, but they are not a basis for dating events of the Âu Lạc period. The worth of the worship layer is that it shows the golden-turtle legend living not only in books but in ritual, in a temple, in the life of one specific village.

## Modern _scholarship_

The An Dương Vương and Kim Quy legend sits within the larger debates of Vietnamese studies over the border between history and myth.

[Keith Taylor (_The Birth of Vietnam_, University of California Press, 1983)](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/the-birth-of-vietnam/paper) devotes a whole appendix to this tale, [Appendix F, "The Legend of the Turtle Claw," pp. 316-319](https://www.degruyterbrill.com/document/doi/10.1525/9780520343108-017/html), analyzing how the turtle-claw legend works within the narrative of An Dương Vương and Âu Lạc. Taylor treats An Dương Vương as a hinge marker in the passage from the Hùng era to an era with a state and citadel walls.

[Liam Kelley (JVS 7/2, 2012, pp. 87-130)](https://doi.org/10.1525/vs.2012.7.2.87) develops the "invented tradition" frame for the high-antiquity tales of the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_: he argues that much of the information about high antiquity was constructed by Vietnamese literati after the country became autonomous, rather than being continuous oral memory from the first millennium BCE. The argument applies to the high-antiquity tradition broadly and bears directly on reading the An Dương Vương cycle as a late-forming text. [Tạ Chí Đại Trường (JVS 7/2, 2012, pp. 139-162)](https://doi.org/10.1525/vs.2012.7.2.139) pushes back, noting that some high-antiquity figures appear in earlier sources and that Hobsbawm's frame should not be applied wholesale to an East Asian setting.

**What remains open.** The textual-dating question (when the turtle-and-crossbow tale took shape) differs from the historical-kernel question (whether there was a real Cổ Loa citadel and a real military technology). On dating, the golden-turtle layer is later than the Cao Thông spirit in the Chinese texts, and the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ records it already in finished form; that is the evidence for the "late-forming" reading. On the kernel, the archaeology of Cổ Loa shows a real rampart and a real weapons center in the last centuries BCE; that is the evidence for the "real core" reading. The two questions do not exclude each other: a late-forming legend can still cluster around a real citadel.

## Variants: _the khảo dị and the Cổ Loa telling_

The tale is told under several titles: "The Tale of the Golden Turtle" and "Thần Kim Quy" in the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_, "Mỵ Châu - Trọng Thủy" or "the magic crossbow story" in the storytelling tradition, "The Thục Era of An Dương Vương" in the _Thiên Nam ngữ lục_.

Nguyễn Đổng Chi places the tale in his _Treasury of Vietnamese Folktales_ under the title "Mỵ Châu - Trọng Thủy, or the magic crossbow story," with a khảo dị (comparative variants) section. Notably, one variant from the Cổ Loa area reverses the familiar ending: the ghost of Mỵ Châu drowns Trọng Thủy, in place of the scene of Trọng Thủy taking his own life out of grief. This variant shows how the local community reread the tragedy: the traitor is not allowed to die in quiet remorse but is called to account by the very victim he wronged. (The exact page in the Nguyễn Đổng Chi set could not be verified against a reputable digitized copy; see Notes for use.)

Among the histories, the level of the marvelous falls as the text nears the annalistic mode: the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ keeps the white-chicken spirit and Mount Thất Diệu in full; the _Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư_ trims them into events; the _Khâm Định Việt Sử Thông Giám Cương Mục_ follows the Toàn Thư without producing a new variant.

## Living legacy

Kim Quy is one of the few figures of Vietnamese myth present at two major sites of the capital, both still alive in daily life. Cổ Loa remains a special national relic site, where An Dương Vương and the Envoy of Thanh Giang are worshipped, with an annual festival. Hoàn Kiếm Lake is the symbolic center of Hà Nội, and the name "returned sword" recalls the Golden Turtle's second cycle every day to millions passing by.

The tale of Mỵ Châu and Trọng Thủy and the legend of Hồ Gươm both sit in the school literature curriculum, making Kim Quy a mythic figure nearly every Vietnamese meets in childhood. The line "the enemy behind" has entered political language as an idiom of vigilance. The image of the great turtle has also been linked to the giant soft-shell turtles that once lived in Hồ Gươm, so that legend and a real animal overlap in the contemporary urban imagination.

## Notes for use

1. **Keep the three layers apart.** The citadel of Cổ Loa (archaeology), the story of An Dương Vương (historical legend), and the deity Kim Quy (myth) are three distinct layers. Do not use the archaeology of Cổ Loa to "prove" a golden turtle, and do not use the turtle story to dismiss the archaeological value of Cổ Loa.

2. **The dates of An Dương Vương belong to the legendary setting.** Sources differ on his reign and the age of Âu Lạc. Do not present these dates as settled history.

3. **The question of the Triệu is open; cite both sides.** How one tells the end of the Cổ Loa cycle depends on what one takes Triệu Đà to be. Vietnamese historiography is split: a classical position counts the Triệu as a legitimate dynasty of the Vietnamese country (Lê Tắc in the _An Nam chí lược_; Ngô Sĩ Liên placing a "Triệu era" in the [_Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư_](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/History-of-Greater-Vietnam?uiLang=vn)), while another, including the mainstream position today, treats Triệu Đà as a foreign invader of Âu Lạc. The question of Nam Việt and the Triệu is discussed by [Keith Taylor (_The Birth of Vietnam_, 1983)](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/the-birth-of-vietnam/paper) in the context of the passage from Âu Lạc to Chinese rule. Present both sides; do not quietly pick one. Read either way, An Dương Vương's resistance was the legitimate self-defense of an independent country, not a "rebellion."

4. **The Hoàn Kiếm cycle formed late.** The scene of returning the sword to the Golden Turtle is not in the _Lam Sơn thực lục_ or the dynastic histories; it took full narrative shape around the late 19th century ([Lê Thời Tân et al.](https://nghiencuulichsu.com/2019/08/05/truyen-thuyet-ho-hoan-kiem-mot-cach-doc-lien-van-ban/)). Do not cite the legend of Hồ Gươm as a 15th-century source.

5. **The Cổ Loa spirit records and jade registers are hagiography.** Use them for religious history and local memory, not for historical chronology.

6. **The "turtle bearing script" of Việt Thường is a different motif.** Chinese texts record Việt Thường thị presenting the sage-king Yáo with a divine turtle (神龜) whose shell was carved with writing. This is a separate sacred-turtle motif, related to but not identical with Kim Quy, and most modern interpretation of "tadpole script" around it circulates on non-scholarly sources. Do not merge this motif with Kim Quy or use it as evidence of an "ancient Việt script."

7. **Quote archaeological figures from the right article.** The _Antiquity_ 2010 article confirms a hoard of about ten thousand bronze projectile points at Cầu Vực; a weight figure of "about 93 kg" often cited in popular material is not in that article. When citing figures, keep to the original.

8. **Check the Taylor quotation against the print edition.** The line attributed to Taylor that An Dương Vương is "the first authentic historical figure" in Vietnam circulates in secondary sources but should be checked directly against the [print edition of _The Birth of Vietnam_](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/the-birth-of-vietnam/paper) before quoting verbatim.

9. **Thi viện misattributes the LNCQ.** Some sites attribute the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ to Nguyễn Dữ; Nguyễn Dữ wrote the _Truyền Kỳ Mạn Lục_. The LNCQ is traditionally ascribed to Trần Thế Pháp, redacted by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú.
