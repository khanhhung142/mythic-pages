## The Story

In high antiquity there lived a Quan Lang, a chieftain, so tall and well-built that the king praised him and granted him the character Cao, meaning "tall," as a surname. Cao had two sons, the elder named Tân and the younger named Lang. The two boys were so alike that when they stood side by side no stranger could tell which was the elder and which the younger. When they were seventeen or eighteen, their parents died one after the other. With no one left to lean on, the brothers went to study under a Daoist teacher of the Lưu family.

The Lưu household had a daughter of about the same age. She was drawn to the two handsome young men and wished to marry the elder, but the brothers were so alike she could not tell which was which. So she devised a test. One day she set out a single bowl of porridge and a single pair of chopsticks and invited both to eat, then watched from where she could not be seen. The younger brother took up the bowl and offered it first to his elder. Now she knew, and she asked her parents for the elder as her husband. From then on Tân and his wife lived under one roof.

From the day he took a wife, the elder brother grew distant from the younger. Where once the two had eaten from one tray and slept on one mat, now the elder was taken up with his household and let the bond cool. The younger felt the hurt of it and told himself his brother had forgotten him for the sake of a wife. One day, without a word, he left home.

He walked on and on until he came to a wide, deep stream that he could not cross. The bank was empty; there was no boat, no oar. He sat down by the water and wept until his strength was gone and he died, and there he turned into a slender tree that grew straight up at the water's edge.

When the younger did not return, the elder brother was seized with remorse and went to look for him. He followed the road his brother had taken and came to the same stream. His brother was nowhere, only a strange tree standing silent by the water. He sat down against its trunk and wept, wept until he too died, and there he turned into a slab of stone that lay curled around the tree's root.

The wife, waiting at home and seeing neither husband return, went out in turn to search. She followed her husband's trail to the same bank, where she found a slab of stone beside a tree. She threw her arms around the stone and wept until she died, and there she turned into a climbing vine, green-leaved and soft-stemmed, that wound around the stone and climbed to embrace the tree. Its leaves had a warm, peppery fragrance.

The Lưu parents, searching for their daughter, found at last only a tree, a stone, and a vine by the lonely stream. When the people of the district told them the whole of it, the old couple in their grief raised a small shrine on the bank. Travelers who passed praised the three: brothers in harmony, husband and wife faithful to the end.

It was then around the seventh or eighth month, in the hard heat of the year, that a Hùng King came through the district on a royal progress. He stopped before the shrine and asked, and so learned the story of the three. Looking at the straight tree, the stone, and the vine clinging to one another, he was moved. He had the fruit of the tree picked and the leaves of the vine gathered, and he raised them to his mouth and chewed. The taste was astringent and fragrant. He spat onto the stone, and a bright red like vermilion appeared. He had that very stone burned into lime, and with a smear of white lime he rolled a betel leaf around a sliver of areca and chewed the three together. This time his mouth was warm, his lips red, his cheeks flushed, the taste fuller than before. Knowing now that these were three precious things, the king ordered all under heaven to grow areca and betel and to burn lime, and from then on to place a quid of betel first at every wedding, every gathering, every rite. The Vietnamese custom of betel chewing began there.

## The Plot according to _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_

In high antiquity a tall chieftain (Quan Lang) received the surname Cao from the king. Cao had two sons, Tân (elder) and Lang (younger), identical in appearance. Their parents died when the two were seventeen or eighteen, and the brothers went to study under a Daoist of the Lưu family. The Lưu daughter wished to marry the elder but could not tell the brothers apart, so she set out one bowl of porridge and one pair of chopsticks; seeing the younger yield to the elder, she knew which was the elder and married him.

After the marriage the elder grew cold toward the younger. The younger, ashamed, left home, reached a deep stream with no boat, wept, and died, becoming an areca palm. The elder went to search, reached the spot, took his own life there, and became a slab of stone curled around the palm's root. The wife searched for her husband, reached the spot, died there, and became a betel vine, its leaves warm and fragrant, winding around the stone and the palm. The Lưu parents raised a shrine.

In the seventh or eighth month, a Hùng King on progress stopped before the shrine and heard the story. He chewed a betel leaf, spat onto the stone, and saw a red color; he had the stone burned into lime and, chewing betel with areca and lime, found his "cheeks flushed and lips red." He ordered the realm to grow areca and betel and to use them first in weddings and rites. The two characters 檳榔 (Tân Lang) joined together are the Chinese name for the areca palm.

The tale ends there. It is a custom-origin story: it does not stop at the tragedy of the three but carries on to the king's decree, turning a private death into a shared national custom.

## The _Structure_ of the tale

Read as a narrative, Tân Lang truyện moves in four beats.

**1. The likeness.** The brothers are so alike that the woman about to marry one of them cannot tell them apart. This motif of indistinguishable twins is the seed of the whole tragedy: it makes confusion possible, and confusion breeds jealousy.

**2. The rift.** The elder's marriage frays the tie between the brothers. The tale assigns no clear blame: the elder cools, the younger feels abandoned. The tragedy grows out of a gap in understanding, not out of a villain.

**3. Three linked deaths.** The younger dies first, the elder follows, the wife dies last. The order matters: each dies while searching for another, so the three deaths are not three separate tragedies but one chain of love and remorse.

**4. Metamorphosis and sanctification.** The three bodies become areca, stone, and betel, three things that yield red when combined. The Hùng King tastes them and issues his decree. This last beat turns the tale from folktale into custom-legend: the private (three kin dead by a stream) is certified by the state and spread as ritual.

The central motif is metamorphosis after death: person into stone (Thompson D231), person into tree (D215), plant from a body (A2611), the origin of a vine (A2681). It is an origin-of-things tale that explains three objects (areca, betel, lime) and one custom (betel chewing, weddings) at once.

## _Position_ within the Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái

Tân Lang truyện is story no. 5 in Vũ Quỳnh's 22-story canon (1492), part of the cluster of custom-origin tales alongside _Chưng Bính truyện_ (the sticky-rice cakes, no. 8) and _Tây Qua truyện_ (the watermelon, no. 9). All three share one function: to explain a living custom through a tale set in the Hùng Vương age, and all three close with a king's endorsement.

Against the other two, this tale leans toward emotional tragedy rather than wit or labor. Lang Liêu wins his contest through filial virtue; Mai An Tiêm survives by his own hands; the three of the Cao and Lưu families die, and only after death become precious things. The betel legend is the only tale in the cluster in which the protagonists must die for the custom to be born.

The Chinese title 檳榔傳 takes the very name of the areca palm (檳榔, tân lang) as its heading, as _Tây Qua truyện_ takes the name of the melon. In some recensions the heading gains a prefix, becoming _Cao Thị Tân Lang truyện_, "the tale of the areca of the Cao family," anchoring the story to the brothers' lineage.

## _Absent_ from official histories

Like the other custom tales in its cluster, Tân Lang truyện does not appear in the dynastic histories.

**Việt sử lược** (c. 1377), the earliest surviving Vietnamese history, has no betel legend. **Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư** (Ngô Sĩ Liên, 1479) records the Hùng Vương period in the Outer Annals only as a general frame, without the story of the three Cao. **Việt Điện U Linh Tập** (Lý Tế Xuyên, 1329) records only spirits granted court investiture, outside the range of custom tales.

The absence marks the same line classical historians drew between tale and history. The betel legend lived in the stream of anomaly literature and oral tradition, entered the Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái as a custom-origin tale, and never entered the annals as an event. The "Hùng Vương period" setting is a genre convention and cannot date the story.

## Variants: _who becomes what_

The sharpest difference among versions is which of the three becomes which object.

In the **Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái**: the younger brother (Lang) becomes the areca palm, the elder (Tân) becomes the stone, and the Lưu wife becomes the betel vine.

In the **Sử Nam chí dị**, the version [Nguyễn Đổng Chi cites in the _Treasury of Vietnamese Folktales_ (The Origin of Betel, Areca and Lime)](https://opac.nlv.gov.vn/chi-tiet-tai-lieu/kho-tang-truyen-co-tich-viet-nam.-t.2-20251142790518142931), the order is reversed: the younger becomes the stone, the elder becomes the areca, and the wife still becomes the betel. This version assigns each form a virtue: the stone signifies purity, the areca signifies uprightness, the betel signifies fragrant fidelity, and it holds that all three had to be buried before Heaven allowed the transformation. Nguyễn Đổng Chi favored this reversed reading as "more fitting" and cited Trần Thanh Mại's agreement.

One point of textual history is worth noting: in his variant notes, [Nguyễn Đổng Chi observes that the detail of the brothers' likeness leading the wife to embrace the wrong brother is not in the Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái](https://opac.nlv.gov.vn/chi-tiet-tai-lieu/kho-tang-truyen-co-tich-viet-nam.-t.2-20251142790518142931), and that he supplied it from the _Tình sử Việt Nam_. Many modern retellings make this mistaken embrace their climax, but it belongs to the later oral layer, not the Chinese original.

Beyond the Kinh-majority versions, related tales exist among other peoples and countries. Nguyễn Đổng Chi records a tale among ethnic minorities in Nghệ An about a woman, Khăm Xuân, and two men who die in fire and become areca and betel, and a Cơ-tu tale from western Quảng Nam. The type of three who die and become betel, areca, and lime (or stone) recurs across much of Southeast Asia, where betel chewing is widespread.

## The _real_ betel quid: what science says

The tale explains the red of the betel quid by a miracle: the king's spittle turns red on the stone. Chemistry explains the same event by a reaction.

A betel quid has three parts: the betel leaf (rich in essential oils and phenolic compounds), the areca nut (containing tannins and the alkaloid arecoline), and slaked lime (an alkali, a mixture of Ca(OH)₂ and CaCO₃). Without lime the quid does not turn red. In an alkaline medium at body temperature, arecoline is converted and the phenolic compounds and tannins react to produce a red color, together with a sensation of warmth and mild stimulation, the "flushed cheeks and red lips" the tale describes. The red that the Hùng King saw on the limestone is, in the end, the color of an alkaline reaction.

Archaeologically, betel chewing in Southeast Asia is very old. The earliest direct biochemical evidence comes from [Piyawit Moonkham et al. (*Frontiers in Environmental Archaeology* 4, 2025)](https://doi.org/10.3389/fearc.2025.1622935): analysis of dental calculus from individuals at the Bronze Age site of Nong Ratchawat in central Thailand, dated to about 4,080-3,850 years ago, by liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry, found arecoline and arecaidine in the teeth of one woman with no visible red staining. This is the earliest direct molecular evidence of betel chewing in the region, and it shows the custom long predates the age the tale assigns to the Hùng King.

On health, the areca nut and the betel quid (with or without tobacco) have been classified by [the WHO's International Agency for Research on Cancer as Group 1, carcinogenic to humans, since 2004 (IARC Monographs Vol. 85)](https://publications.iarc.fr/103), on the basis of sufficient evidence for cancers of the oral cavity, pharynx, and esophagus; the areca nut also causes oral submucous fibrosis, a precancerous condition ([Warnakulasuriya & Ho, *British Dental Journal* 233, 2022, pp. 411-413](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41415-022-4425-1)). According to [Papke et al. (*New England Journal of Medicine* 386, 2022)](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMp2203571), betel chewing is the fourth most common human psychoactive habit, after alcohol, caffeine, and nicotine, with an estimated 200 to 600 million regular users. The cultural value of the quid and its medical risk are two facts that coexist.

## _Cultural_ meaning

The tale explains and sanctifies a custom rooted deep in Vietnamese life. "A quid of betel opens every conversation": betel and areca are what one offers to greet a guest, to become acquainted, to make friends. At a wedding, a tray of betel and areca is a near-obligatory gift, so much so that "to be joined by betel and areca" means to be joined as husband and wife. The art of folding a betel quid into a phoenix shape was once a measure of a woman's dexterity. Betel and areca also appear in ancestor worship, in offerings, in funerals, and in longevity rites.

On the symbolic level, [Trần Ngọc Thêm (*Tìm về bản sắc văn hóa Việt Nam*, NXB TP.HCM, 2nd ed. 1997)](https://search.worldcat.org/title/37746530) reads the quid as a symbol of yin-yang harmony and the three-powers model of heaven, earth, and humanity: the areca rising tall answers to heaven, the lime from stone to earth, the betel vine climbing from the ground the mediating third; only when all three combine does the deep red appear. In the same book he proposes an etymology: that the Chinese names for betel (扶留, phù lưu) and areca (檳榔, tân lang) derive from Old Vietnamese, and that the name of the kingdom Văn Lang is linked to the same forms. This is his own culture-studies hypothesis, still disputed among historical phonologists, and is better presented as a reading than as a conclusion.

## _Scholarship_

Tân Lang truyện has been read along several lines.

The anthropological line sees the tale as the trace of a marriage transition. [Nguyễn Đổng Chi, in the _Treasury of Vietnamese Folktales_](https://opac.nlv.gov.vn/chi-tiet-tai-lieu/kho-tang-truyen-co-tich-viet-nam.-t.2-20251142790518142931), groups this tale with those carrying the residue of an ancient custom in which brothers shared one wife, and reads the Lưu daughter's marrying only one of the two brothers as a marker of monogamy replacing group marriage. This reading follows the 19th-20th-century evolutionary theory of marriage; it is an interpretation laid over the tale, not something the tale states in words.

The textual line places the tale within the larger debate over the whole Hùng Vương cycle in the Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái. [Liam C. Kelley (*Journal of Vietnamese Studies* 7/2, 2012, pp. 87-130)](https://doi.org/10.1525/vs.2012.7.2.87) argues that the cycle is a medieval "invented tradition," built on a Sinitic-literary model rather than a prehistoric oral memory. [Tạ Chí Đại Trường, replying in the same issue (pp. 139-162)](https://doi.org/10.1525/vs.2012.7.2.139), accepts that the text is layered but holds that beneath the Chinese-language surface lies an older folk stratum. [Jenkin Leung (*Beyond the Chinese Façade*, MA thesis, University of California, Berkeley, 2021)](https://escholarship.org/uc/item/2p3293k8) analyzes Tân Lang truyện as one of three tales in which a native core sits beneath a Sino-literary veneer.

**What remains open:** the text of Tân Lang truyện as it survives is a product of the 1492-1493 editing, a point all sides record. Whether the tale has an older oral core or was constructed whole in the medieval period, neither side has settled with decisive evidence. The archaeological record says only that betel chewing is very old; it does not by itself date the betel legend.

## Living _legacy_

According to Nguyễn Đổng Chi's variant notes, the shrine to the three figures is Tam Khương Temple in Nam Hoa village, Nam Đàn district, Nghệ An, which received investiture edicts from successive dynasties. Betel chewing itself has thinned in the cities, but the tray of betel and areca remains a wedding gift hard to drop, and the saying "a quid of betel opens every conversation" is still alive in speech.

The most durable legacy of the tale is one image: three different things, the areca straight and tall, the betel soft and clinging, the lime from stone, each pale on its own, red only when combined. Vietnamese speakers reach for that image to talk about kinship and marriage without retelling the whole tale. The quid has taken over from the story the work of remembering.

## Notes on _use_

**Historicity.** Tân Lang truyện is a custom tale, not a historical source on the Hùng Vương period. The "Hùng Vương" setting is a genre convention. Betel chewing is real and very old, but the tale that explains it belongs to literature and cannot date the founding age.

**State the recension when saying who becomes what.** The Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái version (younger becomes areca, elder becomes stone) and the Sử Nam chí dị version via Nguyễn Đổng Chi (younger becomes stone, elder becomes areca) reverse each other. Citations should say which is being followed.

**The mistaken-embrace detail is a later layer.** The episode of the wife embracing the wrong brother and rousing the elder's jealousy is not in the Chinese original; Nguyễn Đổng Chi records that he supplied it from the _Tình sử Việt Nam_.

**Thi viện misattributes the author.** Some websites, Thi viện among them, wrongly attribute the Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái to Nguyễn Dữ. Nguyễn Dữ wrote the _Truyền Kỳ Mạn Lục_, a different work.

**Medical note.** When presenting the cultural value of betel and areca, it is worth pairing it with the IARC warning on oral cancer risk so that readers have balanced information.
