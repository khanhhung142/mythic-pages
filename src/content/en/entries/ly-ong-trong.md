## The Story

*This telling follows the folk version collected by Nguyễn Đổng Chi in the Kho tàng truyện cổ tích Việt Nam. The Việt điện u linh, the Lĩnh Nam chích quái, and the dynastic annals differ on several points; see Comparing the sources below.*

Long ago, in the village of Thụy Hương in Từ Liêm district, the village now called Chèm on the bank of the Red River just upstream of Hà Nội, a boy was born to a family named Lý. They called him Thân. He grew, and did not stop growing. By the time he was a young man he stood, the story says, two trượng and six thước by the old measures, taller than the ridgepole of any house in the village, and one of his strides covered more ground than another man's run. People in that country soon stopped using his given name. They called him Ông Trọng.

The stretch of river below the village held a monster in those days: a giải, a creature of the thuồng luồng kind, a great water serpent that lay in the deep pools and took people from the landing. One day it took his mother. Ông Trọng came down to the bank, bailed that whole reach of the river dry, found the creature in the mud and killed it, and brought his mother home. After that the people of both banks went down to the water without fear, and word of the giant of Chèm traveled far beyond the district.

The word traveled too far. Officials pressed him into corvée labor, treated the strength heaven had given him as something to be driven like an ox, and beat him at the smallest fault. He ran. A fugitive with nothing left to lose, he killed a man, and the crime carried death. But the king weighed the sentence and set it aside. A man of such strength and bearing was too rare a thing to destroy.

By then the country of the Việt had passed to An Dương Vương, king of Âu Lạc and successor of the Hùng kings. In the north, the state of Qin had swallowed its six rivals, and its ruler now styled himself Qin Shi Huang, the First Emperor, master of more land than any king before him. Qin armies began to threaten war on the south. An Dương Vương sent Ông Trọng north to the Qin court, hoping to spare his people the ruin of an invasion.

The First Emperor was delighted with the giant. He made him an officer and sent him with troops to hold Lintao, the empire's gate in the far northwest, where the Xiongnu, the horse nomads of the steppe, rode across the border to raid year after year. Ông Trọng stood on the wall. Riders who came within sight of that figure on the ramparts turned their horses and did not come back. For all the years he held the pass, no hoof crossed the frontier. The emperor prized him and gave him a princess in marriage.

But a foreign court could not keep a man of Chèm forever. When old age came, Ông Trọng asked leave to go, returned to his own village on the Red River, and died there at home.

The news crossed the mountains to Xianyang, the Qin capital. The emperor grieved for his officer, and worried more than he grieved: once the Xiongnu learned the man was dead, the raiding would begin again. So he ordered a bronze statue cast in Ông Trọng's likeness, immense and hollow, with room inside for several dozen men, and set it up outside the Sima Gate of the capital. Whenever envoys of the Xiongnu came to court, the men hidden in the statue's belly rocked it, and the great figure stirred. The envoys looked up and went cold. The commandant of Lintao was alive after all, standing guard now at the emperor's own gate. They carried the story back to the steppe, and along the whole frontier no one dared touch the border again.

The northerners called that figure the statue of Ông Trọng, and in time they gave the name to every great statue standing guard before a royal tomb. And in Chèm, the villagers raised a temple and worshipped their giant as a god: Đức Thánh Chèm, the Saint of Chèm. Every year in the fifth lunar month, the three villages by the river hold his festival, rowing out to midstream to draw water for the bathing of his statue, and the rowers' chant rolls across the water. The villagers say it is the war cry of Ông Trọng's soldiers, still sounding after two thousand years.

## Background: _Âu Lạc and the rise of Qin_

The tale dates itself precisely: from the last Hùng kings, through the reign of An Dương Vương (who took the throne in 257 BCE by the chronology of the *Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư*), to Qin Shi Huang's unification of China in 221 BCE. Within that frame, Âu Lạc is an independent kingdom in the south facing a newly unified empire in the north. The *Việt điện u linh* and the *Toàn thư* present Lý Thân's journey as one man's decision to seek office abroad; the *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* presents it as a wartime concession, a king surrendering his strongest subject to keep an army off his soil. No version describes any standing submission of Âu Lạc to Qin.

The frame does not hold up as history, and Vietnamese scholars said so early. Under the *Shiji* chronology, Meng Tian's campaign against the Xiongnu began only in 214 BCE, the emperor's thirty-third year. Lintao lies in modern Gansu, at the far end of the Chinese world from the Red River delta. The office the tale gives its hero, Metropolitan Commandant (Tư lệ Hiệu úy 司隸校尉), was created in 89 BCE, more than a century after Qin fell; the Area Commander (Đô đốc) who beats him in one version is an even later invention (Hucker, *A Dictionary of Official Titles in Imperial China*, Stanford UP 1985, pp. 451, 544). Trần Trọng Kim in the *Việt Nam sử lược* and Đào Duy Anh both placed the story among the legends. The anachronisms do not diminish the tale; they identify its authors as people looking backward, centuries after the events they describe.

## The record in _the annals_

The earliest Vietnamese text to record the story is the *Việt điện u linh tập* of Lý Tế Xuyên (1329), in the entry "Hiệu úy Uy mãnh Anh liệt Phụ tín Đại Vương" among the Spirits of Successive Eras. In Lê Hữu Mục's translation (Khai Trí 1961, p. 44) the entry opens:

> "The prince was surnamed Lý, named Ông Trọng, a man of Từ Liêm district, two trượng and three thước tall, upright and fearless in bearing beyond ordinary men."

In this version the young man serves in a district office, is flogged by an Area Commander, and swears that a man's ambition should be like the loan and phượng birds, ten thousand dặm at one beat of the wings, not a life of servitude under other men's insults. He turns to study, masters the classics and histories, enters Qin service, and rises to Metropolitan Commandant. After the unification he garrisons Lintao, and his name alone unsettles the Xiongnu. He grows old, returns home, and dies; the emperor casts the hollow bronze statue at the Sima Gate, and hidden men rock it whenever envoys pass. Lý Tế Xuyên cites his sources as the *Giao Châu ký* and the *Báo cực truyện*, both now lost.

The *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* (story 11 in the 22-story recension edited by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú, 1492-1493) tells the opening differently. The hero is "surnamed Lý, named Thân," born late in the Hùng period, violent by temperament; he kills a man, the sentence is death, and the Hùng king cannot bring himself to execute him. When Qin Shi Huang threatens invasion under An Dương Vương, the king sends Lý Thân to Qin. This version adds the emperor's gift of a princess in marriage, and continues past the hero's death into the Tang period: the protectorate official Zhao Chang 趙昌 (Triệu Xương) dreams of discussing the *Spring and Autumn Annals* and the *Zuo Commentary* with Lý Thân, seeks out his old home, and founds a shrine; later Gao Pian (Cao Biền), campaigning against Nanzhao, credits the spirit with aid in battle and rebuilds the shrine with a carved wooden image, calling it the temple of Lý Hiệu Úy.

The *Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư* (1479) admits the story into the dynastic history, but on Ngô Sĩ Liên's terms: as an appendix (phụ chép) at the end of the An Dương Vương chronicle in the Outer Annals, outside the year-by-year record. Its content follows the *Việt điện u linh* line: the beating, the self-made career in Qin, Lintao, death at home, the statue "with several dozen men inside its belly, secretly making it move," and the Xiongnu convinced the commandant still lived. The *Khâm định Việt sử thông giám cương mục* (Tiền biên I, 9) copies the *Toàn thư* nearly verbatim and adds an editorial note drawing on Cao Hùng Trưng's *An Nam chí nguyên*. The placement is itself a verdict of sorts: the historians kept the story because it mattered, and kept it out of the chronology because they could not vouch for it.

The deepest split concerns why he goes north. One tradition gives a Confucian self-made man who answers humiliation with study; one gives a king's concession under threat of invasion; the folk version gives a conscript with nowhere left to run. Three tellings, three kinds of teller: literati who prized self-cultivation, compilers who wanted the drama of states, and villagers who knew corvée labor from experience.

One philological detail is worth keeping. The *Cương mục* notes that the *Đại Thanh nhất thống chí* and Lu Bosheng's *Quảng dư ký* both call him **Nguyễn Ông Trọng** (阮翁仲). This preserves a Vietnamese scar: the Trần court ordered the Lý clan and Lý-surnamed commoners to change their surname to Nguyễn, as Lê Tắc's *An Nam chí lược* (1335) records. Chinese compilations received the story through post-Trần texts and kept the taboo-altered name.

## Ông Trọng 翁仲 in _Chinese sources_

Chinese texts know an Ông Trọng, but a different one. The earliest witness is Gao You 高誘 (early 3rd century CE) in his commentary on the *Huainanzi*, Fanlun chapter (ch. 13): in 221 BCE, the year of unification, a giant five zhang tall appeared at Lintao, his footprints six chi long; the court cast bronze men modeled on the apparition and named them Wengzhong (Ông Trọng) and Junhe (Quân Hà 君何). The *Shiji*, Basic Annals of Qin Shi Huang (ch. 6), records the casting of "twelve metal men" (金人十二) in that year; Sima Zhen's Tang-era *Suoyin* commentary adds that each weighed a thousand dan, sat two zhang high, and "was called Wengzhong." The *Songshu* Treatise on the Five Phases carries the name forward: in 237 CE Emperor Ming of Wei cast two more colossi, also called Wengzhong.

The decisive point: nowhere in this material is there a man from Giao Chỉ named Lý Thân. In the Chinese record, Wengzhong is a name given to statues, tied to the Lintao apparition, attached to no foreign officer. From a proper name it became a common noun for tomb guardians: the stone files along the spirit road of the Tang Qianling mausoleum (c. 700 CE) are called *thạch ông trọng* (石翁仲), and the civil and military statues before the Lê tombs and the Nguyễn tombs at Huế belong to the same line. The word reached Vietnam through Chinese funerary culture, independently of the Chèm legend; the Chèm legend, on the reading examined below, was then built out of that ready-made vocabulary.

Chinese historians even tracked the twelve colossi to their end. Dong Zhuo melted ten for coinage at the end of the Han; the last two survived being moved from capital to capital until Fu Jian of the Former Qin melted them in the fourth century. The statue of the Vietnamese tale, if it ever stood, left no trace outside the texts.

## The _legend_ layer

The miraculous portion of the dossier is thicker than the biographical one, and the texts do not hide it.

**The hollow statue.** The device of a bronze shell with several dozen men inside, rocking it to frighten envoys, appears in no Chinese source on the twelve colossi. It is the Vietnamese storytellers' invention, and the heart of the tale: a memorial turned into an instrument of border defense, a death converted into a new kind of power.

**Zhao Chang's dream.** Zhao Chang served two terms as the Tang protectorate's chief official in Giao Châu (791-802 and 804-806). The dream of discussing the classics with Lý Thân, followed by the founding of a shrine, and Gao Pian's renovation after his Nanzhao campaigns in the 860s, follow a pattern that recurs throughout the *Việt điện u linh*: a local spirit manifests to an occupying official and receives a cult. That pattern is precisely where the constructed-tradition argument plants its flag.

**The princess Bạch Tĩnh Cung.** Đình Chèm enthrones a consort beside the god, held by tradition to be a daughter of Qin Shi Huang. The marriage appears only in the *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* and in the cult; no Chinese source records a Qin princess given to any southern officer, and the title Marquis Phụ Tín is equally without a documentary basis. It is a rank-raising marriage motif, kin to the other divine marriages of Vietnamese legend.

**The giải-slaying.** The folk version collected by Nguyễn Đổng Chi in the *Kho tàng truyện cổ tích Việt Nam* ("Lý Ông Trọng, or the Origin of the Saint of Chèm") adds an entire chapter absent from every Hán Nôm text above: the young giant kills the water monster, rescues his mother, bails a reach of the river dry, and stands two trượng six thước rather than two trượng three. Nguyễn Đổng Chi's variant notes are the standard reference for this layer.

Separating the layers does not impoverish the figure. It shows each age hiring him for different work: the Tang protectorate wanted a spirit who read the classics, the Trần court wanted a Great King who defended the realm, and the villages wanted a giant of their own whom the greatest empire of the age had needed and feared.

## The _scholarly_ debate: a man or a made tradition?

The central question is whether any historical person stands behind the cult, and if not, who assembled him.

**The constructed-tradition reading.** Liam C. Kelley ("Constructing Local Narratives: Spirits, Dreams, and Prophecies in the Medieval Red River Delta," in *China's Encounters on the South and Southwest*, Brill 2015, pp. 78-105; "The Real Ông Trọng," leminhkhai.blog, June 5, 2016) argues that the story was composed under the Tang, by Zhao Chang or by Zeng Gun, author of the lost *Giao Châu ký* (c. 866-880), from two pre-existing pieces: (i) the Chinese use of "wengzhong" for the Qin colossi, already in Gao You's commentary and later in the *Suoyin*; and (ii) a real late-Han official in Giao Chỉ surnamed Yao 姚, whose courtesy name was Wengzhong, recorded in Ou Daren's *Bách Việt tiên hiền chí* (16th century) from earlier sources. The composition served a standard Tang administrative technique: absorbing a local spirit into a narrative of loyalty to the court. The anachronistic offices are the textual fingerprint of late authorship. Tạ Chí Đại Trường's treatment of Vietnamese spirit cults in *Thần, Người và Đất Việt* belongs to the same current, and Trần Trọng Kim and Đào Duy Anh had already, without the "invented tradition" framework, excluded the tale from usable history.

**The folk-memory reading.** Nguyễn Đổng Chi and Đinh Gia Khánh (editor of the 1960 *Lĩnh Nam chích quái*) read Lý Ông Trọng as a legendary hero of the Vietnamese people: a commoner whose talent outmeasured the north's own generals, remembered because the memory answered something real in the community that kept it. Lê Hữu Mục's preface to the 1960 translation reads the tale as an expression of the will to rise. Trần Quốc Vượng treated the deity system of the Red River villages, Đức Thánh Chèm among them, as folk cultural sediment older than any of the surviving texts. Nguyễn Hòa (vanhoanghean.com.vn) attacks Kelley's method directly, arguing that collective memory cannot be adjudicated by textual criticism alone.

**A middle position.** Trần Trọng Dương (Institute of Hán Nôm Studies), in interviews and articles from 2016 to 2024, proposes an interdisciplinary reading: collate the texts against the village hagiographies of Ninh Sơn and Trạo Thôn and against archaeology. He accepts that the story as we have it is a medieval product while leaving open an older devotional core beneath it.

**What remains open.** The two sides are, in large part, answering different questions. Kelley's evidence is textual: no surviving source carries the story earlier than the Tang, and the anachronistic titles date the composition; no earlier text has been produced to counter this. The folk-memory school's evidence is devotional: a dense and ancient cult, festivals, hagiographies in several villages, and a giant who stands beside Thánh Gióng in the popular imagination, none of which philology can fully measure. Both sides accept that the funerary term "wengzhong" is older than, and independent of, the Vietnamese tale, and that the cult of Đức Thánh Chèm has been alive for at least twelve centuries. Whether a flesh-and-blood man of Chèm ever stood on a wall at Lintao, no source now extant can say.

## Worship and _relics_

The center of the cult is **đình Chèm**, also called đền Lý Hiệu Úy or Thụy Hương từ, in Thụy Phương ward, Bắc Từ Liêm district, Hà Nội, on the south bank of the Red River. It enshrines Đức Thánh Chèm, under the epithet Hy Khang Thiên Vương, together with his consort Bạch Tĩnh Cung. The standing architecture is eighteenth-century in style, on the "nội công ngoại quốc" plan, and faces north toward the river, an orientation few Vietnamese communal halls share. The hall keeps ten wooden statues carved in 1888 (the god and his consort stand over three meters), four stone stelae (one from the Lê Cảnh Hưng era, three Nguyễn), two Nguyễn-era bronze bells, and a set of bronze rain gutters cast between 1748 and 1824, a category of object almost no other đình possesses.

In 1902-1903, with the riverbank eroding, the master builder Vương Văn Địch of Văn Trì village and his guild raised the entire hall 2.4 meters using levers, timber cribbing, and hand labor; a commemorative stele composed by the doctoral laureate Nghiêm Xuân Quảng records the feat. The đình was ranked a national relic in 1990 and a **Special National Relic** by decision of the Prime Minister on December 25, 2017.

The **đình Chèm festival** runs from the 14th to the 16th of the fifth lunar month, staged jointly by three allied villages: Chèm (Thụy Phương), Hoàng (Hoàng Xá), and Mạc (Mạc Xá). Its core rites are the water procession on the Red River, the procession of texts, and the mộc dục bathing of the statues; the boat crews row to a chant of "ù óc," said to echo the war cries of Ông Trọng's troops. The fair includes chè kho cooking contests, boat racing, wrestling, and bird releases. Village law keeps several prohibitions: offerings are vegetarian only; yellow clothing is barred inside the đình, yellow being the color of the robe the emperor gave the Saint; and children are not given the names Trọng or Thân. The festival entered the National Intangible Cultural Heritage list by Decision 2067/QĐ-BVHTTDL of June 13, 2016. A Hà Nội folk verse has ranked it for generations: "First the fair of Cổ Loa, second the fair of Gióng, third the fair of Chèm."

Beyond Chèm, the đình of **Ninh Sơn** (Chúc Sơn, Chương Mỹ) worships him as tutelary deity; its hagiography names it as the place where his parents prayed for a child and where he died. The đình of **Trạo Thôn** holds a hagiography making him a commander under the last Hùng king. In the popular pantheon of the Hà Nội region he stands among the Four Pillar Spirits, "Hương, Bổng, Đổng, Đằng," where "Hương" is Thụy Hương's Lý Thân, ranked after Thánh Tản and Thánh Gióng.

## The figure through _the ages_

Each regime left its mark on him. Under the Tang protectorate he is a dream-spirit who discusses the classics with the occupying governor. The Trần court invested him three times, in the two Trùng Hưng rounds and in Hưng Long 21, as Uy Mãnh Anh Liệt Phụ Tín Đại Vương (威猛英烈輔信大王); the same dynasty's naming taboo turned him into Nguyễn Ông Trọng in later Chinese compilations. The Lê ranked him a Spirit of the Highest Order. The Nguyễn issued three patents of investiture, still kept at the đình, under the epithet Hy Khang Thiên Vương (希康天王).

A couplet at đình Chèm holds his double position in a single breath: "In this Việt sanctuary, a spirit of the highest rank; at that Qin pass, a general of China." The author does not flinch from the Qin commission; the two lines sit side by side, and the reader is left to notice which one is present tense. In the popular imagination he is the giant who walks beside Thánh Gióng; some folklore researchers have proposed that "Ông Trọng" and "Ông Gióng" are phonetic variants of one "giant" word, a hypothesis that remains unverified. In modern art, Nguyễn Văn Tỵ's lacquer painting *Hội đình Chèm* hangs in the Vietnam Fine Arts Museum.

## Living _legacy_

His clearest legacy is lexical. "Ông trọng" is one of the very few proper names from Vietnamese legend to become a common noun across the region, naming the stone and bronze guardians of royal tombs from Tang Qianling to the Nguyễn mausolea at Huế. A visitor standing before the rows of mandarin statues at Huế is, usually without knowing it, calling them by the name of a man from Chèm; making that connection is exactly what the tale was built to do.

At Chèm itself the legacy is a working system, not a monument: the hall above the river, the three-village festival of the fifth month, the pots of chè kho, the "ù óc" chant over the water, and naming taboos that have held for something like a millennium. The đình is a Special National Relic; the festival is national intangible heritage. A 2022 repair campaign drew public criticism when the old banyan before the hall was felled and the steps replaced; the Department of Cultural Heritage ordered an inspection by letter of March 25, 2022.

## Notes _for use_

**Historicity.** No source contemporary with the third century BCE mentions Lý Thân. The documentary record begins under the Tang, and the Vietnamese annals carry the story only as an appendix. The offices named in the tale (Metropolitan Commandant, Area Commander) postdate the Qin. Lý Ông Trọng is solid ground for studying legend and collective memory; he is not evidence for Âu Lạc-Qin relations.

**The age of đình Chèm.** Press figures of "over 2,000 years" or "2,300 years" measure the legend by its own internal dating, not the building. The standing structure is eighteenth-century in style, repaired repeatedly from 1631 onward.

**The poem attributed to Nguyễn Du.** Popular sources credit Nguyễn Du or Cao Bá Quát with a poem on Lý Ông Trọng. The *Bắc hành tạp lục* contains no such title; the poem in question is recorded in the *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* and is most likely anonymous. Until an attribution is verified, quote it without an author.

**The princess and the marquisate.** The marriage to a daughter of Qin Shi Huang and the title Marquis Phụ Tín exist only in the *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* and the Chèm cult; no Chinese source knows either. Flag them as legend when citing.

**Conflicting measurements.** The Hán Nôm texts give his height as two trượng three thước; the folk version gives two trượng six; the Chinese *Ciyuan* (1915) gives one trượng three, probably a corruption transmitted through the *Đại Thanh nhất thống chí*. State which tradition a figure comes from.

**Hagiographies are hagiography.** The thần tích of Ninh Sơn and Trạo Thôn are valuable for the history of the cult and worthless as biographical chronology.

**A standing misattribution.** Several websites, Thi viện among them, credit the *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* to Nguyễn Dữ. It was compiled by Trần Thế Pháp and redacted by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú; Nguyễn Dữ wrote the *Truyền kỳ mạn lục*.
