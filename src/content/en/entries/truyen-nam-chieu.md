## The Story

In the winter of 111 BCE, Phiên Ngung, the capital of Nam Việt, burned. Han troops entered the walls. Nam Việt was the state that Triệu Đà, a general out of the north, had raised in Lĩnh Nam and ruled for close to a century; now the Han destroyed it. The chancellor Lữ Gia was taken. Vệ Dương Vương Kiến Đức, the last king of the Triệu line, was taken with him. The descendants of the Triệu clan scattered each their own way.

Some fled south by sea. They stopped at Thần Phù and the Hoành Sơn range, where the mountains push out to the water, empty country thin with people, a place the northern officials rarely reached. There they built boats and lived off the sea. Their kingdom gone, their royal name gone, their resentment unquieted, they turned their prows back toward the shore and raided: they struck the outposts the Han had newly planted along the coast, killed officials, took what they needed, and pulled back to open water. The coast people feared them and called them the "Nam Triệu," the Triệu clan of the south. Passed from mouth to mouth over the years, the two words Nam Triệu slid into Nam Chiếu. The name stuck. People forgot these had once been a royal house and remembered only a band of sea raiders.

Generation followed generation. The northern dynasties took turns governing Giao Châu, but the Nam Chiếu stayed out of reach among the high mountains and the wide sea. One of them, who also claimed descent from Emperor Triệu Vũ, gathered his people, raised a stronghold, and named himself lord of a region; when northern armies came against him he fought from cover, mountain and forest at his back, and held out for years. The grievance from the day the kingdom fell passed down and did not go out.

Then came the Tang, in the Hàm Thông reign. By now the Nam Chiếu had become a great menace. They poured into Giao Châu, broke the walls, killed the Tang commissioner, and held the land of Tống Bình. For years the whole province lay under Nam Chiếu boots, the people taken and killed past counting. The Tang court was far off and sent generals more than once without settling the matter.

At last the Tang sent Cao Biền. Cao Biền was a hard general, and he led his troops down into the south. He broke the local "tribesmen" who had guided the invaders, then struck the Nam Chiếu main force. In the decisive battle his men killed the Nam Chiếu commander Đoàn Tù Thiên and took more than thirty thousand heads. The Nam Chiếu broke. Cao Biền rebuilt the citadel that had been battered through the years of war, widening it into a great wall by the river, and called it Đại La. On that ground, centuries later, the capital of an independent Vietnamese state would rise.

The name Nam Chiếu settled into the books as the enemy that had been put down. But in the books of the southern people, that enemy was not quite an outsider. The tale writes that they were descendants of Triệu Đà, turned to banditry only because their kingdom fell. Told that way, the enemy turns out to be family gone astray.

## The Tale in _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_

Nam Chiếu truyện (南詔傳) stands in book 2 of the _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_, between Man Nương truyện and Tô Lịch giang truyện in the Chinese table of contents ([the LNCQ text, ctext.org](https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=en&chapter=414213)); in the 22-tale recension of Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú it is tale 18.

The tale opens with a flat statement of descent: "南詔者，趙武帝佗之後也" (the Nam Chiếu are descendants of Emperor Triệu Vũ, Đà). From there it runs downhill: under Han Wudi, the Nam Việt chancellor Lữ Gia (呂嘉) refuses submission and kills Han envoys; Han Wudi sends Lộ Bác Đức (路博德) and Dương Bộc (楊僕) to destroy Nam Việt and capture Vệ Dương Vương Kiến Đức and Lữ Gia. The Triệu descendants scatter, gather at Thần Phù and Hoành Sơn, build boats to raid the coastal people and kill Han officials; the people call them "Nam Triệu" (南趙), later slurred into "Nam Chiếu" (南詔) ([the LNCQ text, ctext.org](https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=en&chapter=414213)).

The middle of the tale carries the story across later reigns, with a leader who also claims the Triệu line raising a stronghold and holding out against northern armies for years. The end shifts fully into Tang history: in the Hàm Thông (咸通) reign Giao Châu falls to the Nam Chiếu, then Cao Biền (高駢) is appointed, breaks them, kills the general Đoàn Tù Thiên (段酋遷), and takes more than thirty thousand heads. The named figures in the middle stretch (the leader Triệu Ông Lý, the country of "Tây Bà Dạ" 西婆夜) can at present be located only in non-scholarly transcriptions and translations, not yet checked against a critical edition; see Textual History and Notes on Use.

## Three Time Layers: _do not read the tale as history_

The tale misleads because it braids three widely separated dates into one thread. Pull them apart:

(i) **The textual layer.** The _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ is by tradition compiled by Trần Thế Pháp in the late 14th century, then edited by Vũ Quỳnh in 1492 and given a separate recension by Kiều Phú in 1493; every surviving copy descends from those two editorial versions ([Jenkin Leung, honors thesis, UC Berkeley, 2022, synthesizing Kelley and Taylor](https://escholarship.org/content/qt2p3293k8/qt2p3293k8_noSplash_488eb599b3f80885c04a2b9dde3d99e0.pdf)). This is when the tale was written in the form we read.

(ii) **The layer the tale claims.** The tale sets itself across the span from the fall of Nam Việt (111 BCE) to the Nam Chiếu wars under the Tang (9th century): nearly a thousand years, stitched together by a single line of descent.

(iii) **The historical layer.** The Nam Chiếu wars against An Nam and Cao Biền's campaign are real events, recorded in both Vietnamese and Tang annals (see below). But the genealogy linking the Triệu clan to the Nam Chiếu appears in no historical source at all.

Read (ii) as a claim the tale makes, not as history. The tale touches real history only at its end, under the Tang; the whole opening genealogy is the writer's construction.

## The Historical Layer: _the Nam Chiếu wars and Cao Biền, 858–866_

After the invented genealogy comes a brutal, real event. According to [Vi An Lu (*Journal of the Royal Asiatic Society* 35, no. 3, 2025, pp. 657–677)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-the-royal-asiatic-society/article/annan-protectorate-in-northern-vietnam-during-the-tang-period-679907/0330A3F32086B5B1AEFC262A54FBF023), drawing on the _Zizhi Tongjian_, the major conflict dates from Dazhong 12 (858), with earlier raids into the Giao Châu core in 846 and 854. Tống Bình fell and the commissioner Cai Xi died in battle in the first month of 863.

The counterattack belongs to Cao Biền. Again from [Vi An Lu (JRAS 35/3, 2025, pp. 657–677)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-the-royal-asiatic-society/article/annan-protectorate-in-northern-vietnam-during-the-tang-period-679907/0330A3F32086B5B1AEFC262A54FBF023): Cao Biền was appointed in the seventh month of 864 but did not march until the seventh month of 865, with 5,000 troops, and won a large battle against 50,000 Nam Chiếu troops at Phong Châu that ninth month. The human cost was heavy: across the two major invasions, roughly 150,000 people were captured or killed. Those who bore it were the people of An Nam, direct ancestors of the Vietnamese, not one of two parties "claiming" a territory in the modern idiom.

The decisive battle falls in 866. The [New Book of Tang (新唐書), ctext.org](https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=gb&res=182378) records that in Xiantong 7 (866) Cao Biền defeated the Nam Chiếu, killed Đoàn Tù Thiên (段酋遷) and took more than thirty thousand heads, and was then made military governor. After the victory Cao Biền rebuilt the citadel into Đại La, the great wall by the Tô Lịch river, the old ground of later Hanoi ([Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư, Ngô Sĩ Liên, 1479, Institute of History](https://viensuhoc.vass.gov.vn/cong-trinh-tieu-bieu/dai-viet-su-ky-toan-thu-434956)). This is the historical layer the tale borrows to close its narrative.

## Cross-Checking the _Sources_

The key difference: the annals treat the Nam Chiếu as a foreign power out of Yunnan, while the LNCQ makes them descendants of Triệu Đà, "family" of Giao Châu. This is where the legendary layer parts from the historical one.

| Source | Who the Nam Chiếu are | Cao Biền and the outcome |
|---|---|---|
| _Nam Chiếu truyện_ (LNCQ) | Exiled descendants of Triệu Đà, sea raiders, name slurred from "Nam Triệu" | Cao Biền defeats them, kills Đoàn Tù Thiên, 30,000-plus heads |
| _New Book of Tang_ | A southern kingdom outside Tang territory (Yunnan) | Xiantong 7 (866) defeats Nam Chiếu, kills 段酋遷, over 30,000 heads, made governor |
| _Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư_ | Foreign invaders of Giao Châu | Cao Biền rebuilds the Đại La citadel after the war |
| Vi An Lu (JRAS 2025) | A Yunnan kingdom; ~150,000 captured/killed over two invasions | Marched 865, won at Phong Châu, settled by 866 |

The Nam Chiếu commander's name carries variant graphs across the sources: 段酋遷, 叚酋遷, 酋遷 (Đoàn Tù Thiên). The four sources agree on the frame of the Tang-era events; only the LNCQ adds the Triệu genealogy, which no historical source has.

## Who the Nam Chiếu _Really_ Were

The Nam Chiếu (南詔) kingdom lay in the Erhai basin of Yunnan. Its majority population was the Bai (白蠻), but the ruling stratum spoke a variety of Yi within the Tibeto-Burman family, close to Burmese, not a Tai state as some older guesses supposed ([Charles Backus, *The Nan-chao Kingdom and T'ang China's Southwestern Frontier*, Cambridge University Press, 1981](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies/article/abs/nanchao-kingdom-and-tang-chinas-southwestern-frontier-by-charles-backus-cambridge-cambridge-university-press-cambridge-studies-in-chinese-history-literature-and-institutions-1981-xii-224-pp-maps-tables-appendix-glossary-bibliography-index-36/F421435DE67043BBEFEE36DE9FD2CEC2)). The graph "詔" (chiếu) in the local tongue means "king" or "chief": among the six "chiếu" of the Erhai region, the southernmost, the Mengshe tribe, was called the "southern chief" (南詔), a name that later covered the kingdom that unified all six.

Put another way, the "Nam Chiếu barbarians" of the tale and the Nam Chiếu kingdom of history share a name but not a root. In history, 南詔 meant "southern king" in the language of Yunnan; in the tale, 南詔 is explained as a corruption of 南趙, "the Triệu clan of the south." The tale takes a real name and fits it with a fabricated etymology. That move is the whole engine of the tale.

Further back in the kingdom's history: the Erhai chiefdoms were unified by Piluoge (皮邏閣) in 738, and the Nam Chiếu lasted until 902, when Zheng Maisi (鄭買嗣) seized the throne and founded the Dachanghe (大長和) state. This lies outside the tale, noted only to show that the Nam Chiếu was a Yunnan state with a history of its own, unconnected to the Triệu line.

## Folk Etymology and the Tale's _Political Function_

The "slurred name" move (Nam Triệu 南趙 into Nam Chiếu 南詔) is a classic LNCQ folk etymology: take a real name and give it a native root by splitting the graphs. In the same manner, many tales in the collection explain a place name or an object by a story cut to fit.

The tale's political function lies in how it absorbs a foreign enemy into the Vietnamese genealogy. By turning the Nam Chiếu into descendants of the Triệu who resisted the North, the writer turns a devastating invasion into a chapter in the southern line of resistance: the invaders become "family" who lost their kingdom and drifted into banditry. [Jenkin Leung (honors thesis, UC Berkeley, 2022)](https://escholarship.org/content/qt2p3293k8/qt2p3293k8_noSplash_488eb599b3f80885c04a2b9dde3d99e0.pdf) reads the LNCQ as draping a "Chinese façade" over indigenous and frontier material to reconcile it with the Neo-Confucian worldview of fifteenth-century scholars. [Liam C. Kelley (*Journal of Vietnamese Studies* 7, no. 2, 2012, pp. 87–130)](https://doi.org/10.1525/vs.2012.7.2.87) places the whole collection under "invented tradition": much of the content is the creation of a medieval elite writing in literary Chinese, not pure oral folklore.

The scope of this argument needs stating plainly. That the Triệu-to-Nam Chiếu genealogy is fiction does not touch what the tale asserts about the south: that it is a distinct realm, with its own royal line, with its own memory of resistance against the North. The tale invents a bloodline, but what that bloodline serves, an awareness of an independent south outside the Chinese sphere, is the awareness the whole LNCQ carries.

## The _Triệu Đà_ Problem

By making Triệu Đà the ancestor, the tale runs straight into a point on which Vietnamese historiography is split. Older historiography, following the _Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư_, records the Triệu as a legitimate Vietnamese dynasty succeeding An Dương Vương. Modern historiography in the Democratic Republic of Vietnam tradition excludes the Triệu, treating Triệu Đà as a northern conqueror and counting the Triệu period as the start of Chinese domination rather than a Vietnamese reign.

Nam Chiếu truyện lands squarely on the older view: it treats the Triệu as a southern royal house worth claiming as an ancestor, and it makes the Triệu descendants' "resistance to the North" the reason to keep the tale. The LNCQ commentary states that the story of the Triệu descendants resisting the North cannot be left out. This entry presents both positions on the Triệu without adjudicating: it is an open historiographical dispute, distinct from claims of sovereignty and independence, which are asserted directly. What is worth recording is that the tale itself picks a side, and that choice is evidence for how a fifteenth-century writer imagined the Triệu.

## Textual History: _recensions and variants_

The _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ (嶺南摭怪, "selected strange tales of the Lĩnh Nam land") was never block-printed in the medieval period; it survives through varying manuscript copies. The National Library of Vietnam holds NLVNPF-0651 / R.1618 (a Hồng Đức-era manuscript whose contents list 38 tales) and NLVNPF-0652 / R.6, both attributed to Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú ([National Library of Vietnam / Nôm Foundation](https://lib.nomfoundation.org/collection/1/volume/819/)). The number of tales varies by recension (22 in the Vũ Quỳnh / Kiều Phú version; 38 in one, up to 43 in another); Nam Chiếu truyện belongs to the core layer in book 2, not to a later supplement.

The most recent critical work is [Nguyễn Thị Oanh, *Lĩnh Nam chích quái – Study, Annotated Translation, Chinese Text* (NXB Khoa học Xã hội, 2024, 720 pp.)](https://nxbkhxh.vass.gov.vn/tin-tuc-su-kien/linh-nam-chich-quai-hanh-trinh-gan-25-nam-phuc-dung-mot-di-san-van-hoa-561342), collating 22 manuscripts held in Hanoi. For Nam Chiếu truyện in particular, the details in the middle stretch (the leader Triệu Ông Lý; the country Tây Bà Dạ 西婆夜; which northern dynasty sends the army) and some variants that identify the leader with Lý Bí can at present be located only in non-scholarly transcriptions and translations. Before citing those details specifically, they should be checked against Nguyễn Thị Oanh (2024) or the Đinh Gia Khánh edition (1960).

The date of composition is also disputed: Kelley and many international scholars place most of the content in the late fifteenth century, while others reject the narrower specific dates. What remains open is the exact date of each textual layer; what all sides grant is that the LNCQ passed through heavy editing by early-Lê scholars.

## Living _Legacy_

Nam Chiếu truyện has no relics, no cult, and is not a widely told story. It survives mainly in two places. First, as a link in the LNCQ, reprinted in translations and critical studies. Second, as a reference point in the legendary layer around Cao Biền: several tales of spirits aiding Cao Biền (Cao Lỗ, Lý Ông Trọng appearing to him) use the "putting down of the Nam Chiếu" as their backdrop without retelling the Triệu genealogy. Through those tales, the Nam Chiếu wars settle in popular memory as a disaster that the spirits of the south had to help put down.

For a reader today, the value of the tale lies not in historical information (its plot is wrong on that count) but in what it shows: how a fifteenth-century scholar rewrote a foreign invasion into a chapter of southern history.

## Notes on _Use_

**The Triệu-to-Nam Chiếu genealogy is fiction.** The historical Nam Chiếu was a Tibeto-Burman-led kingdom in Yunnan with no blood tie to the Triệu clan. Do not use the tale as evidence for the ethnic origin of the Nam Chiếu people.

**Three time layers.** Do not present the span the tale claims (Triệu Đà to the Tang) as a continuous historical record. Only the end, the Nam Chiếu wars and Cao Biền under the Tang, is real.

**The middle details are not fixed.** Triệu Ông Lý, the country Tây Bà Dạ (西婆夜), the identification of the leader with Lý Bí, and which northern dynasty marches: these can at present be located only in non-scholarly sources; check a critical edition before citing.

**Nam Chiếu (南詔) as a slur of Nam Triệu (南趙) is the tale's etymology, not history's.** In history, 南詔 means "southern king" in the language of Yunnan.

**The Triệu question.** Vietnamese historiography is split on whether the Triệu were a Vietnamese dynasty or the start of Chinese domination. The tale takes the older side; this entry presents both and does not adjudicate.

**Cao Biền.** He was a Tang general, not a Vietnamese national hero. In the LNCQ and Việt điện u linh he is also the geomancy-suppressing figure of other tales (Tô Lịch giang truyện, Tản Viên); do not mix that suppression layer into Nam Chiếu truyện.

**Misattributed authorship.** Some websites (Thi viện in particular) attribute the LNCQ to Nguyễn Dữ. This is wrong: Nguyễn Dữ wrote the _Truyền kỳ mạn lục_. The LNCQ is linked to Trần Thế Pháp (by tradition) and Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú (editors).
