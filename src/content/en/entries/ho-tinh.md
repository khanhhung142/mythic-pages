## The Story

The land where the city of Thăng Long would one day rise was called Long Biên in the old days. In high antiquity no one lived there. There was only the river winding through marshland, and empty mounds of earth. West of that land stood a small rocky hill, its foot resting on the bank of the Tô Lịch River. Under the hill was a cave. In the cave lived a white fox with nine tails. It was more than a thousand years old.

A fox that lives that long becomes a spirit. This one could take a thousand shapes: now a man, now a ghost, walking among people who never knew what stood beside them. It passed through markets and river landings, stood in crowds, and no one saw it for what it was. No one knew that under the rocky hill in the west something was living, and waiting.

In those days, at the foot of Mount Tản Viên, the sacred mountain in the west where a powerful spirit dwelt, people built their houses of timber and thatch. The mountain spirit cared for them. He taught them to plow, to weave, and to sew white cloth for their clothes. For this they were called the Bạch y man, the White-Clothed people.

The nine-tailed fox learned of them. It took the shape of a man in white and slipped in among the villagers. When they sang, it sang with them. Its voice was sweet, its melodies true, its white clothes the same as everyone's. No one suspected the stranger singing beside them at the fire.

Then people began to vanish. A girl followed a voice out of the village under the rising moon and did not come back. A young man walked toward the rocky hill and did not come back. Whomever the fox charmed, it carried off and hid in the cave beneath the hill. One by one the sons and daughters of the White-Clothed people disappeared into the dark of that cave mouth. The villagers went out with torches, calling across the marshes until their voices gave out, and only the wind answered. They suffered bitterly, and they did not even know what was preying on them, let alone whom to call for help.

Lạc Long Quân, the lord of dragon blood who watched over the people of the southern land, knew of their suffering. A demon that has kept its den for a thousand years, deep in thick rock, is hard to fight on dry ground. But the dragon lord had one thing the fox had not reckoned with: water.

He sent the six legions of the Water Palace to raise the waters against the cave. The river surged and roared, drove into the cave mouth, and pried the rock apart seam by seam. The stone broke. The cave flooded. The nine-tailed fox fled its den. The troops of the Water Palace hunted it down, tore the lair open, caught the fox, and devoured it. A thousand years of cultivated power ended in one rush of water.

Where the cave had been smashed, the ground fell away into a deep pit, and water poured in until it became a marsh. Later generations called it the Fox Corpse Marsh. That marsh is today Hồ Tây, West Lake, in the heart of Hà Nội.

People raised a shrine beside the water to press down what remained of the demon's aura; it stands today as Kim Ngưu pagoda. On the western shore, where the fields lay flat, farmers plowed land they called Fox Grotto. Where the ground rose dry, they built the village called Fox Hamlet. And the old foxhole itself they called the hamlet of Lỗ Khước. The fox has been dead for thousands of years, but around that wide water, the names of the land still keep its trace.

## The Plot According to _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_

Hồ Tinh truyện (狐精傳) is the third story of fascicle I, standing between Ngư Tinh and Mộc Tinh in the 22-story recension edited by Vũ Quỳnh in 1492. Vũ Quỳnh's preface names it directly: the tales of Ngư Tinh and Hồ Tinh, he writes, show the extermination of demons and the unforgettable grace of the Dragon Lord. The story was therefore certainly present in the 1492 text.

The Hán original (ms. A.1200, published with an English translation by Liam C. Kelley in 2010) fixes several details worth keeping precise:

- The demon is a **white nine-tailed fox** (白狐九尾), aged **over a thousand years** (壽千餘年).
- It lives in a cave under a small rocky hill west of the city, east of which lies the Tô Lịch River (東枕蘇瀝江).
- It **turns into a man in white clothes** (化為白衣人) to mingle with the **Bạch y man** (白衣蠻) below Mount Tản Viên, singing with them and luring young men and women into its cave.
- Lạc Long Quân **dispatches the six legions of the Water Palace** (遣水府六部眾) to flood the cave; the fox is caught and eaten.
- The broken den becomes a deep abyss called the **Fox Corpse Marsh** (狐屍潭), which the text glosses as "today's West Lake" (即今之西湖也).
- A shrine is built to suppress the lingering demonic influence, glossed in the text as "now the Kim Ngưu pagoda" (今金牛寺). The toponyms Fox Grotto (狐洞), Fox Hamlet (狐村), and Lỗ Khước are all tied by the tale to the fox's remains.

One point matters for accuracy: in the original, Lạc Long Quân **does not fight in person**. He gives the order; the Water Palace troops do the rest. The sword duel familiar to modern readers is a later oral addition (see Variants below).

## In the Official Histories: _No_

Hồ Tinh, and the three-demon cycle with it, is absent from every official history. Three texts, three different modes of exclusion:

**Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư** (Ngô Sĩ Liên, 1479): the Hồng Bàng annals (Ngoại kỷ vol. I, fols. 1a-5b) record only the genealogy of Kinh Dương Vương, the marriage of Lạc Long Quân and Âu Cơ, the hundred eggs, and the founding of Văn Lang. The single supernatural detail involving water creatures is the custom of tattooing the body so that river serpents would not attack: a custom, not a battle. Ngô Sĩ Liên states that he consulted the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_, which means he read the fox tale and deliberately left it out.

**Khâm Định Việt Sử Thông Giám Cương Mục** (1856-1884): goes further and rejects the entire Kinh Dương Vương / Lạc Long Quân stratum. The court historians memorialized that these names belong to a primeval age where the author "made something out of nothing," and could not be trusted. Emperor Tự Đức endorsed the verdict, called the material fabulous tales of "ox demons and serpent gods" without standard, and demoted the whole layer to an annotation under the Hùng kings.

**Việt Điện U Linh Tập** (Lý Tế Xuyên, c. 1329): exclusion by editorial principle. Its preface admits only upright and perspicacious beings as spirits, expressly refusing the licentious, the demonic, and the ghostly. Hồ Tinh is a demon by definition, so it has no place there.

The methodological point: Hồ Tinh belongs purely to the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ textual line. The boundary between the tale tradition and the official annals is sharp here, and it was the traditional historians themselves who drew it.

## Geography: _West Lake and Its Seven Names_

Hồ Tây covers about 500 hectares with a 17 km shoreline, the largest lake inside Hà Nội. It has carried at least seven names, and the oldest, by legend, is the one the fox left behind:

| Name | Hán | Period | Note |
|------|-----|--------|------|
| Fox Corpse Marsh (Đầm Xác Cáo) | 狐屍潭 | High antiquity (per LNCQ) | Etiological name from the fox tale |
| Lãng Bạc | 浪泊 | 1st c. | "Breaking waves," era of the Trưng Sisters |
| Dâm Đàm | 霪潭 | Lý - Trần | "Misty marsh," after the lake's morning fogs |
| Tây Hồ / Hồ Tây | 西湖 | From 1573 | Renamed to avoid the taboo name of Emperor Lê Thế Tông (personal name Duy Đàm) |
| Đoài Hồ | (none) | 1657-1682 | Lord Trịnh Tạc tabooed the word "Tây" |
| Kim Ngưu | 金牛 | In parallel | "Golden Buffalo," from a separate bell legend |
| Diêm Hồ / Liêm Đàm | (none) | Variants | Rare |

Bùi Văn Nguyên (_Cảnh trí Hồ Tây_, 1978) documents the 1573 taboo renaming. Nguyễn Vinh Phúc (_Mặt gương Tây Hồ_) remains the fullest survey of the lake's monuments.

Geology gives the legend an unexpected foundation. West Lake is an **oxbow lake**, a meander of the Red River that was cut off. Ground subsiding, water flooding a depression: the physical process matches the tale's collapsing den. Local fishermen report nets snagging on sunken ironwood trunks, a trace of forested land that predates the water. The deepest point (18-20 m, off Quảng Bá) answers to the original's "deep abyss" (深淵).

## The _"Hồ"_ Wordplay: Fox or Lake?

The most consequential modern finding about this tale belongs to Liam C. Kelley (writing as Le Minh Khai, "A Fox Corpse, West Lake, and the Divided Population of Medieval Vietnam," 13 August 2010).

In Sino-Vietnamese, **hồ 狐 (fox)** and **hồ 湖 (lake)** are perfect homophones. The tale's entire toponymic system runs on that sound: Fox Grotto (Hồ Động), Fox Hamlet (Hồ Thôn), Lỗ Khước, and at the summit the pair **Hồ Thi** (狐屍, fox corpse) set against **Hồ Tây** (西湖, western lake). "Fox Corpse Marsh" and "West Lake" sound nearly identical in Vietnamese, but only a reader who knows both characters can see why.

Kelley's argument: Hồ Động and Hồ Thôn were probably existing place names whose original sense had been lost. A Hán-literate scholar composed the fox story to string every "Hồ" toponym onto a single thread. The pun works only in writing: "only someone who was literate could have understood this story, so it is not a story of 'the people.'" He also flags two loaded terms in the original, "man" (蠻, "savage") for the Tản Viên villagers and "động" (洞, a frontier administrative unit under Chinese rule), as fingerprints of medieval ethnic stratification rather than of primeval memory.

The argument's scope: it concerns the **surviving text**, the work of a literate elite. Whether an older layer of tales or cult practice lies beneath the text is a separate question, and an open one (see Modern Scholarship below).

## Motifs and Symbolism: _A Nine-Tailed Fox Goes South_

Hồ Tinh has the full nine tails of the Chinese literary convention of the **jiǔwěihú** (九尾狐). The motif first appears in the _Shān Hǎi Jīng_ (compiled roughly 4th c. BCE to 1st c. CE): on Qīngqiū mountain lives a beast "shaped like a fox, with nine tails." Guō Pú (276-324) glossed the nine-tailed fox as an auspicious omen of peaceful times. From the figure of Dájǐ (妲己), the fox spirit inhabiting the favorite consort of the last Shang king, the creature turned decisively sinister; by the _Liáozhāi Zhìyì_ (Pú Sōnglíng, 17th c.) fox spirits ranged across the whole moral spectrum, from predator to devoted companion.

Set side by side, the Vietnamese fox stands apart:

- **Scale of operation.** The Chinese fox works at court and brings down dynasties. Hồ Tinh torments one rural district. It is a local calamity, not a palace conspiracy.
- **Etiological function.** The Vietnamese tale exists to explain a landmark: West Lake. The Chinese fox tradition has no such function. The Vietnamese fox dies and leaves a lake; the Chinese fox dies and leaves a moral about desire and power.
- **No cult.** The Japanese kitsune has more than 30,000 Inari shrines, a taxonomy of benevolent zenko and feral yako, foxfire, fox possession. Hồ Tinh is not worshipped anywhere; the shrine at its death site exists to **suppress** it. In moral coloring it is closer to the Korean kumiho (구미호), the almost invariably malevolent fox, than to the many-sided kitsune.

Etymology tells the same story. Vietnamese "hồ ly tinh" is a direct borrowing of Chinese "húlí jīng" (狐狸精); the native word for the animal is "cáo," while "hồ" appears only in supernatural and literary contexts. The motif is imported. The function is thoroughly naturalized: it explains land, it does not admonish kings.

One absence is telling: **there are no foxes on Đông Sơn bronze drums**. Birds, deer, frogs, crocodiles abound; foxes never appear. The Xuân La site (Tây Hồ district) confirms Đông Sơn era settlement on the very shore of the lake, yet no native fox iconography has surfaced. The nine-tailed fox most likely arrived by book, not from the soil.

## The Three-Demon Cycle: _Three Demons, Three Ecologies_

Hồ Tinh is the middle link of the demon-slaying trio that opens the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_:

| Demon | Hán | Ecology | Territory | Method | Outcome |
|-------|-----|---------|-----------|--------|---------|
| Ngư Tinh | 魚精 | Sea | Eastern seaboard | Red-hot iron pillar | Killed; corpse becomes an island |
| Hồ Tinh | 狐精 | Plains and lakes | Long Biên / West Lake | Water Palace troops flood the den | Killed; den becomes a lake |
| Mộc Tinh | 木精 | Forest and mountains | Phong Châu | Arts of power, then a ruse | Survives until the Đinh dynasty |

Read as a set, the three tales are a program: Lạc Long Quân clears the dangers of the sea, the land, and the forest, the three ecologies the early Việt had to live with, before the Hồng Bàng story divides the hundred children between mountain and sea. Only after the clearing does he teach the people to grow rice, build houses, and tattoo their bodies. This is a civilizing plot, ground-laying for settlement; modern retellings tend to inflate it into military epic, but the original reads closer to land clearance than to conquest.

Within the trio, Hồ Tinh has one distinction: it is the demon of the **future capital**. The tale opens by locating the land of Thăng Long and even cites Lý Thái Tổ's dragon-sighting at the river. The writer looks at West Lake from Thăng Long and projects backward into antiquity: the imperial ground was once demon ground, cleansed before there were kings. That all three demons carry the character **"tinh"** (精), the Daoist term for a long-lived being that ripens into a spirit, is one more Hán fingerprint on the cycle's design.

For the other two battles and the full career of the dragon lord, see the entries on Ngư Tinh, Mộc Tinh, and Lạc Long Quân.

## Variants: _From Flood to Sword_

**The popular modern version** (the one most Vietnamese readers know): Lạc Long Quân goes to the cave alone, sword in hand, summons wind, rain, and thunder, fights the fox for three days and three nights; the weakened fox flees, is beheaded, and reverts to its true form as a giant nine-tailed fox; only then is the den flooded. None of this single combat exists in the LNCQ original, which records only the order to the Water Palace. Oral tradition turned the commander into a swordsman, a familiar heroizing drift.

**A children's variant** has Lạc Long Quân snare the fox with a lasso braided from five-colored thread, unlike any other version.

**The Trấn Vũ variant** at Quán Thánh temple: according to temple custodian Bùi Hồng Sơn, in the temple's own tradition the fox is destroyed by Huyền Thiên Trấn Vũ (玄天鎮武), the Daoist guardian of the north, not by Lạc Long Quân. The fox-slaying motif was transferred to another deity, a layering that likely reflects Daoist influence in the Lý and Trần periods.

**The Trích Sài legend**: the Phúc Lộc temple in Trích Sài village, on the lake's shore, venerates the princesses Vạn Phúc and Vạn Lộc, said to have destroyed fox demons around the lake in the time of Lý Nam Đế (6th c.) with the aid of Vạn Thọ Phu Nhân. One body of water, and the fox-killing motif retold at least three times with three different slayers.

One textual slip worth recording: the table of contents of the Đinh Gia Khánh - Nguyễn Ngọc San translation (1960) prints "Truyện **Hổ** tinh," the Tiger Spirit, almost certainly confusing the characters 狐 (fox) and 虎 (tiger); the content is unmistakably the nine-tailed fox.

## Modern Scholarship

Hồ Tinh sits at the center of the larger debate over the nature of Vietnamese mythology, with three main lines of reading.

**Folk memory.** Nguyễn Đổng Chi (_Lược khảo về thần thoại Việt Nam_, 1956), the first systematic study of Vietnamese mythology, called myths "the shadow of historical events": not reliable records, but reflections of real social conditions. He placed the demon-slaying tales in the culture-hero class and observed that Vietnamese mythology leans "realist, humanized, humanistic," with little of the monstrous gigantism of other traditions. On this reading, Hồ Tinh encodes the experience of taming the marshlands along the Red River. Lê Văn Hảo, reviewing the book in BEFEO (Tome 52, No. 1, 1964, pp. 253-256), praised it but objected that Nguyễn Đổng Chi separated mythological gods too neatly from the gods of living cult; behind the village tutelary cults, he argued, lie "secret myths and rituals of agrarian origin" still to be excavated.

**Revisionist.** Tạ Chí Đại Trường (_Những bài dã sử Việt_, 2009; _Sử Việt đọc vài quyển_, 2004) treated the Hùng kings and the Lạc Long Quân stratum as late constructions of the medieval elite, later elevated by the modern state into an untouchable national origin myth. Liam C. Kelley works the same vein, and for this particular tale he holds the most concrete textual evidence: the "hồ" pun visible only to the Hán-literate, the fox motif borrowed from Chinese letters, and the vocabulary of "man" and "động" reflecting medieval ethnic hierarchy.

**Synthetic.** Keith Taylor (_The Birth of Vietnam_, 1983, pp. 1-41 and Appendix A, pp. 303-305) read the whole Lạc Long Quân cycle as the memory of "a sea-oriented culture coming to terms with a continental environment," popular lore accumulated over time and edited in the fifteenth century. He notes that the Dragon Lord "subdued all evil demons in the land and civilized the people," but does not analyze the fox tale separately. Trần Đình Hoành (commentary, 2015) reads it philosophically: the fox in white clothes is deception living inside the community itself, and it takes a flood, the mobilized force of a whole system, to break its den; the suppression shrine built afterward concedes that such an evil can be held down but never uprooted.

What remains open: the three lines of reading answer three different questions, and none has closed the debate. Kelley's textual evidence concerns who wrote the surviving text; Nguyễn Đổng Chi's line concerns what social experience the tale reflects; Taylor's synthesis holds both layers at once. One point every line records: the etiological function (explaining West Lake) and the tale's place in the three-ecology civilizing program have no counterpart in the Chinese fox corpus. The fox is borrowed; the lake is Vietnamese.

## Living Legacy

Of the three demon tales, Hồ Tinh has the most visible physical remainder, because its "evidence" is 500 hectares of water in the middle of the capital.

**Kim Ngưu pagoda** (Quảng An ward, Tây Hồ district): the only shrine the LNCQ text itself names as the suppression site. Destroyed by French artillery in 1947, rebuilt in 2000; it preserves ten royal certificates of investiture.

**Quán Thánh temple**: at the lake's southeastern corner, one of the Four Guardian Temples of Thăng Long, recognized as a Special National Monument (2024), and keeper of the Trấn Vũ variant of the fox slaying.

**Trấn Quốc pagoda**: the oldest pagoda in Hà Nội (founded under Lý Nam Đế, 541-548, originally named Khai Quốc), moved onto the Kim Ngư islet in the lake in 1615. Not connected to the tale directly, but it stands on the very water the legend calls a collapsed foxhole.

**Phúc Lộc temple (Trích Sài)**: keeper of the lake's second fox-slaying legend.

The name "Fox Corpse Marsh" still opens most accounts of West Lake as its oldest name. In popular culture, Vietnamese fox spirits in games and comics are usually drawn after the Japanese kitsune or the Chinese nine-tailed fox; the one detail that is distinctively Vietnamese, a dead fox leaving behind a lake, is the one least often used.

## Notes for Use

**Historicity.** The tale is not a source for antiquity. It is absent from every official history, and the textual evidence (the literate pun, the borrowed motif, the medieval administrative vocabulary) points to a composition by a Hán-literate scholar, anchored at the earliest to the late 14th century date of the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ itself. Its value lies in its etiological function and its place in the civilizing cycle, not in the dating of events.

**Who kills the fox, and how.** In the original, Lạc Long Quân sends the Water Palace troops. The sword, the three-day duel, and the beheading are modern folk accretions; when citing the original, leave them out.

**"Hổ tinh" in the Đinh Gia Khánh edition.** The 1960 table of contents misprints the title as "Tiger Spirit"; citations should read "Hồ tinh" (狐精) per the content and the Hán original.

**"Cáo Nhi tự."** This pagoda name circulates in some online articles but could not be verified in any source consulted; do not use it.

**Thi viện misattribution.** Some websites (Thi viện among them) attribute the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ to Nguyễn Dữ. The LNCQ was composed by Trần Thế Pháp and redacted by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú; Nguyễn Dữ wrote the _Truyền Kỳ Mạn Lục_, a different work.

**The tail count.** The original specifies nine tails (白狐九尾); illustrations and adaptations should keep the number.
