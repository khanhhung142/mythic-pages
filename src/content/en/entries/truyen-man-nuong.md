## The Story

The Dâu region lies on a river, in the old land of Luy Lâu, a place where trading boats had long gone up and down and people from many lands had gathered. The people here lived by the wet rice fields, and their harvest hung on the sky. When the sky gave rain the rice rose; when it withheld it the fields cracked and the wells ran dry. So the people of Dâu worshipped trees and stones, prayed to cloud and rain, and held that a spirit dwelt in the old tree by the road and in the stone under the river.

Into that country came monks from far away, who raised temples and chanted sutras strange to local ears. One of them was Già La Xà Lê, a man of deep practice said to hold powers. He was abbot of the temple in the heart of the Dâu region, and in that temple a poor girl kept the kitchen.

Her name was Man Nương, an orphan the temple had taken in. She stammered and could not chant the sutras aloud, so the monks gave her the cooking: rice, water, the meals for the whole community. Every night she waited for the monks to finish chanting before she laid out the food. That night they chanted late, and while she sat waiting by the kitchen door she drifted off, her legs drawn up against the cold, lying across the threshold.

Deep in the night, the abbot came down from the temple hall. The passage was narrow and the girl lay across the threshold. He stepped over her. That single step, and from that night Man Nương was with child.

Frightened, she went to the monk. He neither denied it nor scolded her. When her time came, Man Nương bore a daughter, and she carried the child back to return it to him. The monk led her into the forest, to a great tree. He called out, and the trunk split open along a seam. He laid the infant inside, and the tree closed as if it had never opened, the little girl gone into the wood. Before they parted, the monk gave Man Nương a staff, and told her that in a year of drought, when the ground went dry, she should strike it into the earth and water would come.

Years later a great drought came to Dâu. The fields cracked, the wells ran dry. Remembering the monk's words, Man Nương struck the staff into the ground, and water rose through the hole it left. The whole region got through the thirst because of her.

By then the monk was past ninety. A storm of wind and rain tore the tree from the forest. Uprooted, it drifted down the current until it reached the river landing right in front of the temple, and there it bobbed and would not float on. The villagers ran ropes, yoked buffalo, set a hundred people heaving at it, and the tree would not stir. Man Nương came down to the landing to wash, and her hand brushed the trunk. All at once it was weightless. She lifted it alone and set it on the bank.

Taking this for an omen, they brought in woodworkers to carve the tree into statues. Cutting into the heart of the wood, they struck a stone, round and hard, that threw sparks against the adze. They flung the stone into the river. The moment it was gone, the workers dropped dead. Word reached Man Nương, and she went down to the landing and burned incense in prayer. Divers went to the riverbed, found the stone again, and set it back into the base of the statue; the spot where it had fallen became a deep pool.

The four statues were finished. The monk named them one by one: Pháp Vân, Pháp Vũ, Pháp Lôi, Pháp Điện, which is Cloud, Rain, Thunder, Lightning. The four were carried to four temples across the region. From then on, whenever the sky held back its rain, people came to pray, and the rain fell.

Man Nương lived out her days in Dâu. When she died, the region worshipped her as Buddha Mother, mother of the four Buddhas. The day she died they kept as the Buddha's birthday. Her daughter, the infant given to the tree long ago, had become a glowing stone within the wood, worshipped as Thạch Quang Phật, set beside the statue of Pháp Vân at Dâu Temple, where the story begins and where the whole of it is kept.

## The Plot in _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_

Man Nương is a poor orphan at a temple in the Dâu region who stammers and cannot chant, and so cooks for the monks. One night the monks chant late; Man Nương, waiting to serve the meal, falls asleep at the door. The monk Già La Xà Lê steps over her and she conceives. She bears a daughter, returns it to the monk, and he places the child in a tree and gives her a staff to draw water in drought. When the monk is past ninety, the tree falls and drifts to the temple landing; no one can retrieve it, only Man Nương can lift it onto the bank. Carvers cut four statues, strike a stone, throw it in the river, and die; Man Nương prays, and the stone is recovered and returned to the statue. The monk names the four Pháp Vân, Pháp Vũ, Pháp Lôi, Pháp Điện. Man Nương is called Buddha Mother, and the day of her death is kept as the Buddha's birthday.

The tale belongs to Volume 2 of the main compilation, in the run of stories after the _Tale of the Golden Turtle_. Its number shifts between 14 and 15 depending on whether a given recension counts the _Tale of the Trưng Sisters_ into the sequence ([Nguyễn Thị Anh, _Journal of Buddhist Studies_, 2024](https://tapchinghiencuuphathoc.vn/tim-hieu-ve-tu-phap-voi-tin-nguong-tho-mau.html)).

## Two Original Recensions: _Cổ Châu_ and _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_

The tale of Man Nương has no single text. Its earliest version lies not in _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ but in the _Cổ Châu Pháp Vân Phật bản hạnh ngữ lục_ (古珠法雲佛本行語錄), a Chinese-character text with a line-by-line Nôm translation, cut in woodblock in 1752 and drawn from the lost _Báo cực truyện_ (報極傳). [K. W. Taylor (_The Journal of Asian Studies_ 77/1, 2018, pp. 107-122)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies/article/what-lies-behind-the-earliest-story-of-buddhism-in-ancient-vietnam/DB39DCFD3FAD0971F056A896E3664C2F) dates the _Báo cực truyện_ to roughly the late 11th through early 14th century. The original blocks belong to the Dâu Temple woodblock set, manuscript A.818 at the Institute of Hán-Nôm Studies.

The two versions differ on several core points ([Taylor 2018](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies/article/what-lies-behind-the-earliest-story-of-buddhism-in-ancient-vietnam/DB39DCFD3FAD0971F056A896E3664C2F)):

| Detail | Cổ Châu (earlier) | Lĩnh Nam chích quái (15th c.) |
|---|---|---|
| The girl's name | A Man (阿蠻) | Man Nương (蠻娘) |
| The monk's name | Khâu Đà La (丘陀羅) | Già La Xà Lê (伽羅闍梨) |
| Court setting | Includes Sĩ Nhiếp and Tu Định (A Man's father) | Absent |
| The conception | She conceives after years serving the monk | A virgin conceives when the monk steps over her |
| Length | The full text | Cut to about a third |

The Cổ Châu recension sets the tale under Sĩ Nhiếp (士燮), governor of Giao Châu: a Brahmin named Khâu Đà La arrives from India, is taken in by Tu Định, and names his host's daughter A Man. _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ drops this entire court layer, keeps only the girl and the monk, and turns the conception into a virgin birth. [Taylor](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies/article/what-lies-behind-the-earliest-story-of-buddhism-in-ancient-vietnam/DB39DCFD3FAD0971F056A896E3664C2F) reads that shortening as a "cleansing" of the tale along Confucian moral lines, converting an ordinary pregnancy into a chaste prodigy. He also explains Man Nương as a Sinicized form of "Ả Man" in old Vietnamese, where "ả" means a young woman.

The _Cổ Châu Tứ Pháp phả lục_ (古珠四法譜錄, manuscript A.2051), printed in 1919, adds a further detail: that Tu Định came from Cao Man (Cambodia), used to gloss the surname "Man" ([Nguyễn Thị Anh, _Journal of Buddhist Studies_, 2024](https://tapchinghiencuuphathoc.vn/tim-hieu-ve-tu-phap-voi-tin-nguong-tho-mau.html)). The tree is usually written as the dung thụ (榕樹, a fig of the mulberry family); many popular translations mistake it for the phù dung.

## The Tứ Pháp: _Cloud, Rain, Thunder, Lightning_

The four statues carved from the sacred tree carry four Buddhist titles: Pháp Vân (法雲, cloud), Pháp Vũ (法雨, rain), Pháp Lôi (法雷, thunder), Pháp Điện (法電, lightning). Four faces of a single storm, named in the order a farmer reads the sky: cloud gathers, rain falls, thunder rolls, lightning strikes. Four nature goddesses of the native rainmaking cult are given Buddhist names, and Man Nương, the mother in the tale, becomes mother to all four.

Dâu Temple, enshrining Pháp Vân, is the center. Pháp Vân is treated as the "eldest sister," standing for the whole Tứ Pháp system, and was carried to the capital to pray for rain again and again through the Lý, Trần, and Lê dynasties ([Nguyễn Thị Anh, _Journal of Buddhist Studies_, 2024](https://tapchinghiencuuphathoc.vn/tim-hieu-ve-tu-phap-voi-tin-nguong-tho-mau.html)). This is one of the clearest cases of syncretism between imported Buddhism and Vietnamese agrarian belief: the old rain ritual stays intact, and only the names of the deities change to the names of Buddhas.

## _Three Time Layers_: Text, Setting, Object

Reading the tale of Man Nương means keeping three different dates apart, or the framing device gets mistaken for history.

**The story's self-declared setting** is the Sĩ Nhiếp era, the 2nd to 3rd century. This is the frame of a temple legend, not an established date. [Taylor (2018)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies/article/what-lies-behind-the-earliest-story-of-buddhism-in-ancient-vietnam/DB39DCFD3FAD0971F056A896E3664C2F) notes there is no firm evidence in Chinese sources for a Buddhist presence in northern Vietnam under Sĩ Nhiếp; the temple-founding dates of "187-226" are cited as legend, not archaeology.

**The textual layer** is much later: the Cổ Châu version derives from the _Báo cực truyện_ (c. 11th-14th c.), _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ took shape in the 15th century, and the Dâu Temple blocks were cut in 1752.

**The material layer** is later still: the Tứ Pháp statues, per [Bắc Ninh Museum](https://baotangbacninh.vn/ds-bao-vat/10), "share a single dating to the Lê period, around the 16th century," and were recognized as a National Treasure by Decision 2090/QĐ-TTg of 25 December 2017. The statues venerated today were made roughly thirteen centuries after the story's setting.

## Textual History: _the Hán-Nôm Recensions_

Four core Hán-Nôm texts are cited by manuscript number in [Nguyễn Thị Anh (_Journal of Buddhist Studies_, 2024)](https://tapchinghiencuuphathoc.vn/tim-hieu-ve-tu-phap-voi-tin-nguong-tho-mau.html): the _Cổ Châu Pháp Vân Phật bản hạnh ngữ lục_ (A.818), _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ (A.33), _Việt điện u linh tập_ (A.751), and _Cổ Châu Tứ Pháp phả lục_ (A.2051).

The Dâu Temple woodblock set holds 107 carved blocks, dated 1752-1859, recognized as a National Treasure by Decision 73/QĐ-TTg of 18 January 2024 ([Government Committee for Religious Affairs](https://btgcp.gov.vn/doi-song-tin-nguong-ton-giao/moc-ban-chua-dau-duoc-cong-nhan-bao-vat-quoc-gia-postMXRB27LR0D.html); [Department of Cultural Heritage](https://dsvh.gov.vn/moc-ban-chua-dau-bac-ninh-22166)). Among them, the _Cổ Châu hạnh_ is a verse rendering in the lục bát meter and the _Cổ Châu lục_ is a Chinese prose text with a Nôm translation. That Nôm text is itself a linguistic source: [John D. Phan (Springer, 2024)](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-981-97-4314-8_4) uses the _Cổ Châu Pháp Vân Phật bản hạnh ngữ lục_ to trace the semantic broadening of the Nôm particle "chưng."

The fullest modern critical edition is [Nguyễn Thị Oanh, _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ (Study, Annotated Translation, Chinese Original Text), NXB Khoa học Xã hội, 2024](https://science.thanglong.edu.vn/index.php/vola/article/view/346), which collates the recensions held in Hanoi. The long-standard translation is that of Đinh Gia Khánh and Nguyễn Ngọc San (NXB Văn hóa, 1960).

## _One Reading_: Taylor and the Native Cult Layer

The tale of Man Nương is often cited as the earliest evidence for Buddhism in Vietnam. [Taylor (2018)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies/article/what-lies-behind-the-earliest-story-of-buddhism-in-ancient-vietnam/DB39DCFD3FAD0971F056A896E3664C2F) reads it the other way. He holds that the tale, for all its Buddhist vocabulary, "contains no distinctively Buddhist content, but rather is a tale of thaumaturgy, the worship of trees and of rocks, and rainmaking." A child turned to stone, a sacred tree adrift, a staff that draws water from the ground, statues that answer prayers for rain: these are motifs of nature worship, not of doctrine.

He goes a step further and proposes a South Indian origin for the two central names: Khâu Đà La may transcribe Kaundinya (a South Indian Brahmin line), while A Man connects to Amman, whom he describes as "a popular Mother Deity and Rain Goddess among the Tamils of southern India; she has many forms and names." The inference rests on phonology and on the maritime trade between India and Giao Châu.

**What remains open.** The Kaundinya and Amman hypothesis is a phonological inference and not yet a scholarly consensus. What the sources agree on is clearer: the tale wraps a native cult core, tree and rock worship and rainmaking, inside a layer of Buddhist naming, and the Tứ Pháp system is a product of that fusion. [Taylor](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/journal-of-asian-studies/article/what-lies-behind-the-earliest-story-of-buddhism-in-ancient-vietnam/DB39DCFD3FAD0971F056A896E3664C2F) also credits the earliest study to use this tale for dating the beginnings of Vietnamese Buddhism to Trần Văn Giáp (1932).

## Sites and _Worship_

The cult of the Tứ Pháp and Man Nương clusters around the Dâu region, the old land of Luy Lâu, today in Thuận Thành, Bắc Ninh. Five main sites:

**Dâu Temple** (Diên Ứng tự 延應寺 / Pháp Vân tự 法雲寺, also Cổ Châu), enshrining Pháp Vân and Thạch Quang Phật, the center of the whole system. A Special National Monument, listed in 2013; the Hòa Phong tower and the woodblock set are its signature objects.

**Đậu Temple** (Thành Đạo tự 成道寺 / Pháp Vũ tự 法雨寺), enshrining Pháp Vũ. **Tướng Temple** (Phi Tướng đại thiền tự 非相大禪寺), enshrining Pháp Lôi. **Dàn Temple** (Trí Quả tự 智果寺, also called Phương Quan), enshrining Pháp Điện.

**Tổ Temple** (Phúc Nghiêm tự) in Mãn Xá village, Hà Mãn commune, enshrines the Buddha Mother Man Nương ([Bắc Ninh Provincial Portal](https://bacninh.gov.vn/news/-/details/20182/chua-to)). This is the main site to the mother in the tale, set apart from the four temples to her four children.

The Dâu Temple festival opens on the 8th of the fourth lunar month, coinciding with the Buddha's birthday, with a procession that carries the Tứ Pháp statues to Dâu Temple and pays homage to the Buddha Mother at Tổ Temple, all directed at prayer for timely rain. Both the Tứ Pháp statues and the Dâu woodblock set are National Treasures ([Bắc Ninh Museum](https://baotangbacninh.vn/ds-bao-vat/10); [Government Committee for Religious Affairs](https://btgcp.gov.vn/doi-song-tin-nguong-ton-giao/moc-ban-chua-dau-duoc-cong-nhan-bao-vat-quoc-gia-postMXRB27LR0D.html)).

## Notes _on Use_

**The Sĩ Nhiếp setting is not history.** The period the tale places itself in, the 2nd to 3rd century, is the frame of a hagiography with no archaeological basis. The temple-founding dates of "187-226" are legend. Using the tale to fix a date for "when Buddhism entered Vietnam" reads a temple legend as a chronicle.

**Do not collapse the three time layers.** The story's setting, the date of the text, and the date of the objects are centuries apart. The Tứ Pháp statues are Lê-period work of around the 16th century ([Bắc Ninh Museum](https://baotangbacninh.vn/ds-bao-vat/10)), not primordial images from the time of Sĩ Nhiếp.

**Two original recensions differ.** When citing character names, state which version: A Man and Khâu Đà La are the Cổ Châu recension; Man Nương and Già La Xà Lê are _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_. Mixing the two is a common error.

**The South Indian hypothesis is a hypothesis.** Taylor's reading of Kaundinya and Amman rests on phonological inference and should be presented as a scholarly hypothesis, not a settled conclusion.

**Thi viện misattributes the author.** Some websites, Thi viện among them, wrongly ascribe _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_ to Nguyễn Dữ. The work was compiled by Trần Thế Pháp and revised by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú; Nguyễn Dữ is the author of _Truyền Kỳ Mạn Lục_.

**Administrative names are shifting.** The administrative units around Thuận Thành (Thanh Khương, Trí Quả, Hà Mãn) have had recent changes in name and tier; verify the current administrative name when citing a specific address.
