## The Story

*This telling follows the Vietnamese tradition attached to the Hát Môn temple, built on the sequence of events in the Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư. The earliest source, the Hou Han shu, was written from the Han side and gives a different ending; see Comparing the sources below.*

Before the Han came down, the Red River delta was the land of the Lạc Việt. People lived in districts strung along the rivers, worked the wet rice fields, and cast bronze drums whose faces carried boats and dancers in feathered headdresses. At the head of each district stood a Lạc lord, an office passed from father to child since the time of the Hùng kings. This was land the Lạc Việt had held for generations, with its own order and its own leaders.

Then the empire of the north came down. The Han folded the whole region into the commandery of Giao Chỉ, one of the empire's southernmost, and set grand administrators over it. The Han officials brought Han law, Han taxes, and a machinery meant to bend the native people to a northern mold; the authority of the Lạc lords narrowed year by year under the hand of the commandery. When Tô Định became grand administrator of Giao Chỉ, the yoke grew heavier still: harsh law, extortion, killing.

In the spring of the year Canh Tý, the year 40 of the common era, at the mouth of the Hát River on the bank of the Red River, people came in from every direction. Standing before them was Trưng Trắc, daughter of the Lạc lord of Mê Linh district, and beside her stood her younger sister, Trưng Nhị.

The anger of a whole region had gathered in her house. Trưng Trắc's husband was Thi Sách, son of the Lạc lord of Chu Diên, another Lạc lineage. Tô Định put Thi Sách to death, and the wrong done to her husband's house rose on top of the common grievance of the governed. Trưng Trắc chose to raise an army.

She did not raise it alone. The Lạc lords, the heads of the Lạc Việt districts who had chafed under the Han officials, brought their own people over to her. The rebels drove straight at the provincial seat where the administrator held his office. The walls broke. Tô Định cut off his hair and beard, slipped through the fighting in disguise, and escaped back to Han soil, abandoning the whole commandery of Giao Chỉ.

Word of the victory ran outward. Cửu Chân, Nhật Nam, and Hợp Phố, the Lạc Việt lands stretching south and east, rose in answer. In a short time the sisters' forces held more than sixty walled towns across the region the Han called Lĩnh Ngoại. A whole belt of country that had lain under the grand administrators was back in native hands.

Trưng Trắc took the throne. She styled herself Trưng Vương and made her capital at Mê Linh, her own home ground, in the country where her ancestors had been Lạc lords. A woman of the Lạc Việt stood at the head of a state, in an age when neither the empire of the north nor the ritual order of the north left any room for such a thing. She reigned for three years.

The Han did not leave it alone. In 42 the emperor sent Ma Yuan, the seasoned old general given the title General Who Calms the Waves, down with a great army by sea and over the mountains. This was one of the most battle-hardened commanders of the empire, a man who had fought from the northwest to the far south. The two armies met at Lãng Bạc, the marsh country to the north. The Han were many, drilled, and used to great battles; the sisters' army was a coalition of Lạc lords two years old. At Lãng Bạc the sisters' forces took the worse of it and had to fall back.

Ma Yuan pursued. The sisters withdrew to Cấm Khê and tried to hold. Their soldiers thinned, and the ground under them narrowed. Then the last line broke too.

By the Vietnamese tradition, when everything was lost, the sisters would not fall into the enemy's hands. They turned back toward the mouth of the Hát River, where three years earlier the fighters of every district had gathered to them, and gave themselves to the water. The year was 43.

The Lạc Việt did not forget. At Hát Môn, where the sisters are said to have died, the people raised a temple; every year on the anniversary they offer bánh trôi, small white dumplings set floating on water. At Mê Linh, where she was born and reigned, there is a temple too. Generations later, people told of the sisters' stone statue drifting up the Nhị River, shining in the dark, until the villagers wrapped it in red cloth and carried it ashore to found the Đồng Nhân temple inside the capital. From two women who took up arms to hold their land, the people made two gods.

## Background: _Giao Chỉ under the Eastern Han_

At the start of the common era, the Red River delta was the land of the Lạc Việt, organized into districts under native Lạc lords who held their office by inheritance. The Han had made the region into the commandery of Giao Chỉ, one of the empire's southernmost, and posted grand administrators there. The authority of the Lạc lords and that of the Han officials overlapped, and the pressure of sinicization grew heavier by the year. Trưng Trắc and Trưng Nhị were born into that Lạc-lord class, daughters of the Lạc lord of Mê Linh; Trưng Trắc married Thi Sách, son of the Lạc lord of Chu Diên ([Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư, Annals of the Trưng Queen, 1697 woodblock](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/history-of-greater-vietnam/Fulltext/5-Ky-Trung-Nu-Vuong?uiLang=vn)).

This was a native rising to recover self-rule on their own soil, not a "rebellion" as the sources of the side that crushed them call it. That reading is this encyclopedia's baseline. Archaeology shows that before the Han arrived, the Red River basin already held a native political order: the study of the Cổ Loa ramparts by [Nam C. Kim and colleagues (*Antiquity* 84, 2010, pp. 1011-1027)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/antiquity/article/abs/co-loa-an-investigation-of-vietnams-ancient-capital/00332829F65222D3FB94642A83A09979) dates the main rampart to the centuries before the common era. The world of Lạc lords the annals describe has a material footing; it is not an invention.

## The record: _the Hou Han shu and the Toàn thư_

The sequence of events can be reconstructed from two layers of source, with the discrepancies noted below.

The [*Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư*](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/history-of-greater-vietnam/Fulltext/5-Ky-Trung-Nu-Vuong?uiLang=vn) records that Trưng Trắc, "aggrieved that the administrator Tô Định bound her with the law, and hating that Định had killed her husband," rose with her sister Trưng Nhị in the year Canh Tý (40 CE), stormed the provincial seat, and drove Tô Định back to China. Nam Hải, Cửu Chân, Nhật Nam, and Hợp Phố answered, and sixty-five towns in Lĩnh Ngoại fell to them. Trưng Trắc proclaimed herself queen, made Mê Linh her capital, and held the throne for three years.

The far earlier source is the [*Hou Han shu*, Biography of Ma Yuan](https://ctext.org/hou-han-shu/ma-yuan-lie-zhuan) of Fan Ye, written from the Han court's viewpoint: "the woman of Giao Chỉ, Trưng Trắc, and her younger sister Trưng Nhị revolted... Trắc set herself up as queen" (交阯女子徵側及女弟徵貳反... 側自立爲王), and took more than sixty towns. The word "revolted" (反) here is the language of the side that suppressed them, not the judgment of this encyclopedia.

On the suppression itself, the *Hou Han shu* is fuller than any Vietnamese source. The Han made Ma Yuan the General Who Calms the Waves. In the spring of the eighteenth year of Jianwu (42 CE), the Han army fought the sisters' forces at Lãng Bạc (浪泊), took several thousand heads and more than ten thousand surrenders, and pursued the sisters to Cấm Khê (禁谿). Early the next year, "in the first month he beheaded Trưng Trắc and Trưng Nhị and sent their heads to Luoyang" (明年正月，斬徵側、徵貳，傳首洛陽), that is, in 43 CE ([*Hou Han shu*, Biography of Ma Yuan](https://ctext.org/hou-han-shu/ma-yuan-lie-zhuan)). This is the point where the Han history and the Vietnamese tradition part ways, discussed below.

## The rising of 40: _a state rebuilt in three years_

What both the Han and the Vietnamese sources agree on is the scale of the revolt. The *Hou Han shu* gives more than sixty towns; the *Toàn thư* and *Cương mục* give sixty-five in Lĩnh Ngoại. The figures differ, but the order of magnitude is the same. The rising did not stop at the seat of Giao Chỉ but spread down to Cửu Chân and Nhật Nam and across to Hợp Phố, taking in nearly the whole of the Lạc Việt and Lĩnh Nam lands under Han control. That Tô Định, an administrator of the empire, had to flee back to China shows that Han district government in Giao Chỉ had collapsed in fact.

Trưng Trắc's proclaiming herself queen, with a capital at Mê Linh, is the crux. The [*Hou Han shu*](https://ctext.org/hou-han-shu/ma-yuan-lie-zhuan) itself uses the phrase "set herself up as queen" (自立爲王), a sovereign title, not an office granted by the Han. For three years this land had a native court of its own. That the Han had to send Ma Yuan, one of the foremost generals of the day, with a great combined army by land and water, is an indirect measure of the weight of the Trưng court: the empire did not treat it as local banditry.

## Ma Yuan and Lãng Bạc: _an uneven field_

The Han counterattack was led by Ma Yuan (馬援), the General Who Calms the Waves, with subordinate commanders under him. By the [*Hou Han shu*](https://ctext.org/hou-han-shu/ma-yuan-lie-zhuan), the Han advanced along the coast, cutting a road through the mountains for more than a thousand li, and then joined battle at Lãng Bạc. There the sisters' forces suffered heavily and had to fall back to Cấm Khê. The Han were the professional army of a unified empire, practiced in long supply lines and set-piece battle; the sisters' force was a coalition of Lạc lords assembled within two years. The *Hou Han shu* also records that Ma Yuan petitioned to cast a bronze pillar (銅柱) to mark the empire's southernmost border after the campaign; the pillar's location was later lost, and this motif appears only in later sources and calls for caution.

## Source asymmetry: _who wrote, and when_

The fact that governs every page written about the sisters is source asymmetry, and it has to be stated plainly.

All the information close to the events comes from the Han side. The [*Hou Han shu*](https://ctext.org/hou-han-shu/ma-yuan-lie-zhuan) was composed by Fan Ye in the 5th century, roughly four hundred years after the events, from the viewpoint of the side that put them down, and it calls the sisters "rebels." The [*Shui jing zhu*](https://ctext.org/shui-jing-zhu/zh) of Li Daoyuan, early 6th century, is the same. No contemporary 1st-century Vietnamese text exists.

The Vietnamese sources come centuries later and are oriented toward the celebration of national heroes: the *Việt điện u linh* of 1329, the [*Toàn thư*](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/history-of-greater-vietnam/Fulltext/5-Ky-Trung-Nu-Vuong?uiLang=vn) cut in the 1697 woodblock, the [*Cương mục*](https://lib.nomfoundation.org/collection/1/volume/251/) compiled in the later 19th century, and hagiographies later still. The concrete consequence: every number, place name, and motive has to be cross-checked and its uncertainty stated. Trưng Trắc and Trưng Nhị are real, recorded in the standard histories of both sides; but much of the personal detail and the fine-grained narrative comes from late sources, and this encyclopedia keeps the two layers apart rather than fusing them.

Three divergences deserve separate treatment, below.

## What remains open

**Whether Thi Sách died before the revolt.** The [*Toàn thư*](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/history-of-greater-vietnam/Fulltext/5-Ky-Trung-Nu-Vuong?uiLang=vn) and the *Cương mục* build the motive of "avenging a husband"; but the [*Hou Han shu*](https://ctext.org/hou-han-shu/ma-yuan-lie-zhuan) and the *Shui jing zhu*, which record the husband under the name "Shi," describe him as alive. [Keith Taylor (*The Birth of Vietnam*, UC Press 1983, pp. 37-41)](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/the-birth-of-vietnam/paper) argues that the detail of Thi Sách's prior death may be a later patriarchal addition, since later historians found it hard to accept a woman leading a revolt while her husband lived, and so needed a death to "spur" her; the Han sources instead show the husband following his wife's lead. This is a question about the texts, and it remains open. It does not touch the certain point: the one who launched and led the revolt was Trưng Trắc.

**Death by their own hand or by beheading.** The [*Hou Han shu*](https://ctext.org/hou-han-shu/ma-yuan-lie-zhuan) records that the sisters were beheaded and their heads sent to Luoyang early in 43. The Vietnamese tradition attached to the Hát Môn temple and to the *Việt điện u linh* records that the sisters gave themselves to the Hát River. The earliest source stands on the first account; devotional memory stands on the second. There is no third source to adjudicate, and the two belong to different kinds of record.

**Whether "Trưng" is a surname.** The [*Toàn thư*](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/history-of-greater-vietnam/Fulltext/5-Ky-Trung-Nu-Vuong?uiLang=vn) asserts that the surname was Trưng, originally Lạc, and rejects taking "Lạc" as the surname. [Stephen O'Harrow (*Asian Perspectives* 22(2), 1979, pp. 140-164)](https://www.jstor.org/stable/pdf/42928006.pdf) is taken as the origin of the hypothesis reading "Trưng Trắc" and "Trưng Nhị" through sericulture (silkworm eggs of the first and second grade); the hypothesis is not firmly established and is often misattributed to Taylor. What the sources agree on: the two sisters were named Trắc and Nhị, of the Lạc-lord house of Mê Linh.

## The _legend_ layer and deification

From the Lý dynasty onward the sisters were not only historical figures but worshipped deities, and the devotional texts do not conceal what they are. This has to be labeled clearly: it is data on the history of the cult and on local memory, not a biographical chronology.

The *Việt điện u linh tập* (1329) carries the tale "The Ladies Trưng," in which the sisters manifest, are prayed to for rain with success by a Lý king, and receive the titles Trinh Linh phu nhân and Chế Thắng phu nhân. This is a devotional text. The motif of the sisters' stone statue drifting on the Nhị River, shining, drawn ashore in red cloth, is tied to the founding of the [Đồng Nhân temple under the Lý](https://360ditich.haibatrung.hanoi.gov.vn/vi/vtour/details/di-tich-lich-su-mieu-hai-ba-trung-11). The founding date varies across sources: some dossiers give 1142, while the *Đại Việt sử lược* records the villagers of Đồng Nhân founding the temple in the year Canh Thìn (1160). [Phan Kế Bính's *Nam Hải dị nhân liệt truyện*](https://nxbkimdong.com.vn/products/nam-hai-di-nhan-liet-truyen) opens its section on the great heroes with Trưng Vương, in the register of retold anecdote.

Many details familiar from the popular image belong to this late layer: the roster of female generals (Lê Chân, Thánh Thiên, Bát Nàn), the mother named Man Thiện, the figure of "thirty-six female generals," the oath at the mouth of the Hát River. Most come from hagiographies and unofficial histories many centuries later, not from 1st-century sources, and should be read as memory built up over time rather than as a record of events.

## Worship and _relics_

The three main cult centers, all Special National Relics, keep the memory of the sisters in three complementary ways.

**Đền Hát Môn** (Hát Môn commune, Phúc Thọ, Hà Nội) is tied to the tradition of the sisters mustering their troops, offering the war banner, and dying. Its festival opens on the 4th to the 6th of the third lunar month, with the custom of offering bánh trôi. By the [Department of Cultural Heritage dossier](https://dsvh.gov.vn/di-tich-lich-su-den-hat-mon-2971), the temple keeps hundreds of antiquities ranging in date from the Lê, Tây Sơn, and Nguyễn to the present; it was ranked a Special National Relic by Decision 2383/QĐ-TTg of December 9, 2013.

**The temple of Hai Bà Trưng at Hạ Lôi** (Mê Linh commune, Mê Linh district) is held by tradition to be where the sisters were born and where they reigned, and is likewise a Special National Relic by [Decision 2383/QĐ-TTg](https://dsvh.gov.vn/di-tich-lich-su-den-hai-ba-trung-2970).

**The Đồng Nhân temple-pagoda-hall complex** (Hai Bà Trưng district, Hà Nội) enshrines the sisters, the statue of Trưng Trắc traditionally in a yellow robe and Trưng Nhị in red, together with attendant female generals; it was ranked a Special National Relic in 2019. On the number of female generals the sources differ: the [district heritage portal's official dossier](https://360ditich.haibatrung.hanoi.gov.vn/vi/vtour/details/di-tich-lich-su-mieu-hai-ba-trung-11) records the sisters and six trusted female generals, while many popular sources give twelve; the official figure is the one to follow. The temple also keeps a stele composed in 1848 by Dương Duy Thanh, the education commissioner of Hà Nội.

## Archaeological _background_: Đông Sơn and Cổ Loa

The material setting of the sisters is the Lạc Việt society of the Đông Sơn culture in the Red River basin, known for its bronze drums and casting, lasting to about the 1st century CE. The Cổ Loa citadel, some seventeen kilometers north of central Hà Nội, holds Phùng Nguyên and Đông Sơn cultural layers. The study by [Nam C. Kim and colleagues (*Antiquity* 84, 2010, pp. 1011-1027)](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/antiquity/article/abs/co-loa-an-investigation-of-vietnams-ancient-capital/00332829F65222D3FB94642A83A09979) dates the main rampart to the centuries before the common era, proving that a native political order existed before Han rule and matching the Lạc-lord picture of the sources. No archaeology ties directly to the sisters as individuals; its value here is in reconstructing the world they stepped out of, not in confirming any single detail of the tale.

## The figure through _the ages_

Each age read the sisters by its own need. Under the Lý they were rain-bringing deities, invested as ladies through the *Việt điện u linh*. Later dynasties continued to grant titles and restore their temples, placing the two among the realm-guarding spirits. Confucian historians like Ngô Sĩ Liên gave the sisters their own reign-chapter in the [*Toàn thư*](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/history-of-greater-vietnam/Fulltext/5-Ky-Trung-Nu-Vuong?uiLang=vn), the Annals of the Trưng Queen, acknowledging this as a legitimate dynasty, however short. In the modern period the sisters became a symbol of the spirit of independence and of the place of Vietnamese women in the history of building and defending the country; their name is given to an inner district of Hà Nội, to streets and schools across the country. International historians, from [Keith Taylor](https://www.ucpress.edu/books/the-birth-of-vietnam/paper) to [Stephen O'Harrow](https://www.jstor.org/stable/pdf/42928006.pdf), read the revolt as evidence for the high standing of women in pre-sinicized Lạc society.

## Living _legacy_

The legacy of the sisters lies not in a statue or a line of stone but in a memory that still runs. On the 6th of the third lunar month, the devotional anniversary, the Hát Môn temple holds its festival and offers bánh trôi; the temples at Mê Linh and Đồng Nhân keep their own spring festivals. The three temples are Special National Relics, holding a chain of worship that runs from the Lý to the present. In the political and educational language of modern Vietnam, "Hai Bà Trưng" is a phrase everyone knows, at the head of a tradition of resistance to invasion that later generations carried on. The most certain thing left, across nearly two thousand years and through the sources of the opposing side, is that two women of the Lạc Việt rebuilt an independent government on their own land and held it three years against the strength of an empire.

## Notes _for use_

**Source asymmetry.** The source nearest the events, the *Hou Han shu*, is from the Han side and was written four centuries later; the Vietnamese sources are later still and celebratory. Using the sisters as real historical figures is solid ground; quoting personal details or micro-narrative as certain fact requires a citation and a statement of uncertainty.

**Uncertain dates.** The sisters' birth year (often given as 14 CE) appears only in late sources and is not secure. The death date, the 6th of the third lunar month, is a devotional anniversary, not a verified solar date. The year of the rising (40 CE) and of the defeat (43 CE) are relatively firm because the *Hou Han shu* (Jianwu 16 and 18-19) and the [*Toàn thư*](https://nomfoundation.org/nom-project/history-of-greater-vietnam/Fulltext/5-Ky-Trung-Nu-Vuong?uiLang=vn) agree.

**Details of the late layer.** Ma Yuan's bronze pillar, the figure of "thirty-six female generals," the mother Man Thiện, the oath at the Hát River, and the full roster of female generals all belong to late sources or to hagiography and unofficial history. Label them accordingly and do not use them as secure biographical fact.

**Troop numbers.** Specific army figures (thirty to eighty thousand) appear only in popular and secondary sources, not in 1st-century material; do not cite them as history.

**Contested place names.** The locations of Lãng Bạc, of Cấm Khê (禁谿, written Cẩm Khê 錦溪 in some texts), and the bounds of old Mê Linh district are all still debated. State this when placing them on a modern map.

**Hagiographies are hagiography.** Temple hagiographies and temple-founding legends are valuable for the history of the cult and for local memory, but cannot serve as a chronology of the sisters' lives.
