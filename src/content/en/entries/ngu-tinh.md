## The Story

The boats went out before first light. By evening, a few splintered planks drifted back onto the sand. The people did not. Along that stretch of the East Sea coast, in the deep past, the fishing villages knew such evenings well: women standing on the beach watching the water, old men looking at a far line of waves and shaking their heads. Everyone knew where the boats were lost. No one could do anything about it.

This was remote antiquity, before the kingdom of Văn Lang was founded, when the ancient Việt already lived by the water, building boats to fish and trade along the coast. Across that sea road ran a reef of jagged rock, teeth of stone cutting the shoreline, wild with wind and waves the whole year round. Under the rocks was a cave. In the cave lived a monster.

It had once been a fish, grown old in the sea until it turned into a spirit. Its body ran more than fifty trượng from head to tail, and along it swarmed countless legs, like the legs of a centipede. It could change shape endlessly; no one ever knew what form it would take. Whenever it left its cave, storms boiled up across the whole gulf. It lurked at the foot of the rocks, and any boat that passed, it swallowed, boat and crew together. People called it Ngư Tinh, the Fish Spirit, and called the reef the Ngư Tinh rocks. The sea road through there was as good as cut: to travel the coast you went the long way around, and even the long way was not safe.

Immortals in heaven took pity on the people. Night after night they came down and chiseled at the rock, meaning to open a channel so boats could slip through without passing the monster's door. Ngư Tinh found out. It turned itself into a white rooster, stood on the mountain, and crowed. The immortals heard the cock-crow, thought dawn was breaking, and flew home, leaving the work half done. The half-cut channel is called Phật Đào Cảng, the harbor the immortals dug. The monster stayed in its cave, and the sea kept taking people.

Ruling the land of the Lạc Việt in those days was Lạc Long Quân. He was the son of Kinh Dương Vương, first king of the southern land, and he carried the blood of dragons: he moved through water as other men move on solid ground, and he knew the roads of the water palace the way a farmer knows his own field paths. The people of this land looked to him as a father; whenever a great trouble came, they called, and he came. When word reached him that Ngư Tinh was eating people and had cut the sea road, Lạc Long Quân resolved to destroy it.

You do not fight a monster in its own cave, in its own storm, by strength. He used cunning. First he sent the Dạ Xoa, the fierce demon guards of the water palace, to carry an order to the gods of the sea: no wind, no waves. The water around the reef went still. Then he worked his transformation and became an ordinary commoner's boat, the very kind of boat Ngư Tinh had been swallowing like bait fish. The boat rowed straight for the mouth of the cave.

Ngư Tinh heard it come and slid out. On the boat, Lạc Long Quân held up a man, feigning, as if about to throw the offering down to it. The monster knew that meal well. It opened its mouth wide to swallow. In that instant he hurled into its gullet a block of iron burned glowing red.

The iron seared its throat. Ngư Tinh convulsed, heaving the sea up in walls, and lashed its tail against the boat. Lạc Long Quân cut the tail clean off and stripped the skin, spreading it over a mountain out at sea. That place was afterward called Bạch Long Vĩ, the White Dragon's Tail. The severed head floated out to open water, turned into a dog, and swam for its life. He walled the sea with stone, cut off its road, and struck it down. The dog's head became a mountain, called Cẩu Đầu Sơn, Dog's Head Mountain. The body drifted away to the region of Mạn Cầu, and the place bears the name Mạn Cầu Thủy; one manuscript calls it Cẩu Đầu Thủy.

From then on the sea lay quiet. The reef is still there, the waves still pound it year round, but nothing waits in the cave for boats anymore. The fiercest monster of the southern sea lies scattered as mountains, water, and a handful of names along the coast. That was Lạc Long Quân's first battle against the demons. Later he would go down to the land of Long Biên to destroy a nine-tailed fox, and later still the people of the south would face a thousand-year tree spirit at Phong Châu. But those are stories for another telling.

## The Tale in _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_

Ngư Tinh truyện (魚精傳) is tale 2 of book I in the 22-tale recension edited by Vũ Quỳnh in 1492, immediately after the Hồng Bàng clan tale and opening the demon-slaying trio of Ngư Tinh, Hồ Tinh, and Mộc Tinh. Vũ Quỳnh's preface names it directly: the tales of Ngư Tinh and Hồ Tinh, he writes, show the extermination of demons and the unforgettable grace of the Dragon Lord.

The Chinese text supplies the details worth keeping exact:

- The demon is a **fish-snake spirit** (魚蛇之精; one manuscript reads **Ngư Tinh** 魚精), in the **East Sea** (東海), **over fifty trượng long** (長五十丈餘), **many-legged** (多足), **shaped like a centipede** (似蜈蚣形), transforming endlessly (變化萬端), raising wind and rain as it moves (行則動風雨), able to devour people (能食人).
- It lives in a cave under the Ngư Tinh rocks: "jagged teeth of stone cutting across the shore" (齒齟齬，橫截海濱), "wind and waves so perilous there is no way through" (風濤險惡，無由可通).
- **Immortals** (tiên nhân 仙人; one manuscript reads dị nhân 異人) chisel the rock at night to open a passage; Ngư Tinh **becomes a white rooster and crows on the mountain** (化為白雞嗚山上), tricking them into flight; the unfinished work is **Phật Đào Cảng** (佛陶港).
- The battle runs in four steps: the Dragon Lord **becomes a commoner's boat** (化為民船); orders the **Dạ Xoa of the water palace to forbid the sea gods** to raise wind and waves (令水府夜叉禁海神，不得風濤); **feigns holding up a person** as if to feed the demon (佯持一人); and throws in the **red-hot iron block** (鐵塊通紅火熱).
- The ending: the tail is cut off and its skin spread on a mountain, **Bạch Long Vĩ** (白龍尾); the head floats seaward, becomes a dog, is trapped behind a stone sea-wall and cut down, **Cẩu Đầu Sơn** (狗頭山); the body drifts to Mạn Cầu, **Mạn Cầu Thủy** (曼求水; one manuscript: Cẩu Đầu Thủy 狗頭水).

Two textual points belong here. First, the original uses both **龍君** (Long Quân, Dragon Lord) and **龍王** (Long Vương, Dragon King) for the same figure, an internal inconsistency of the text itself. Second, the annotation **一作** ("another copy reads") recurs throughout, a trace of multiple manuscript variants circulating in parallel: 魚蛇之精 against 魚精, 佛陶港 against 涇, 蛋人 against 蜒人.

Popular retellings compress the ending into "cut the monster into three pieces." The original tells a different sequence: the tail is severed first, the head escapes and is killed separately, the body simply drifts. The three place names are right; the single three-way cut is a later shorthand.

## Textual History: _Manuscripts and Translations_

The Ngư Tinh tale stands in every major surviving manuscript of the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_. The principal copies: **HV.486** (Institute of History, base text for the 1992 Taipei edition), **VHV.1473** and **VHV.2914** (Institute of Hán-Nôm Studies), and **NLVNPF-0652 / R.6** (National Library of Vietnam, digitized): a 39-folio manuscript, 27x15 cm, missing folio 37 and half of folio 40, its cover inscribed "皇黎洪德萬年之二十", attributed to Vũ Quỳnh (武瓊) and Kiều Phú (喬富) and listing 38 tales. Nguyễn Thị Oanh's 720-page monograph (2024) sorts the roughly 15 surviving manuscripts into three families; the details are given in the Mộc Tinh entry under Textual History.

Four main Vietnamese translations:

- **Đinh Gia Khánh and Nguyễn Ngọc San** (NXB Văn học 1960, often reprinted, including an illustrated Kim Đồng edition of 2017 drawn by Tạ Huy Long): the standard scholarly and school text, rendering 似蜈蚣形 as "legs as many as a centipede's" and 化為民船 as "a commoner's boat".
- **Lê Hữu Mục** (Khai Trí, Saigon 1960; repr. Trăm Việt 1982): published the same year, more literary in phrasing, same core content.
- **Nguyễn Hữu Vinh** (translated from the 1992 Taipei edition, with Trần Đình Hoành's commentary, dotchuoinon.com 2015): the translation made directly from the fullest collated Chinese text, but it calls the demon "a fish turned spirit, with a body like a snake" and renders 化為民船 as "a large boat".
- **Nguyên Vũ** (study and annotated translation based on manuscript A.2914): "Truyện Thần ngư tinh" appears at page 390, upper book, tale 2.

The most consequential inconsistency across translations: the original writes **似蜈蚣形** (shaped like a centipede) together with **多足** (many legs), yet several modern translations give "a body like a snake", presumably leaning on the 蛇 in the name 魚蛇之精. The text offers two data points: the name carries "snake", the physical description is a many-legged centipede. A translation that drops the centipede drops the only bodily description the original provides.

## In the Official Histories: _Absent_

Ngư Tinh, like the whole demon-slaying trio, is absent from the official histories, and each work excludes it in its own way.

**Đại Việt Sử Ký Toàn Thư** (Ngô Sĩ Liên, 1479): the Hồng Bàng annal keeps the genealogy of Kinh Dương Vương and Lạc Long Quân (personal name Sùng Lãm 崇纜), the marriage to Âu Cơ and the hundred eggs, and drops all three demon battles. The nearest trace of the water-monster layer is the tattooing custom: "He ordered the people to tattoo their bodies in ink with the form of the Dragon Lord and the shapes of water monsters" (令人以墨刺畫其身，為龍君之形、水怪之狀). Ngô Sĩ Liên states that he consulted the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_; he read the Ngư Tinh tale and chose to leave it out of the national history.

**Khâm Định Việt Sử Thông Giám Cương Mục** (1856-1884): rejects the entire Kinh Dương Vương and Lạc Long Quân layer. The court historians memorialized that these names "belong to the age of chaos, where the author built something out of nothing, and are likely unreliable", and pointed to borrowings from the Tang tale "Liễu Nghị truyện". Emperor Tự Đức pronounced the material baseless stories of "demon buffalo and spirit snakes" and demoted all of it to an appendix note.

**Việt Điện U Linh Tập** (Lý Tế Xuyên, ca. 1329): exclusion by editorial principle. The collection records only upright deities who receive cult; demons have no place in it.

The boundary between the marvellous-tale corpus and the official-history corpus is sharp here, and the old historians drew it themselves.

## Geography: _Four Place Names, Three of Them Lost_

The Ngư Tinh tale is built as an etiological myth: it exists to answer where certain place names came from. The irony is that most of the places it explains have since gone missing.

**Bạch Long Vĩ (白龍尾)**, "White Dragon's Tail", is the one name still attached to a physical place: an island in the middle of the Gulf of Tonkin, 110 km from Hải Phòng and 120 km from Hải Nam island, 2.5 to 4 km² depending on the tide, rising to 58 m, today an island district of the city of Hải Phòng (since 1992). But the join between island and legend is recent, and its record is tangled:

- Before the 20th century the island was called "Vô Thủy" (the waterless island); in Chinese it was known as Dạ Oanh Đảo (夜莺岛, Nightingale Island).
- The name "Bạch Long Vĩ" was officially assigned to the island under Emperor Bảo Đại in 1937.
- Before that, the mythological Bạch Long Vĩ was generally understood to be a peninsula at Phòng Thành (Guangxi, China).
- In 1957 China transferred the island to the Democratic Republic of Vietnam; in 1992 it became an island district of Hải Phòng.

The dating of the toponym is therefore a genuinely contested point, and two things must be kept apart: the island is Vietnamese territory today, inhabited and administered as a district of Hải Phòng; whether that island is the "tail" of the 14th-century tale is a separate question, and the present evidence points to a 20th-century attachment.

**Cẩu Đầu Sơn (狗頭山)** and **Mạn Cầu Thủy / Cẩu Đầu Thủy (曼求水 / 狗頭水)** have never been located. Nguyễn Hữu Vinh's commentary (2015) says it plainly: "Today we do not know where these place names are." **Phật Đào Cảng (佛陶港)** is likewise unlocated. A story composed to explain four names now has three names no one can point to on a map.

## Motifs and Symbols: _Iron, Dragon, Fish_

**The red-hot iron block.** The deciding weapon of the battle is no enchanted sword but a lump of hot metal. Đông Sơn culture (roughly 1000 BCE to 100 CE) was a bronze civilization; iron arrived in its final phase. Keith Taylor (_The Birth of Vietnam_, 1983, pp. 1-41) reads the Lạc lords of this story layer against the chiefs of the Đông Sơn age, the age of bronze drums engraved with warriors standing on boats. The iron-kills-the-monster motif recurs in the Thánh Gióng legend: iron horse, iron rod, iron armor. In this layer of myth, iron is what lets people master the thing that strength alone cannot.

**Dragon against fish.** Lạc Long Quân is of the dragon line, the emblem of ordered rain and water; Ngư Tinh is the destructive power of the same element. After the battle, the severed fish tail takes the name Bạch Long Vĩ, a dragon's tail. Trần Đình Hoành (commentary, 2015) reads the detail this way: the fish spirit's cut tail becomes the tail of a White Dragon, promoted a rank, from fish to dragon, which likely reflects the Vietnamese tradition of respect for the dead. The defeated enemy is not erased; it is absorbed into the victor's own name.

**Cross-cultural motifs.** The tale indexes cleanly against the Thompson Motif-Index: A955 (islands from a monster's corpse), A901.1 (topography from a divine battle), K714 (victim tricked into opening its mouth), B16 (devastating sea beast), A531 (culture hero slays monster). The kill by hot object thrown into the mouth has close parallels in Philippine myth, where heroes feed sea monsters heated stones or metal. On the widest frame, the hero-against-sea-monster pattern runs through Perseus and Cetus, Thor and Jörmungandr, Marduk and Tiamat. The underlying concept of **tinh** (精), the long-lived creature that absorbs numinous energy and turns spirit, is the jing-guai (精怪) frame borrowed from the Chinese literary world and shared by all three demons of the trio.

**The bồ lao and the bell mallet.** An article in Heritage, the Vietnam Airlines magazine ("Mythical Creatures"), records a craft tradition of casting the bồ lao, a sea dragon, on bells and shaping the striking mallet like Ngư Tinh, so the bell rings out like a bồ lao crying at the thing it fears. This is journalism, not verified scholarship; it is recorded here as a piece of the figure's modern reception (see Notes for Use).

## The Trio Structure: _Three Demons, Three Ecologies_

Ngư Tinh is the **opening** link of the demon-slaying trio in the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_. In Vũ Quỳnh's 1492 sequence, Ngư Tinh is tale 2 (immediately after the Hồng Bàng clan tale), Hồ Tinh tale 3, Mộc Tinh tale 4. The sequence moves from the sea to the delta to the forested hills.

| Demon | Chinese | Ecology | Territory | Method of destruction | Outcome |
|-------|---------|---------|-----------|----------------------|---------|
| Ngư Tinh | 魚精 | Sea | The East Sea | Red-hot iron block | Dies; corpse becomes islands and mountains |
| Hồ Tinh | 狐精 | Delta, lakes | Long Biên / West Lake | Water armies flood the den | Dies; den becomes West Lake |
| Mộc Tinh | 木精 | Forested hills | Phong Châu | Arts, then stratagem | Survives into the Đinh dynasty |

Nguyễn Đổng Chi (_Lược khảo về thần thoại Việt Nam_, 1956, chapter VII) proposed the systematic reading: the three demons stand for the threats of the three ecologies the ancient Việt had to live with, and the Dragon Lord's victories reflect "la lutte des hommes primitifs face à la nature encore mal domptée", early people's struggle with a nature not yet tamed. Within that frame, Ngư Tinh's opening position means the menace of the sea is dealt with first. This matches Taylor's reading of the whole cycle (1983), in which sovereign power comes from the sea: the lord who belongs to the water fights his first battle on his own element, then moves inland. The program is one of clearing the land so the people can settle, not conquest; modern retellings tend to inflate this battle into a naval epic, but the original has one boat, one block of iron, and one sea that needed to be made safe.

The three outcomes are asymmetric. Ngư Tinh dies outright, its carcass becoming place names. Hồ Tinh dies, its den becoming West Lake. Mộc Tinh is only "slightly subdued" in the first battle (the Chinese text names Kinh Dương Vương as the fighter there; see the Mộc Tinh entry) and survives into the reign of Đinh Tiên Hoàng, when the sorcerer Văn Du Tường finally destroys it. Mapped onto the ecologies: the sea and the delta could be mastered; the forest held out.

See the Hồ Tinh and Mộc Tinh entries for the two later links, and the Lạc Long Quân entry for the Dragon Lord's full career.

## Variants: _From Iron Block to Trident_

**Tân đính Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái** (Đoàn Vĩnh Phúc, 1554, manuscript A.2914) expands the tale considerably: Ngư Tinh is no longer one creature but a host of water monsters, among them a crocodile-like beast with a red tail and pink scales that ate people and a human-faced fish that swallowed children, some weighing "tens of thousands of bells"; the battle lasts three days and nights, with sea gods helping to still the waves and cut off escape. The monsters' range is widened to the area of present-day Guangdong and the Gulf of Tonkin.

**Children's retellings** (Giáo dục and Kim Đồng imprints among others) usually swap the iron block for a trident, drop the place-name explanations, and keep only a beheading.

**Modern web retellings** add layers found in no old text: nine tails for Ngư Tinh (most likely contamination from Hồ Tinh's nine-tailed motif), a flattened body and shield-shaped head; the demon absorbing the numinous energy of the Pillar-of-Heaven god; and a philosophical deathbed exchange in which the monster argues that the sea is its territory and humans the intruders. These details are contemporary inventions.

## Modern _Scholarship_

**The folk-memory reading.** Nguyễn Đổng Chi (1956) reads the trio as set out under the Trio Structure above: myth reflecting the real social conditions of early settlers. Lê Văn Hảo, reviewing the book in BEFEO (Tome 52, N.1, 1964, pp. 253-256), praised the work but objected that Nguyễn Đổng Chi separated mythological deities too sharply from the deities of living folk religion, passing over the agrarian ritual base connecting them.

**Position within the mythic layers.** Cao Huy Đỉnh (_Tìm hiểu tiến trình văn học dân gian Việt Nam_, 1974) analyzed the Hùng Vương legend cycle as turning on two struggles: against floods (Sơn Tinh and Thủy Tinh) and against invasion (Thánh Gióng). The Dragon Lord's three demon battles belong to an older layer than either: the conquest of nature before the state takes form, the world-clearing stage before the nation-founding stage.

**Literary reworking.** Đinh Gia Khánh, the tale's translator and the first scholar to write a monograph on the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_, judged that its compilers "often used a creative pen to raise the literary quality of the tales", to the point of moving beyond mere recording. The Ngư Tinh we read today has passed through literati hands; it is no longer raw folklore.

**The revisionist reading.** Tạ Chí Đại Trường (_Những bài dã sử Việt_, 2009) works by seeking "the face formed by time" rather than "the face blurred by time": tracing the layers a legend accumulates in transmission. Liam Kelley (Journal of Vietnamese Studies 7.2, 2012, pp. 87-130) goes furthest, treating the whole Hồng Bàng cycle, demon battles included, as an invented tradition of 15th-century literati; Tạ Chí Đại Trường replied in the same issue (pp. 139-162). The full shape of that debate, and what remains open in it, is laid out in the Mộc Tinh entry under the Sinicization Paradox; what matters here is that the question of the text's date does not touch what the tale's own details assert about the Việt as a distinct people, with their own land, their own sea, their own troubles and their own ways of ending them.

**What remains open.** For Ngư Tinh specifically, two questions stay unresolved: whether the tale encodes a Đông Sơn-era memory of working the sea or is a literati composition using the Chinese spirit-creature frame; and which place the toponym Bạch Long Vĩ originally named. Each side has its data; neither has the evidence to close the file.

## Living _Legacy_

The relic assigned to the tale is Bạch Long Vĩ island, Hải Phòng's island district in the middle of the Gulf of Tonkin, with residents, a harbor, and a radar station. But as the Geography section sets out, the join between island and tale is a 20th-century affair, and the other three place names are lost.

In popular culture, Ngư Tinh appears in games, comics, and film as part of the classic trio with Hồ Tinh and Mộc Tinh, and it is usually the least developed of the three. The reason is geographic: West Lake sits in the middle of Hanoi, where people pass it every day; Bạch Long Vĩ lies far offshore, and its tie to the legend is loose. A monster attached to a place people touch daily lives on in the imagination; a monster far out at sea lives in books.

## Notes _for Use_

**Body shape.** The original: many legs, shaped like a centipede (多足，似蜈蚣形). "Snake-bodied" is the rendering of some modern translations; nine tails, flattened body, and spiral teeth are additions of web retellings.

**"Cut into three pieces."** A popular shorthand. The original's sequence: tail severed, head escapes and is killed separately, body drifts away.

**Bạch Long Vĩ.** The present island has carried the name only since 1937 (under Bảo Đại); before that the island was called Vô Thủy, and the mythological toponym was generally understood as a peninsula at Phòng Thành (Guangxi). Do not use the tale to date the island's name, or the island to date the tale.

**Long Quân / Long Vương.** The original alternates the two titles for the same figure; when quoting, keep the inconsistency rather than silently correcting the text.

**Three-day battle, host of monsters.** Only in the _Tân đính_ (1554), the 16th-century expansion; do not cite these details as the original LNCQ.

**The trident.** Belongs to children's retellings; the original is a red-hot iron block (鐵塊通紅火熱).

**The bồ lao and the Ngư Tinh mallet.** Journalistic source (Heritage, Vietnam Airlines), not verified scholarship; attribute it if used.

**Naming.** In English prose this encyclopedia uses the East Sea for 東海 / Biển Đông throughout.

**Thi viện misattribution.** Several websites (Thi viện among them) misattribute the _Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái_ to Nguyễn Dữ. The LNCQ was compiled by Trần Thế Pháp and edited by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú; Nguyễn Dữ wrote the _Truyền Kỳ Mạn Lục_.
