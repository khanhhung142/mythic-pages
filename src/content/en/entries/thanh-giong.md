## The Story

In the reign of the sixth Hùng King, the king of Ân in the north, claiming that the southern country had failed to pay him homage, sent his armies across the border. Where the invaders passed, they burned houses and carried off the harvests. The king was troubled, for his soldiers could not hold them.

In those days, in the village of Phù Đổng in the region of Vũ Ninh, there lived an aging woman, alone, hardworking, known among her neighbors for her kindness. One morning she went out to her field and found, pressed into the earth, a footprint larger than any human foot she had ever seen. Curious, she set her own foot inside it to measure the difference. She returned home, and she was with child. Twelve months later she gave birth to a boy with a bright, handsome face.

She was overjoyed. But the boy reached the age of three without speaking a word, without laughing, without sitting up. Wherever she laid him, there he stayed. The villagers looked on and pitied her.

Then the Ân army reached the foot of Trâu Mountain, and its strength grew by the day. The king sent heralds through every village, calling for a man of talent to come forward and save the country.

The herald's call rang into every house. The boy lying on his bamboo cot suddenly spoke: "Mother, go out and invite the king's envoy in."

His mother stood frozen. In three years her son had not said one word. Half in joy and half in fear, she went out and brought the envoy in. The envoy looked at the three-year-old on the cot, and before he could ask anything, the child spoke first: "Go back and tell the king to forge me an iron horse, an iron rod, and a suit of iron armor. I will smash this enemy."

The envoy hurried back to the court, astonished. The king set every smith in the country to work, day and night, on the iron horse, the iron rod, the iron armor.

From the day he met the envoy, the boy grew as fast as the wind fills a sail. No amount of rice could fill him. A shirt was barely sewn before it split at the seams. His mother alone could not feed him, so the neighbors came: one house brought rice, another brought eggplant, another wove cloth for his clothes. Seven baskets of rice, three baskets of eggplant, and the boy ate it all. The whole village fed him, and every person in it hoped he would destroy the invaders.

The enemy was now at the foot of Trâu Mountain. The country stood at the edge of ruin. At that moment the envoy arrived with the iron horse, the iron rod, and the iron armor.

The boy rose from his cot, stretched his arms, and in that single stretch became a warrior more than ten feet tall. He put on the armor, took up the rod, and leapt onto the horse. The iron horse gave a long cry, breathed fire, and galloped straight for the enemy.

The warrior met the invaders head-on and cut them down rank after rank. In the middle of the battle, the iron rod snapped. He did not pause. He tore whole clumps of bamboo from the roadside and swung them like clubs into the enemy lines. The invading army broke and ran, trampling one another, and the survivors fell to their knees and begged to surrender.

With the enemy destroyed, the warrior did not return to court. Alone on his horse he rode to the foot of Sóc Mountain, climbed to the summit, took off his iron armor and laid it down, and then, horse and rider together, rose slowly into the sky. He never came back for his reward. No one ever saw him again.

The king, remembering what he owed, gave him the title Phù Đổng Thiên Vương, Heavenly King of Phù Đổng, and raised a temple to him in his home village.

People afterward said that the chains of ponds running through the region are the hoofprints his iron horse left in the earth. The yellow-stemmed bamboo of those parts is golden because the horse's fire scorched it. One village that burned in his passing is still called Làng Cháy, the Burned Village. And the child who did not speak for three years is known by the familiar name the people gave him: Ông Gióng, Thánh Gióng.

## The Tale in _Lĩnh Nam chích quái_

The story above is the fully developed folk version, familiar to Vietnamese readers today through Nguyễn Đổng Chi's retelling in *Kho tàng truyện cổ tích Việt Nam* and through the school curriculum. The earliest text to give the complete plot, the "Tale of Đổng Thiên Vương" in *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* (compiled by Trần Thế Pháp in the late 14th century, revised by Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú in 1492-1493), tells it differently at several points.

In *Lĩnh Nam chích quái*: during the time of the Hùng kings, the Ân ruler uses the south's failure to pay homage as a pretext for invasion. Long Quân announces in a dream that a savior will appear. A wealthy man of Phù Đổng village in Vũ Ninh district has a son, born on the seventh day of the first month, who does not speak at the age of three. Hearing the royal herald, the boy speaks and asks for "an iron horse eighteen thước tall, an iron sword seven thước long, and an iron helmet." He stretches to a height of more than ten trượng, destroys the Ân army at Trâu Mountain, kills the Ân king, then rides to An Việt hamlet at Sóc Mountain, sheds his clothing, and ascends. The Hùng King grants him the title Phù Đổng Thiên Vương and a thousand mẫu of land for his cult.

Set the two versions side by side and the differences stand out. The *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* text has **no** footprint conception, no twelve-month pregnancy, no iron rod, no iron armor, and no bamboo torn up when a weapon breaks. Those motifs entered later, through village spirit-records, vernacular verse narratives, and folk retellings of the 16th and 17th centuries. Nor does *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* number the Hùng King; the Tale of the Việt Well in the same collection places its events under the third Hùng King, so even the era was unsettled. The number six was fixed later by *Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư*.

## Textual History: From Earth God to _Hero_

The written record of Thánh Gióng runs through Hán and Nôm texts from the 14th to the 19th century, and the content shifts visibly with each compilation.

The earliest layer is not about a warrior at all. *Việt điện u linh tập* (Lý Tế Xuyên, 1329) records the deity under the full title "Xung Thiên Dũng Liệt Chiêu Ứng Uy Tín Đại Vương", citing the lost *Báo Cực truyện*, with the key sentence: "Tradition holds that the King was the earth god of Kiến Sơ Pagoda, reincarnated." In this stratum the Phù Đổng deity is a nature god of trees and stones attached to a pagoda, not a general. *An Nam chí lược* by Lê Tắc (c. 1335) already mentions "Xung Thiên Thần Vương", which places the title and the story before 1285. The negative evidence matters too: *Việt sử lược* (anonymous, late 14th century) has no Gióng passage in its Hùng King section. The Phù Đổng deity entered official historiography only with Ngô Sĩ Liên.

*Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư* (1479) placed the tale in the Outer Annals under the Hồng Bàng section, fixed the date to the sixth Hùng King, and stripped the marvels: no Long Quân, no superhuman dimensions, no helmet, just "one sword, one horse." Most striking of all, the Toàn thư never names the "Ân invaders"; it writes only that "there was urgent news within the country." Ngô Thì Sĩ, in *Đại Việt sử ký tiền biên*, remarked on the passage: "The Phù Đổng story says nothing of the Ân army, nothing of casting an iron horse, nothing of stretching into a giant; clearly it was already suspected of being fantastical." Tô Như (*Tia Sáng*, 2021) adds a further political reading: Ngô Sĩ Liên may have reshaped the image, one sword and one horse, to evoke Lê Thái Tổ and the sword Thuận Thiên.

A separate branch ties Gióng to Buddhism. *Việt điện u linh tục bổ* (Nguyễn Văn Chất, 15th century) preserves the "Record of Sóc Thiên Vương" in two variants: a folk account of a child hero who answers the king's dream-summons, destroys the invaders, and ascends at Vệ Linh Mountain; and an account drawn from *Thiền uyển tập anh*, in which Vaiśravaṇa, one of the Four Heavenly Kings of Buddhism, appears in a dream to the master Khuông Việt in the time of Lê Đại Hành and aids the defeat of the Song army in 981. *Thiền uyển tập anh* itself never identifies Vaiśravaṇa with Phù Đổng. The identification begins with Nguyễn Văn Chất, and when Đoàn Vĩnh Phúc carried it into the 16th-century *Lĩnh Nam chích quái*, his sentence opens with the word "perhaps": the compiler himself marked it as conjecture.

The Nguyễn-era texts keep the Toàn thư frame. *Khâm định Việt sử thông giám cương mục* (1856-1884) repeats the account while flagging doubts about the dating. *Đại Nam nhất thống chí*, volume 19, records the temple of Đổng Xung Thiên Thần Vương at Phù Đổng. Emperor Tự Đức composed a poem, "Phù Đổng nhi" (The Child of Phù Đổng), whose woodblocks survive.

## Modern Scholarship: The Layers of _Belief_

Scholars read the Gióng figure as a construction of several strata, with the resistance hero as the most recent. Three studies built this framework.

**Cao Huy Đỉnh**, in *Người anh hùng làng Dóng* (1969, posthumously awarded the Hồ Chí Minh Prize), remains the foundational work. Drawing on fieldwork in Phù Đổng village and the smithing villages of the Bắc Ninh region, he proposed that Gióng was originally a god of thunder, storm, and rain in wet-rice agrarian religion, resting his case on the phonetic chain đổng, dóng, dông (the last meaning "storm") and on the folk figure of Ông Đổng that he recorded in the field (printed in *Hùng Vương dựng nước*, vol. I, 1972, p. 110): "Ông Đổng is impossibly tall... His voice rolls out as thunder. His eyes flash lightning. His breath pours out black clouds, gales, and rainstorms." In this frame, the giant footprint is a thunder god's print; the three years of silence are a nature deity's dormancy before the storm season; the scorched bamboo of the Burned Village marks where lightning struck. Cao Huy Đỉnh also read the motifs of the "hollow horse", the "armor open at the back", and the "broken rod" as traces of two attitudes running side by side in the early communities: disdain for the itinerant smiths who worked iron, and awe at what the new metal could do. The legend, in his reading, belongs precisely to the moment of metallurgical transition in Đông Sơn culture.

**Trần Quốc Vượng** extended the argument in "Căn bản triết lý người anh hùng Phù Đổng và hội Gióng" (in *Lễ hội Thánh Gióng*, 2009, pp. 435-438). He wrote that "the Gióng myth was in its origin a myth of the Sun, and the Gióng Festival was in its origin an agrarian rite" for timely rain and sun. The Đông Sơn solar symbol, he argued, migrated from the star on the bronze drum face to the white horse in the temple and the iron horse of the tale: a fire-breathing horse that runs from east (Châu Cầu, Phả Lại, Lục Đầu) to west (Sóc Sơn), tracing the sun's apparent path. The festival's "three winning rounds, three losing rounds" answer the sun's turning; the 28 girl generals of the Ân correspond to the Twenty-Eight Lunar Mansions, the stars of night, Yin against Gióng's Yang; the "red-robed village" and "black-robed village" are day and night. He also connected the pampas-grass plumes worn by the buffalo boys of Hội Xá to the plume-wearing figures on Đông Sơn drums, a link first noticed by Lê Thị Nhâm Tuyết.

**Tạ Chí Đại Trường**, in "Thần Phù Đổng" (*Đường Mới*, Paris, no. 5/1986; reprinted in *Những bài dã sử Việt*, 1996, pp. 75-99), drew the clearest evolutionary map, in six steps: (i) a tree-and-stone earth deity at Phù Đổng, whose name may echo a Tày-Thái form "Pù Đống", meaning mountain; (ii) in the 9th century, the Chan master Vô Ngôn Thông founded Kiến Sơ Pagoda in 820 and had to come to terms with the local cult; (iii) the Lý court granted the title Xung Thiên Thần Vương and built a second shrine near West Lake; (iv) the Trần court added honorifics three times, each during the Mongol wars, turning a nature god into a resistance hero; (v) in the early 14th century the Phù Đổng deity merged with the Sóc Thiên Vương and Vaiśravaṇa figure of Vệ Linh Mountain; (vi) in the late 14th century *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* completed the hagiography with a Hùng-era date. His conclusion: "Phù Đổng Thiên Vương, or Thánh Dóng, appeared only about seven centuries ago; he was not born in the reign of the sixth Hùng King." He added, in the same essay, that "the Phù Đổng deity still keeps the nature-god origins of his beginning." The conclusion remains contested.

Before all three, **Nguyễn Văn Huyên** laid the groundwork with two French-language studies, *Les Fêtes de Phù Đổng* (1938) and *Les chants et les danses d'Ai-Lao aux Fêtes de Phù Đổng* (1941). He was the first to analyze the Gióng Festival as an agrarian fertility rite rather than a reenacted battle, and the first to document the role of the Ải Lao troupe. **Nguyễn Đổng Chi** classified the tale as a "historical folk tale" in *Kho tàng truyện cổ tích Việt Nam*; his retelling is the version printed in all three current Grade 6 literature textbooks. **Nguyễn Chí Bền** (*Hội Gióng ở đền Phù Đổng và đền Sóc*, 2010, p. 202) added the hypothesis that Gióng also carries traces of a patron deity of ironsmiths.

## The Ân Invaders: What Remains _Open_

No archaeological evidence and no contemporary Chinese text attests a war between the Shang (Ân) dynasty and the ancient Việt. The Shang (c. 1600-1046 BCE) had their capital at Anyang in Henan. The oracle bone archive of the Shang kings, one of the largest bodies of early writing anywhere, records no campaign south into what is now northern Vietnam, and the distance of well over a thousand kilometers makes such an expedition implausible on logistics alone. At the height of Shang power, northern Vietnam was in the Đồng Đậu cultural phase: no state, no iron.

Iron appears clearly in the region only in late Đông Sơn contexts, from about the 3rd-2nd century BCE. The Đường Mây excavations at Cổ Loa (1969-1983) yielded 11 iron artifacts against 58 bronze ones. Horses appear on Đông Sơn bronzes no earlier than the 2nd century BCE, according to Nguyễn Việt of the Center for Southeast Asian Prehistory, the type case being the Sangeang drum with its Central Asian costumes, elephants, and horses, reflecting the influence of the Dian culture of Yunnan. An iron horse, iron armor, and an iron rod therefore reflect the mental world of the late metal age, more than a thousand years after the Shang.

The tale says the invaders were Ân; the sources confirm no such enemy. The question is open, and the main readings are these:

(i) **Cao Huy Đỉnh and Trần Quốc Vượng**: the "Ân invaders" are a late historical layer laid over an older agrarian myth; the name "Ân" stands for northern invasion in general rather than any specific dynasty.

(ii) **Nguyễn Việt**: the legend fuses the bamboo-deity tradition of the Yelang people of Guizhou, carried south by migration in the 3rd century BCE, with the native Việt-Mường giant figure Ông Đổng / Ông Đùng; the story crystallized in the 3rd-2nd century BCE as the Âu Lạc confederation formed. He also points to an archaeological asymmetry: Đông Sơn sites from the 3rd century BCE cluster densely around Phù Đổng (Trung Mầu, Dương Nội, Đa Tốn), while around Sóc Mountain, where the tale has Gióng ascend, late prehistoric remains are nearly absent. On this basis he argues that Sóc Mountain was attached to the legend late, around the 14th-15th century.

(iii) **Tô Như** (*Tia Sáng*, 2021): stresses the political dimension of Ngô Sĩ Liên's editing, since it was the official historian himself who removed the "Ân invaders" from the record.

What no side disputes: the Hùng Kings Era Research Program (1969-1972), led by Phạm Huy Thông with the participation of Đinh Xuân Lâm, Phan Huy Lê, Hà Văn Tấn, and Trần Quốc Vượng, published as *Thời đại Hùng Vương* (1973), concluded that the Hùng era is real as an archaeological sequence, through the Phùng Nguyên, Đồng Đậu, Gò Mun, and Đông Sơn cultures, but that no individual figure of the legends can be documented. The legends are read as "historical kernels wrapped in myth": collective memory of the conflicts of the state-forming age, with each later era adding a layer. Saying "unverified" here does not shrink the figure. It is precisely because the image could carry the invasion memories of many generations that a village god became a saint of the whole country.

## The Four _Immortals_

Thánh Gióng stands second among the modern Tứ Bất Tử, the Four Immortals: Tản Viên Sơn Thánh, Phù Đổng Thiên Vương, Chử Đồng Tử, and Liễu Hạnh Công Chúa, answering to protection from natural disaster, defense against invasion, economic life, and spiritual life.

The term "Tứ bất tử" appears as early as Nguyễn Trãi's *Dư địa chí* in the 15th century. The 17th-century commentary of Nguyễn Tông Quai gives an early list with the monk Từ Đạo Hạnh in fourth place: "Tản Viên Đại Vương went from the sea to the mountain; Phù Đổng Thiên Vương rode his horse into the sky; the young Chử with staff and hat rose to heaven; at Ninh Sơn, Từ Đạo Hạnh pressed his mark into stone to be reborn. These are the Four Immortals of An Nam." Other variants put Nguyễn Minh Không (Kiều Oánh Mậu, *Tiên phả dịch lục*) or Không Lộ in the fourth seat. Liễu Hạnh joined the list only in its modern form, fixed through Nguyễn Văn Huyên's 1944 study and the 1990 volume *Tứ bất tử* by Vũ Ngọc Khánh and Phạm Minh Thảo. Chu Xuân Giao (*Tạp chí Nghiên cứu Tôn giáo*, no. 1/2014) adds a caution: *Hội chân biên* (1847), often cited as the ancient source of the list, contains no such list of four; the fixed set is a modern compilation. In every variant, Gióng keeps his seat. Two of his companions on the list have their own entries: see **Chử Đồng Tử** and **Tiên Dung**.

## Temples and _Worship_

**The Phù Đổng Temple complex** (Phù Đổng commune, Gia Lâm, Hanoi) was designated a Special National Monument in 2013. Per the Department of Cultural Heritage, it comprises eight component sites over 60,343.7 m²: the Upper Temple (the main shrine), the Lower Temple dedicated to Gióng's mother, Miếu Ban, the Hạ Mã pavilion, the Cố Viên garden, the Giá Ngự platform, the Đống Đàm chess ground, and the Soi Bia sandbank. The temple holds 37 royal investiture decrees from the Lê Trung Hưng, Tây Sơn, and Nguyễn periods, two stone dragons carved in 1705, and a stele of 1660. Tradition ties the founding of the shrine to Lý Thái Tổ in 1010, the year the capital moved to Thăng Long, but no contemporary document confirms it.

**The Sóc Temple complex** on Vệ Linh Mountain (Phù Linh commune, Sóc Sơn) was designated a National Monument in 1962. It comprises the Trình Temple, the Mother Temple, Non Nước Pagoda, Đại Bi Pagoda, the Upper Temple, the Stacked Stone, and a modern monumental statue. Tradition holds the shrine dates from 980, when Lê Đại Hành stopped to pray on his march against the Song and, after his victory, had a statue carved and a temple raised.

The chain of investitures, assembled from the *Toàn thư*, *Đại Nam nhất thống chí*, and the Nguyễn woodblock archive: Lý Thái Tổ conferred Xung Thiên Thần Vương; Trần Nhân Tông added Dũng Liệt (1285) and Chiêu Ứng (1288), both in years of the Mongol wars; Trần Anh Tông added Uy Tín (1313); Lê Thái Tổ reconfirmed Xung Thiên Thần Vương; the Nguyễn emperors from Minh Mạng to Khải Định continued the offerings and honorifics. The rhythm of these grants, clustering in wartime, is the core evidence for Tạ Chí Đại Trường's argument: each time the country was invaded, the court raised the god of Phù Đổng another step.

## The Gióng Festival and _UNESCO_

**The festival at Phù Đổng Temple** runs from the 7th to the 9th day of the fourth lunar month, with the main day on the 9th. The sequence: offerings to the Saint; a water procession from the well of the Mother Temple, carrying the sense of praying for rain and of quenching forged weapons; a flag procession from the Mother Temple to the Upper Temple; the scouting of the roads; the review of the generals; then the two battle performances at Đống Đàm and Soi Bia. The battlefield is three reed mats, each bearing an upturned bowl on a sheet of white paper: the bowl stands for the hills, the paper for the clouds. The Hiệu Cờ, the Flag Master who stands for Gióng himself, dances a 2.45-meter silk banner through the strokes of the character "Lệnh" (Command); the one forbidden fault is letting the flag wrap its staff. After each battle the crowd rushes in to tear off pieces of the mats, held to bring luck.

The cast: five Hiệu masters (Flag, Drum, Gong, Central Command, Small Drum), some 120 Phù Giá escorts, the Ải Lao troupe with its "tiger hunt", and 28 girls aged 9 to 13 playing the Ân generals, the "female enemy generals" that Trần Quốc Vượng read as Yin set against Gióng's Yang. At the close, the 28 girl generals dismount from their palanquins and kneel in surrender; two commanders are symbolically "executed", and the rest are pardoned. This ending in clemency is one of the features the UNESCO dossier singles out: the battle ends in mercy.

**The festival at Sóc Temple** runs from the 6th to the 8th day of the first lunar month, opening on the 6th, with the procession of bamboo-flower wands (standing for the yellow bamboo Gióng swung as a weapon), the elephant procession, the betel and areca procession, and the "Cầu Húc" sun-ball procession, ending in the scramble for bamboo flowers as tokens of luck.

UNESCO inscribed the "Gióng Festival of Phù Đổng and Sóc temples" on the Representative List of the Intangible Cultural Heritage of Humanity at 6:20 pm on November 16, 2010, in Nairobi, Kenya, at the fifth session of the Intergovernmental Committee of the 2003 Convention. The dossier was prepared by the Vietnam Institute of Culture and Arts Studies (VICAS), submitted on August 31, 2009, with Ngô Đức Thịnh among the reviewing scholars. The UNESCO evaluation called the festival "a museum of Vietnamese culture, preserving many sedimented layers of culture and belief."

## Gióng Among the World's _Mythic_ Heroes

The wonder-child who grows at impossible speed appears in many traditions: Heracles strangles serpents in his cradle, Cú Chulainn enters his battle frenzy at seven, Nezha is born speaking, Momotarō shoots up to strength from the moment he is found in a peach. Gióng carries the standard marks of the type: miraculous conception, prodigious signs in infancy, great deeds in childhood, a supernatural end. Three things set him apart.

First, the three years of silence broken at the moment of national danger. Heracles, Cú Chulainn, and Nezha display their power from the start; Gióng lies still until the herald's voice reaches his house. In Cao Huy Đỉnh's reading, this is the dormancy of a nature god before the storm season.

Second, the community raises him. The seven baskets of rice and three of eggplant come from the village; the villagers sew his clothes; the bamboo he swings grows from the village earth. Heracles has the gods behind him, Cú Chulainn has his teacher Scáthach, Nezha has a general of heaven for a father. Gióng has the whole village, and not one of the rice-bringers is named. Cao Huy Đỉnh (1969) placed this at the center of his analysis, calling Gióng a "historical giant" as distinct from purely cosmogonic giants of the Thần Trụ Trời type: the individual Gióng is collective strength given a single body.

Third, Gióng carries no personal tragedy. Heracles kills his wife and children in madness; Cú Chulainn dies alone after turning on his own; Nezha carves the flesh from his bones to repay his father. Gióng defeats the enemy, lays down his armor, and ascends, asking nothing. Among ascension motifs he is closer to Elijah (2 Kings 2:11) and to the Yellow Emperor than to Heracles: there is no physical death. But where Elijah leaves his prophetic mantle to a disciple, Gióng leaves marks on the land itself: hoofprint ponds, golden bamboo, the Burned Village. A hero departs and the terrain keeps the shape of his passing; this is how Vietnamese legend anchors myth to geography.

## A Living _Legacy_

Gióng has been a central figure of patriotic letters across eras. In the 19th century, Cao Bá Quát wrote a Hán poem on visiting the Phù Đổng temple and a couplet for the shrine, recorded by Hoa Bằng (*Tạp chí Văn học*, no. 2/1972, pp. 61-64): "Crushing the enemy, his one regret was coming at three years old, too late; / riding the clouds, his one resentment was that the nine heavens hung too low." Tố Hữu, in *Theo chân Bác* (1970, in the collection *Ra trận*, 1972), turned the stretching child into an image of the nation's awakened youth: "What strength of youth! The Phù Đổng boy of old / stretched once and rose a thousand-weight strong / rode the iron horse breathing fire / tore up the village bamboo and drove out the Ân." Nguyễn Đình Thi analyzed Gióng in his 1944 essay *Sức sống của dân Việt Nam trong ca dao và cổ tích*; he later confirmed that the detail of "Gióng bathing in West Lake" was his own invention, not a folk motif.

In the visual arts, the Đông Hồ woodblock tradition includes the print *Phù Đổng Thiên Vương Defeating the Ân Army* on dó paper; Nguyễn Tư Nghiêm painted a well-known Thánh Gióng series, exhibited for the 70th anniversary of the Điện Biên Phủ victory (2024). For the millennium of Thăng Long, a bronze statue of Thánh Gióng, about 85 tonnes and 11.7 meters, by the sculptor Nguyễn Kim Xuân, was raised on the summit of Đá Chồng peak in Sóc Sơn and inaugurated on October 5, 2010. The animated film *Chuyện ông Gióng* (The Story of Gióng), directed by Ngô Mạnh Lân, won the Golden Dove at the Leipzig film festival in 1971, one of the earliest international awards for Vietnamese animation.

In education, the tale in Nguyễn Đổng Chi's retelling opens the legend unit of Grade 6 literature in all three current textbook series. "Strong as Phù Đổng" and "grows as fast as Thánh Gióng" are everyday Vietnamese idioms. The festival opens on schedule every year at both temples, and at each festival the battles of Đống Đàm and Soi Bia are fought again on three reed mats.

## Notes for _Use_

- The footprint conception, the twelve-month pregnancy, the iron rod, the iron armor, and the bamboo weapon are **absent from Lĩnh Nam chích quái**; they are 16th-17th century accretions. When citing "the old text", distinguish the Lĩnh Nam chích quái version from the fully developed folk version of the Nguyễn Đổng Chi type.
- *Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư* never mentions the "Ân invaders"; the sixth Hùng King date is Ngô Sĩ Liên's assignment, not independent evidence.
- "Gióng bathing in West Lake" is Nguyễn Đình Thi's invention, by his own confirmation; it caused controversy when it appeared in a Grade 5 textbook (2015). Do not cite it as folklore.
- The couplet "Every young boy dreams of an iron horse / Every river longs to become the Bạch Đằng", commonly attributed to Chế Lan Viên, has not been verified against his published collections.
- The tradition that Lý Thái Tổ founded the Phù Đổng shrine in 1010 is oral; no contemporary document survives. The dates and texts of the 37 investiture decrees held at the temple have not been fully published.
- Many websites (including Thi viện) attribute *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* to Nguyễn Dữ; this is an error. The traditional attribution is Trần Thế Pháp.
