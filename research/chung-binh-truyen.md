# Chưng Bính Truyện (蒸餅傳) — The Origin Legend of *Bánh Chưng* and *Bánh Giầy* in *Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái*: A Research Report

## TL;DR
- **Chưng Bính truyện** (蒸餅傳, "The Tale of the Steamed Cake") is the eighth tale of Quyển 1 of *Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái* (LNCQ, 嶺南摭怪), a late-Trần (c. late 14th-c.) collection in literary Chinese conventionally attributed to Trần Thế Pháp and redacted by Vũ Quỳnh (1492) and Kiều Phú (1493); it explains why the eighteenth prince of the sixth Hùng king, Lang Liêu (郎僚), invented the square *bánh chưng* (earth) and round *bánh giầy/薄持餅 bạc trì bính* (heaven) after a divine dream, and was thereby chosen heir.
- The standard reference editions are the parallel 1960 translations — Lê Hữu Mục (Khai Trí, Saigon) and Đinh Gia Khánh & Nguyễn Ngọc San (Văn Hóa/Văn Học, Hà Nội, 137 pp.) — both based on different *truyền bản* (transmission copies); the most authoritative critical edition of the Hán text is in the 1992 *Việt Nam Hán văn tiểu thuyết tùng san* (Trần Khánh Hạo, ed., Học Sinh Thư Cục, Taipei) and the recent collation by Nguyễn Thị Oanh (using bản A.2914 etc., Viện Nghiên cứu Hán Nôm).
- Scholarship is divided: classical Vietnamese readings (Vũ Quỳnh, Đinh Gia Khánh, Cao Huy Đỉnh, Nguyễn Đổng Chi) treat the tale as a genuine reflection of pre-Sinic Vietic rice-civilization values (filial piety, the cosmology of "round heaven, square earth," ancestor worship), while French and Anglophone critical historiography (Henri Maspero, BEFEO 1918; Émile Gaspardone, BEFEO 1934; and Liam C. Kelley, *Journal of Vietnamese Studies* 2012) view the Hùng-king material in LNCQ as a medieval "invented tradition" elaborated under Confucian-classical influence, a thesis sharply contested by Tạ Chí Đại Trường (JVS 2012) and Vietnamese state historiography.

## Details

### 1. Location of the Tale and Original Text

In the textual tradition that goes back to the Vũ Quỳnh / Kiều Phú redaction (1492–1493), Chưng Bính truyện is the **eighth chapter of Quyển 1** of *Lĩnh Nam chích quái liệt truyện* (嶺南摭怪列傳). The 22-tale "Vũ Quỳnh canon" runs: 1 Hồng Bàng thị – 2 Ngư tinh – 3 Hồ tinh – 4 Mộc tinh – 5 Tân lang (Trầu cau) – 6 Nhất Dạ Trạch – 7 Đổng Thiên Vương – **8 Chưng Bính** – 9 Tây qua – 10 Bạch trĩ … (continuing into Quyển 2). This ordering is the one used in the 1960 translations by both Lê Hữu Mục (Saigon) and Đinh Gia Khánh / Nguyễn Ngọc San (Hà Nội), and reproduced by the 1992 Học Sinh Thư Cục edition of *Việt Nam Hán văn tiểu thuyết tùng san* (Trần Khánh Hạo, ed.), based on EFEO holdings.

**Original Hán text (from the standard recension, as preserved at Chinese Text Project / Wikisource 中國哲學書電子化計劃, 嶺南摭怪/蒸餅傳):**

> 雄王既破殷軍之後，國家無事，欲傳於子，乃會官郎，公子二十二人，謂曰：「我欲傳位，有能如我願，欲珍甘美味，歲終薦於先王，以盡孝道，方可傳位。」於是諸子各求水陸奇珍之物（一作異味），不可勝數。惟十八子郎僚母氏單寒微，先已病故，左右寡少，難以應辦。晝夜思想，夢寐不安。夜夢神人告曰：「天地之物所貴於人，無過米。所以養人，人能壯也。食不能厭，他物莫能先。當以糯米作餅，或方或圓，以象天地之形，葉包其外，中藏美味，以寓父母生育之重（一作狀）。」郎僚驚覺，喜曰：「神人助我也。」遵而行之。乃以糯米擇其精白，選用圓完無缺折者，淅之潔靜，以青色葉包裹為方形，置珍甘美味在其中，以象天地包藏萬物焉。煮而熟之，故曰蒸餅。又以糯米炊熟，搗而爛之，捏作圓形以象天，故曰薄持餅。

**Working translation (after Lê Hữu Mục 1960 and Đinh Gia Khánh 1960; my rendering):**

> "After the Hùng king [the sixth] had defeated the Yin army and the country was at peace, he wished to transmit the throne to a son. He summoned the *quan-lang* (royal princes), twenty-two in number, and said: 'I wish to transmit my position. Whoever can fulfil my desire — bringing rare and delicious things at the year's end to offer to the former king, so that I may complete the filial way — to him I shall transmit the throne.' Thereupon the princes each sought rare delicacies from waters and lands beyond counting. Only the eighteenth, Lang Liêu, whose mother had been low-ranked and had already died of illness, with few attendants, found it hard to comply. Day and night anxious, dreams troubled, one night he dreamed of a divine person who told him: 'Among the things of Heaven and Earth treasured by people, none surpasses rice; with it people are nourished and grow strong; one never tires of eating it, and no other thing precedes it. You should make cakes of glutinous rice, some square, some round, to figure forth the shapes of Heaven and Earth; wrap them outside with leaves and conceal sweet delicacies within, to signify the deep care of parents who beget and rear (一作 "the shape" 狀).' Lang Liêu woke startled and rejoiced: 'The divine man has helped me!' Doing as instructed, he chose the whitest, plumpest, unbroken glutinous rice, washed it clean, wrapped it in green leaves into a square shape, and placed sweet delicacies inside, in the image of Heaven and Earth containing the ten-thousand things. He boiled them; this is called 蒸餅 *chưng bính*. He also took glutinous rice, steamed it, pounded it soft, and shaped it into rounds to figure Heaven; this is called 薄持餅 *bạc trì bính*."

The remainder of the tale (continued in all manuscripts) recounts that on the appointed day Lang Liêu offered only these two cakes; the king tasted them, was astonished, asked about them, heard the dream, judged them above all other dishes, and named Lang Liêu first. The Hùng king then transmitted the throne to him, while the twenty-one other princes were dispersed to "*phiên trấn*" (frontier domains), founding villages, hamlets, and "*trang*" — a passage that doubles as an aetiology of the Vietnamese village (lang/làng) name.

### 2. Authorship and Dating

The authorship question has been litigated since the 18th century:

- **Trần Thế Pháp** (style Thức Chi 識之, of Thạch Thất, Sơn Tây, late 14th c.). Lê Quý Đôn, *Kiến văn tiểu lục*, q. IV, lists "*Lĩnh Nam chích quái*, 1 quyển, tương truyền là tác phẩm của Trần Thế Pháp*" ("by tradition the work of Trần Thế Pháp"). Phan Huy Chú in *Lịch triều hiến chương loại chí* (Văn tịch chí) and Đặng Minh Khiêm's *Vịnh sử thi tập* corroborate the tradition. No autograph or 14th-c. manuscript survives.
- **Vũ Quỳnh** (1452/3–1516, *tự* Thủ Bộc, *hiệu* Trạch Ổ, of Mộ Trạch, Đường An), *Đại Việt thông giám* compiler, wrote the standard Preface dated *Nhâm Tý* 1492 (Hồng Đức 23): "Tên họ người viết là ai đều không thấy ghi rõ. Viết ra đầu tiên là những bậc tài cao học rộng ở đời Lý, Trần. Còn những người hiệu đính, tu bổ là các bậc quân tử uyên bác, hiếu cổ ngày nay" — explicitly disclaiming authorship and assigning original drafting to "men of broad talent and learning of the Lý–Trần."
- **Kiều Phú** (b. 1446, of Lạp Hạ / Yên Sơn, Quốc Oai, Sơn Tây; *tiến sĩ* 1475) signed an independent Hậu tự / *bạt* dated Hồng Đức 24 = 1493, redacting more aggressively than Vũ Quỳnh. Phan Huy Ôn, *Thiên Nam lịch triều liệt huyện đăng khoa bị khảo*, q. IV, wrongly named Vũ Quỳnh as author, while Nguyễn Hoàn's *Đại Việt lịch triều đăng khoa lục*, q. 1, fol. 20a, equally wrongly named Kiều Phú; Vũ Quỳnh's own preface refutes both attributions.
- **Đoàn Vĩnh Phúc** (Tú lâm cục, Hàn lâm viện, Mạc dynasty): in the *Hậu bạt* dated *Quang Bảo sơ niên* (1554) he added a third *quyển* (Tục biên) drawn largely from *Việt điện u linh tập*; his colophon also records that Vũ Quỳnh and Kiều Phú had fixed the canon at "two *quyển*, twenty-two tales, beginning with Hồng Bàng and ending with Dạ Xoa" — a key piece of textual evidence.
- Modern philological consensus, following Trần Văn Giáp (*Tìm hiểu kho sách Hán Nôm*) and Đinh Gia Khánh (1960; rev. 1990), holds that LNCQ is **not the work of any single author** but a layered collection: oral material (Lý–Trần) → drafted (attributed to Trần Thế Pháp) → redacted by Vũ Quỳnh and (independently) Kiều Phú in 1492–93 → expanded by Đoàn Vĩnh Phúc (mid-16th c.) → further supplemented in the late 18th c. by Vũ Khâm Lân and others.
- **Liam C. Kelley** ("The Biography of the Hồng Bàng Clan as a Medieval Vietnamese Invented Tradition," *Journal of Vietnamese Studies* 7, 2 (Summer 2012): 87–130) argues that the entire Hùng-king cycle in LNCQ — including by extension the milieu of Chưng Bính truyện — is a 14th–15th-c. Sinicized literary "invented tradition" rather than the residue of an oral pre-Sinitic memory; the rebuttal by Tạ Chí Đại Trường ("Comments on Liam Kelley's…", JVS 7,2: 139–162) accepts the layered character of the text but argues for older folk substrate.

### 3. Manuscript Variants (*dị bản*)

At least **11 transmission copies** (truyền bản) survive. The principal ones, catalogued in Émile Gaspardone, "Bibliographie annamite," *BEFEO* XXXIV (1934): 1–173 (esp. p. 128–9), are held at the Viện Nghiên cứu Hán Nôm (Hanoi) under shelfmarks **A.33, A.749, A.750, A.1200, A.1300, A.1897, A.1920, A.2914**, with additional copies at the Société Asiatique (Paris) and EFEO. They divide into:

- **22-tale "Vũ Quỳnh canon"** (e.g. A.33), with Chưng Bính as I.8;
- **38-tale and 43-tale expanded recensions** (e.g. *Lĩnh Nam chích quái liệt truyện* with mục lục of 38 truyện preserved in fragmentary form, listing Kiều Phú as *tác* and Vũ Quỳnh as *chí* — see the Nôm Foundation digitisation lib.nomfoundation.org/collection/1/volume/820);
- **Lĩnh Nam chích quái ngoại truyện** and **Lĩnh Nam chích quái khảo chánh** (later titles).

Within Chưng Bính truyện itself, the textual variants are minor but worth noting (variant readings preserved in the 1992 Trần Khánh Hạo edition and the Nguyễn Thị Oanh critical apparatus, *Lĩnh Nam chích quái — Khảo luận, Dịch chú, Nguyên bản chữ Hán*, NXB KHXH, recent reprint by Đinh Tị Books):

| Locus | Variant 1 (Vũ Quỳnh / A.33 line) | Variant 2 (alternative reading 一作) | Effect |
|---|---|---|---|
| The princes seek "rare things" | 奇珍之物 *kỳ trân chi vật* | 異味 *dị vị* "rare flavors" | flavour vs. object |
| Symbolism of filling | 父母生育之重 "the weight of parental rearing" | 父母生育之狀 "the form/shape of parental rearing" | abstract vs. iconic |
| Number of princes | 22 (公子二十二人) | "20 sons" (some folk retellings) | folk versions |
| Hero's name | 郎僚 Lang Liêu (LNCQ) | 薛郎僚 Tiết Lang Liêu / Tiết Liêu (popular tradition; Hùng Vương ngọc phả) | onomastic |

A second important branch of transmission is **non-LNCQ**: the *Hùng Vương Ngọc phả* / *Hùng Đồ thập bát diệp thánh vương ngọc phả cổ truyền* (court-authorised 1472, under Lê Thánh Tông; reproduced through the Lê and Nguyễn periods for distribution to Hùng-temple villages), which renames Lang Liêu "Hùng Chiêu Vương Minh Tông hoàng đế" and identifies him as the seventh Hùng king. Henri Maspero ("Études d'histoire d'Annam, IV. Le Royaume de Văn-lang," *BEFEO* 18, 3, 1918: 1, fn 2) noted that an anonymous Vietnamese-language pamphlet, *History of the eighteen reigns of the Hùng kings*, distributed at the annual Hùng-temple ceremony, contained "paraphrased local myths probably dating from the end of the 15th century" — i.e. the same Lê Thánh Tông project.

A third branch is the **modern Vietnamese folktale** (the *kể chuyện dân gian* version retold for children and in school textbooks since the early 20th c., e.g. the version in *Sách giáo khoa Ngữ văn lớp 6*; Wikisource "Sự tích bánh chưng bánh giầy" public-domain text). Its differences from LNCQ are:
- Number of sons "twenty" (not 22) and 18th son consistently called Lang Liêu / Tiết Liêu;
- Mother's death described in pathetic detail;
- The deity in the dream is anonymous and emphasises rice as "the most precious thing";
- The square cake is *bánh chưng* and the round *bánh giầy / dày / dầy*, not *bạc trì bính*;
- The leaves and stuffing are explicitly glossed as "parents enveloping children" — an innovation absent from the Hán original (which mentions parents only obliquely).

### 4. Narrative Analysis

**Plot structure** (LNCQ recension): (a) victorious king after foreign war; (b) succession crisis with 22 candidates; (c) competition framed as a *filial* offering to the *tiên vương* (former king); (d) hero's deficiency (orphan, marginalised mother, no retainers); (e) divine intervention via dream; (f) rice-based invention valorised over "land and sea delicacies"; (g) judgment scene; (h) accession; (i) dispersion of the losers as territorial founders. The pattern is the classic "youngest-and-poorest wins by virtue and divine aid" tale-type (Aarne-Thompson 425/735 environs) inflected by Confucian filial piety (孝 *hiếu*) and rice-civilisation values.

**Symbolic meaning.** The cosmological scheme is the explicit hinge:
- 方 (square) → 地 (earth) → *bánh chưng*
- 圓 (round) → 天 (heaven) → *bánh giầy / 薄持餅*
- 葉包其外 (green leaves outside) + 中藏美味 (savoury filling within) → "天地包藏萬物" (heaven and earth containing the ten-thousand things) and "父母生育之重" (the weight of parental rearing).

Đinh Gia Khánh & Nguyễn Ngọc San (1960; rpt. NXB Văn Học, 1990) point out that this is the standard Chinese **gaitian (蓋天)** cosmology — "trời tròn, đất vuông" — already at least 2,000 years old in classical sources (cf. *Zhoubi suanjing*). The motif is not narrowly Vietnamese; what is distinctive is its embedding in a *rice* / *nếp* / *lá dong* matrix and its narrative subordination to *hiếu* and to the legitimacy of dynastic succession.

Lê Hữu Mục, in his Introduction to LNCQ (Khai Trí, Saigon, 1960), classifies Chưng Bính with Trầu cau and Dưa hấu as a **"truyền thuyết phong tục"** (custom-aetiology legend) rather than a *cổ tích* (folktale): "Bánh dày hình tròn là hình trời; bánh chưng hình vuông là hình của đất, nhân là chất làm cho trời đất có liên hệ và do đấy có sinh sản … lời giải thích rất khách quan và có cứ điểm trong thực tại" ("the round cake is the form of heaven; the square cake is the form of earth; the filling is the substance through which heaven and earth communicate and thereby beget; the explanation is highly objective and grounded in reality").

### 5. Comparative Parallels

- **Chinese parallels.** The cosmological binary 天圓地方 ("round heaven, square earth") belongs to the *gaitian* (蓋天) tradition of Chinese cosmology, attested at least from the *Lüshi chunqiu* and *Zhoubi suanjing*. The competition-and-dream-by-divine-helper motif is a commonplace of Chinese *zhiguai* (志怪) and *chuanqi* (傳奇) literature; Vũ Quỳnh's preface explicitly compares LNCQ to genres such as *Nam truyện ký, Thế thuyết, Dã lục, Chí dị, Tạp biên*. The Chinese deity *Hậu Tắc* (后稷, Lord of Millet) — invoked in *Việt điện u linh* — is the canonical agriculture culture-hero whose worship the Lý-Trần-Lê *xã tắc* (社稷) altars and *lễ Tịch điền* (籍田) ceremony reproduce; some commentators (e.g. *Suckhoecong* citing *Việt điện u linh*) read the dream-deity in Chưng Bính truyện as an avatar of Hậu Tắc.
- **Mường parallel.** The Mường epic *Đẻ đất đẻ nước* ("Birth of Earth and Water"; collected versions e.g. Đặng Văn Lung, Vương Anh & Hoàng Anh Nhân, NXB Khoa học xã hội; Nguyễn Từ Chi & Nguyễn Trần Đản, NXB Kim Đồng 1974; Đinh Văn Ân, NXB Văn hoá dân tộc 2005) shares the rice-and-cosmogony complex but lacks the heaven-square / earth-round binary; Phan Đăng Nhật, "Chứng tích văn hóa dân gian về cội nguồn dân tộc: Những yếu tố trùng hợp giữa sử thi Mo và sách Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái," *Nghiên cứu Lịch sử* 3 (1981): 43, traces specific motif overlaps. The Mường are widely accepted as the closest surviving Vietic relative of the Kinh.
- **Other Southeast-Asian rice-origin myths** (e.g. Tai-Kadai *khwan khao* "rice-soul" cycles, Khmer *Preah Mae Kongkea / Preah Phuum* offerings) similarly anthropomorphise rice as gift / feminine principle / mother, but none parallel the Lang Liêu narrative closely; the cosmological square-round geometry is distinctively Sinic.

### 6. Mentions in Other Vietnamese Classical Texts

- **Đại Việt sử ký toàn thư** (Ngô Sĩ Liên 1479; printed Chính Hòa 18 = 1697; Nội các quan bản, Nôm Foundation digital edition; standard Hà Nội 1993 Phan Huy Lê translation, vol. I, NXB Khoa học xã hội): Ngô Sĩ Liên integrated the Hồng Bàng material into the *Ngoại kỷ*, listing eighteen Hùng generations beginning *Nhâm Tuất* 2879 BCE. The bánh-chưng story itself, however, is **not narrated in extenso** in *Toàn thư*; the *Toàn thư* gives only the basic Hùng-king framework, with the prince-and-cake story carried by the LNCQ tradition. Toàn thư's use of LNCQ-derived material is explicit in its commentary on, e.g., Phù Đổng Thiên Vương and Kim Quy. Trần Trọng Kim, *Việt Nam sử lược* (1920), reproduces the LNCQ outline.
- **Việt điện u linh tập** (Lý Tế Xuyên, c. 1329; standard ed. Lê Hữu Mục, Khai Trí, Saigon 1961). The work as Lý Tế Xuyên left it concerns *đế vương / phụ thần / hạo khí anh linh* — a cult-spirits compendium, **not a Hùng-king mythography**. It refers to Hùng kings only obliquely (e.g. in *Truyện Tản Viên Sơn thánh* and the *Hậu Tắc* cult passage). Lý Tế Xuyên thus is *not* a source for the bánh chưng story; LNCQ is the earliest narrativisation.
- **Việt sử lược** (anonymous, 14th c.; surviving Ming-period text) gives the famous note that under King Zhuang of Zhou (696–682 BCE) "in the Gia Ninh region [Phú Thọ] a remarkable man used magical arts to subdue the tribes, called himself Hùng Vương, established his capital at Văn Lang … 18 generations all called Hùng Vương" — but, again, no bánh-chưng episode.
- **Hùng Vương Ngọc phả** texts (the *Nam Việt Hùng Vương ngọc phả Vĩnh Truyền*, dated Thiên Phúc 980 [back-dated], and the *Hùng đồ thập bát diệp thánh vương ngọc phả cổ truyền*, Hồng Đức 3 = 1472): canonise Lang Liêu as the seventh Hùng king "Hùng Chiêu Vương Minh Tông hoàng đế" and link him to local cult-sites (e.g. miếu Lang Liêu, Hương Trầm village, Dữu Lâu, Việt Trì).
- **Khâm định Việt sử thông giám cương mục** (Quốc sử quán, 19th c.) reproduces the Toàn thư genealogy but rationalises some material.

### 7. French Colonial-Era Scholarship (BEFEO and others)

The decisive French interventions are:

- **Henri Maspero**, "Études d'histoire d'Annam. IV. Le Royaume de Văn-lang," *Bulletin de l'École française d'Extrême-Orient* 18, 3 (1918): 1–10 (with footnote 2 referencing the popular *History of the eighteen Hùng-king reigns* pamphlet). Maspero argued that Văn Lang was a misreading of Yelang (夜郎) and that 雄 (Hùng) was a Tang scribal corruption of 雒 (Lạc); his conclusion — that the Hùng-king tradition rests on Chinese textual error — became the cornerstone of all subsequent critical treatments and is sharply criticised by post-war Vietnamese scholarship. See also Maspero, "Légendes mythologiques dans le Chou King," *Journal Asiatique* 204 (1924): 1–2.
- **Paul Pelliot & Léopold Cadière**, "Première étude sur les sources annamites de l'histoire d'Annam," *BEFEO* IV (1904): 617 ff., catalogues LNCQ among the principal Vietnamese sources.
- **Émile Gaspardone**, "Bibliographie annamite," *BEFEO* XXXIV (1934): 1–173 (esp. 128–129), provides the foundational descriptive catalogue of LNCQ manuscripts (A-series shelfmarks at the EFEO/Viện Hán Nôm), distinguishing between the Vũ Quỳnh recension, the Đoàn Vĩnh Phúc-augmented recension, and the late "Tiếm đính" copies.
- **Maurice Durand**, "Recherches sur la littérature vietnamienne — Lý dynasty according to Việt điện u linh," *BEFEO* (1954) — context for Lý Tế Xuyên but not for Chưng Bính specifically; Durand also reviewed Nguyễn Đổng Chi's *Lược khảo về thần thoại Việt Nam* in *BEFEO*.
- **Lê Văn Hảo**, review of Nguyễn Đổng Chi, *Lược khảo về thần thoại Việt Nam* (Hà Nội: Văn Sử Địa, 1956, 185 pp.), in *BEFEO* LII, 1 (1964): 253–256: emphasises the importance of the *agricultural* mythological substratum that Nguyễn Đổng Chi did not fully exploit, and explicitly groups bánh-chưng among aetiologies of rice-civilisation.

### 8. Modern Vietnamese Scholarship

- **Nguyễn Đổng Chi**, *Lược khảo về thần thoại Việt Nam* (Hà Nội: Ban Văn Sử Địa, 1956; 2nd ed. same year, 185 pp.), and *Kho tàng truyện cổ tích Việt Nam* (5 vols., 1957–1982; reprinted Đông A Books). Vol. I includes "Gốc tích bánh chưng và bánh dày" (no. 1 in the section "Sự tích đất nước Việt"). Nguyễn Đổng Chi treats the Lang Liêu story as a paradigm of the "*sự tích nguồn gốc sự vật*" type, emphasising rice as the "ngọc thực" (jade-grain) of an *agriculture mère* — a society-wide rice cult the LNCQ tale dramatises but does not invent.
- **Đinh Gia Khánh** (with Nguyễn Ngọc San), *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* (Hà Nội: NXB Văn học, 1960; rev. 1990; reissued NXB Kim Đồng 2017 with Tạ Huy Long illustrations). The introduction by Đinh Gia Khánh is the standard modern Vietnamese scholarly treatment, fixing the textual genealogy and dating LNCQ's substratum to the Lý–Trần.
- **Lê Hữu Mục**, *Lĩnh Nam chích quái* (Saigon: Nhà sách Khai Trí, 1960; 1961 imprint by Quán Sách Mùa Thu). Lê Hữu Mục's introductory chapters on (i) the title, (ii) Trần Thế Pháp as author, (iii) date of composition, (iv) sources, (v) analytical schema, and (vi) value, are still cited; his rebuttal of attempts to attribute LNCQ to Vũ Quỳnh or Kiều Phú is forceful.
- **Cao Huy Đỉnh**, *Tìm hiểu tiến trình văn học dân gian Việt Nam* (1974), reads the bánh-chưng story as a marker of the transition from a matrilineal forest society (the orphaned mother) to a patrilineal Confucianised state (the filial offering to *tiên vương*).
- **Phan Huy Lê** et al., eds., *Hùng Vương dựng nước* (4 vols., NXB Khoa học xã hội, 1970s), the official Hà Nội defence of the Hùng-king historicity.
- **Nguyễn Thị Oanh**, *Lĩnh Nam chích quái — Khảo luận, Dịch chú, Nguyên bản chữ Hán* (NXB Khoa học xã hội; reissue Đinh Tị / Đức Bà Hòa Bình Books). The most thorough modern philological edition: she works principally from manuscript A.2914 of the Viện Hán Nôm and provides parallel Hán original, dịch nghĩa, khảo dị, and chú thích.
- **Trần Đình Hoành & Nguyễn Hữu Vinh**, *Lĩnh Nam Chích Quái Bình giải* (PDF, 2015), translation from the 1992 Học Sinh Thư Cục Hán original, with running commentary; this is the source most frequently quoted in current Vietnamese-language religious and cultural-studies discussions of the tale.
- **Liam C. Kelley**, "The Biography of the Hồng Bàng Clan as a Medieval Vietnamese Invented Tradition," *Journal of Vietnamese Studies* 7, 2 (2012): 87–130; "Inventing Traditions in Fifteenth-Century Vietnam," in V. Mair & L. C. Kelley, eds., *Imperial China and Its Southern Neighbours* (Singapore: ISEAS, 2015), 161–193. Kelley's argument bears on the Chưng Bính tale only indirectly (he focuses on the Hồng Bàng prologue), but his thesis — that the LNCQ matrix is a Sinicised Lý-Trần-Lê literary construction rather than an autochthonous oral memory — frames how the bánh-chưng story is read by post-2010 Anglophone and overseas Vietnamese historians.
- **Tạ Chí Đại Trường**, "Comments on Liam Kelley's article," *JVS* 7, 2 (2012): 139–162, and *Thần, người và đất Việt* (Westminster, CA: Văn nghệ, 1989; rev. Smashwords / Kệ Sách, 2011), defends the layered character of the tradition while accepting that LNCQ's textual authority cannot be projected back to actual pre-Sinitic times.
- **Keith W. Taylor**, *The Birth of Vietnam* (Berkeley: UC Press, 1983), Appendix A "The Legend of Lac Long Quan and Au Co" and Appendix B "Hung and Lac in the Sources," uses LNCQ critically; Taylor in later work (notably *A History of the Vietnamese*, Cambridge 2013) has stepped back from arguments grounded in the Hùng-king cycle, partly under Kelley's influence — see Le Minh Khai blog posts of 2010 and 2013.
- **Eric Henry**, "Chinese and Indigenous Influences in Vietnamese Verse Romances of the 19th Century," *Crossroads* 15, 2 (2001): 1–40, treats Hùng-king tales from a literary-and-gender angle.
- **Phạm Hoàn Mĩ**, "18 Vị vua dựng nước ta?", *Bách Khoa* 49 (1959): 78, is an earlier Saigon-era critical reprise of the Lạc-vs.-Hùng debate.
- **Nguyễn Thị Điểu** (T. Đ. Nguyễn), "A mythographical journey to modernity: The textual and symbolic transformations of the Hùng Kings founding myths," *Journal of Southeast Asian Studies* (2013/2014), is the most synthetic recent treatment of the textual transformations from LNCQ through the colonial pamphlet to the post-1945 state-cult, with full citation of Maspero, Gaspardone, Đinh Gia Khánh, Phan Đăng Nhật, Tạ Chí Đại Trường, and Kelley.

### 9. Summary of the Scholarly Stakes

The Chưng Bính / Lang Liêu legend stands at a triple intersection: (i) **textual** — it survives in a specific Hán recension whose stemma can be reconstructed from c. 1492 onward but whose oral antecedents cannot be securely dated; (ii) **cosmological** — its heaven-round / earth-square figure is a Sinic universal which the tale localises through the matter of *gạo nếp* and *lá dong*; (iii) **political-cultural** — from the Vũ Quỳnh redaction onwards it has served as a Vietnamese self-defining founding aetiology, particularly under the Lê Thánh Tông cult-promulgation of 1470–72, and continues to do so in the contemporary state's UNESCO-recognised "Tín ngưỡng thờ cúng Hùng Vương" complex (since 2012) and in the recognition of "Nghề làm bánh chưng, bánh giầy" as national intangible heritage (May 2023). Modern scholarship divides over how much of this can be projected back into pre-imperial Việt prehistory and how much is medieval Sinicised invention.

## Caveats

1. **No autograph exists.** Every line of Chưng Bính truyện cited above descends from copies made after 1492 (Vũ Quỳnh / Kiều Phú); the attribution to Trần Thế Pháp is a 17th–18th-c. tradition, not a contemporary witness.
2. **The 1960 Hà Nội edition (Đinh Gia Khánh) is variously cited as published by NXB Văn Học or NXB Văn Hóa**, depending on later imprints (the 1990 reprint is NXB Văn Học; the source landed in Saigoneer cites NXB Văn Hóa, 137 pp.); both refer to the same translation. Page numbers vary from edition to edition; the present report does not give page numbers because the modern Đinh Gia Khánh / NXB Kim Đồng 2017 reprint paginates Chưng Bính truyện in the high 50s–60s, while the Lê Hữu Mục 1960 (Khai Trí) places it differently. *Researchers needing precise pagination should consult the NXB Kim Đồng 2017 edition, the Nguyễn Thị Oanh critical edition (NXB KHXH), or the Việt Nam Hán văn tiểu thuyết tùng san vol. 1 (Học Sinh Thư Cục, Taipei, 1992, ed. Trần Khánh Hạo).*
3. **Maspero's 1918 thesis is contested but not refuted.** Vietnamese state historiography (Phan Huy Lê et al.) maintains the historicity of a Hùng-Vương-era polity correlated with Đông Sơn archaeology (c. 7th c. BCE onward); but the *literary form* of Chưng Bính truyện is undisputedly medieval.
4. **Liam Kelley's "invented tradition" thesis (JVS 2012)** has provoked vigorous push-back from Vietnamese scholars (Nguyễn Hòa, Lê Việt Anh, Tạ Chí Đại Trường's nuanced reply); the report presents both sides without endorsing either.
5. **Many Vietnamese-language web sources** (e.g. dkn.tv, suckhoecong.vn, lichsuvietnam.org) read the tale through neo-Confucian / Đạo / Falun-adjacent lenses ("Vương đạo," "Ngũ hành," "Tam tài") that are interpretive overlays not present in the LNCQ text itself. They are useful as evidence of contemporary reception, not of the medieval text.
6. **The popular folktale form** (anonymous Wikisource version; school textbook version) differs from LNCQ in numerous small ways (20 sons vs. 22; Tiết Liêu vs. Lang Liêu; *bánh giầy* vs. *bạc trì bính*); the present report distinguishes these variants but the public understanding in Vietnam today derives largely from the popular version.
7. **The full Hán text quoted above** comes from the digital edition at the *Chinese Text Project* and Chinese Wikisource (嶺南摭怪/蒸餅傳); these correspond to the standard "Vũ Quỳnh canon" recension and align in all substantive points with the texts edited by Trần Khánh Hạo (1992), Đinh Gia Khánh (1960/1990), and Nguyễn Thị Oanh (recent). Variants are noted in the comparative table above.
8. **No specifically Chưng-Bính-focused BEFEO article** appears to exist. Maspero (1918) and Gaspardone (1934) treat the larger LNCQ / Hùng-king complex within which Chưng Bính sits, but no French colonial scholar wrote a dedicated study of this tale.
